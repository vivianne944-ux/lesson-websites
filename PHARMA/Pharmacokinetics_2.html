Drug elimination

The lecture defines elimination as an irreversible shift of drugs from one part of the body to another.

Removal of the parent molecule does not always end the effect when active metabolites remain.

Elimination→Excretion of intact drug+Biotransformation / metabolism

Three central parameters

  • Bioavailability: fraction reaching circulation unchanged.
  • Volume of distribution: apparent space into which the drug distributes.
  • Clearance: the body’s ability to eliminate the drug.

Removal from the body

Drug Excretion

Excretion removes unchanged drug or drug metabolites. The kidney is the major route, and renal handling depends on filtration, active secretion, and passive reabsorption.

Pages 1–2, 9

Forms of excreted drug

  • Unchanged drug
  • Drug metabolites
✅ Polar / water-soluble: high excretion, low absorption.
⚠️ Nonpolar / lipid-soluble: high reabsorption back to plasma, low excretion.

Routes of excretion

  • Kidneys — major route.
  • GIT — biliary/enterohepatic and fecal routes.
  • Lungs — exhalation.
  • Skin, sweat, saliva, tears, breast milk.

Renal journey

Glomerulus→Passive filtration→ Proximal tubule→Active secretion→ Distal tubule→Passive reabsorption

Glomerular filtration

  • Passive process.
  • Removes small molecules below albumin size.
  • Highly protein-bound drugs are not filtered efficiently.
  • Small, unbound drugs are cleared rapidly.
  • The lecture states about 20% of unbound drug is filtered.
🔬 Lab cue: Inulin clearance is used to estimate GFR because it is neither reabsorbed nor secreted.

Proximal tubular secretion

  • Active process.
  • Uses transport carriers.
  • Saturable because transporter number is limited.
  • Transporters are relatively nonspecific, allowing competition.
  • Lecture associations: weak bases ↔ histamine substrate; weak acids ↔ uric acid substrate.
⭐ Must know: Active drug excretion occurs mainly in the proximal tubule.

Distal tubular reabsorption

  • Passive simple diffusion.
  • Depends on the concentration gradient.
  • Uncharged drug can diffuse out of the tubule and escape excretion.
  • Ionized drug does not diffuse passively and is more readily excreted.

Biotransformation

Drug Metabolism

Metabolism chemically alters a drug. It may produce active, inactive, or toxic metabolites, often increasing polarity and water solubility to support renal elimination.

Pages 2–5, 8–9

Metabolite types

  • Active metabolite: retains pharmacologic activity, remains reabsorbable, and is described as less polar and more lipid-soluble.
  • Inactive metabolite: no pharmacologic activity, prepared for excretion, more polar and water-soluble.

Where metabolism occurs

Liver is the main site.

Other tissue sites listed: lungs, intestinal mucosa, skin, placenta, and kidney.

Subcellular sites include ER, mitochondria, cytosol, lysosomes, nuclear envelope, and plasma membrane.

Phase I — nonsynthetic

Direct modification that inserts or unmasks a polar group.

  • Oxidation
  • Reduction
  • Hydrolysis
  • Deamination
✅ Typical result: a more polar, water-soluble form that is more readily excreted.

Phase II — synthetic

Conjugation with endogenous substances.

  • Acetate
  • Glucuronate
  • Sulfate
  • Glycine
✅ Typical result: a more polar, less lipid-soluble, usually inactive metabolite.
Phase I reactions in detail Page 3

Oxidation

  • Predominant reaction.
  • May be CYP450-dependent or independent.
  • CYP450 is concentrated in smooth ER of liver cells.
  • The lecture states CYP3A4 handles about 50% and CYP3A4 + CYP2D6 together account for about 75%.
  • Examples: paracetamol N-oxidation; ibuprofen hydroxylation.

Reduction

  • Substrate gains electrons.
  • Favored when oxygen content is low.
  • Example: dantrolene nitroreduction.

Hydrolysis

  • Nonmicrosomal.
  • Adds water and breaks a bond.
  • Uses esterases and amidases.
  • Procaine → pseudocholinesterase; lidocaine → liver microsomal CYP450.

Nonmicrosomal pathways

  • Hydrolysis
  • Monoamine oxidase for 5-HT, dopamine, and tyramine
  • Alcohol → aldehyde → acid via dehydrogenases
Phase II reactions in detail Page 3

Glucuronidation

Most common conjugation reaction, inducible, and may undergo enterohepatic cycling. Activity is low in neonates.

Acetylation

Shows genotypic variation between fast and slow metabolizers. The lecture repeatedly connects this with isoniazid.

Glutathione conjugation

Protects hepatocytes and other cells. Depleted hepatic GSH is linked to acetaminophen toxicity and NAPQI formation.

↑ metabolism → ↓ plasma level and ↓ effect of the affected drug.

Elimination patterns

Clinical Pharmacokinetics

The key distinction is whether the body removes a constant fraction or a constant amount per unit time.

Pages 5–6, 10

First-order kinetics

  • Most common.
  • Linear, nonsaturable.
  • Constant fraction or percentage removed per unit time.
  • Rate is proportional to concentration.
  • Double dose → double concentration.
  • Concentration decreases by 50% each half-life.
  • Elimination rate variable; half-life constant.

Zero-order kinetics

  • Also called Michaelis–Menten / zero-order elimination.
  • Nonlinear, saturable.
  • Constant amount removed per unit time.
  • Rate becomes independent of concentration.
  • Small dose increases may produce toxic levels after saturation.
  • Elimination rate constant; half-life variable.

Dosing & Drug Levels

Half-life, clearance, peak, trough, steady state, loading dose, and maintenance dose guide how quickly a target level is reached and how it is maintained.

Pages 6–7

Half-life

Time required for plasma concentration to fall by 50%.

t½ = 0.693 × (Vd / Cl)
  • Applies to first-order kinetics in the lecture.
  • Higher Vd at constant clearance → longer half-life.
  • Higher clearance at constant Vd → shorter half-life.

Clearance

Volume of blood cleared of drug per unit time.

Cl = Rate of elimination / Plasma concentration
Total systemic Cl = Clrenal + Clhepatic + Clothers

At steady state, the lecture emphasizes drug input = drug output.

Rate of administration

Rate of administration = Clearance × Plasma concentration

This relationship defines the administration rate required to maintain a plateau concentration.

⭐ Steady state: The trans repeatedly states that steady state is reached in about 4–5 half-lives; page 7 also uses 4 half-lives as the practical waiting point before drawing blood.

Peak level

Highest plasma concentration after each dose.

  • IV: within minutes.
  • Oral: 1–2 hours.

Trough level

Lowest plasma concentration between doses.

✅ Key point: Best time to obtain blood samples.

Steady state

Equilibrium between the administered dose and the amount eliminated.

Loading dose

Single dose or series of doses used to reach target concentration rapidly without waiting several half-lives.

LD = Vd × Target concentration

Usually followed by a maintenance dose.

Maintenance dose

Repeated dose or continuous infusion used to maintain steady state in the therapeutic range.

Maintenance dose = Dosing rate × Dosing interval
Dosing rate = Clearance × Target concentration

The lecture notes that it is usually given after the loading dose or after one half-life and is approximately half the loading dose.

Safety and monitoring

Therapeutic Window

The therapeutic window separates useful exposure from ineffective or toxic exposure and determines when serum drug levels need close monitoring.

Pages 7–8

Therapeutic range / window

Range between the minimum effective therapeutic concentration and the minimum toxic concentration.

Below threshold→No clinical effect|Therapeutic range|Above threshold→Toxicity
⭐ Must know: The larger the therapeutic range, the safer the drug. The lecture gives ibuprofen and paracetamol as examples with larger windows.

Therapeutic index

A measure of drug safety defined as the ratio of TD50 or LD50 to ED50.

Determined from a quantal dose–response curve.

Repeated doses

With first-order kinetics, repeated dosing produces an exponential rise in concentration until a plateau is reached.

At steady state, peaks and troughs stop progressively rising when dosing remains regular and no doses are missed.

Cram Sheet

Ultra-condensed rules, formulas, mnemonics, and clinical cues from the lecture.

Pages 1–11

Core elimination rules

  • Kidney = major excretion route.
  • Liver = main metabolic site.
  • More polar / ionized = easier excretion.
  • More lipid-soluble = more reabsorption and longer persistence.
  • High protein binding = less filtration and slower elimination.
  • Renal impairment = lower clearance and longer half-life.

Renal process map

  • Glomerulus: passive filtration.
  • Proximal tubule: active secretion.
  • Distal tubule: passive reabsorption.
  • Aspirin overdose: sodium bicarbonate → alkalinize urine.

Metabolism map

  • Phase I: oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis, deamination.
  • Phase II: conjugation.
  • CYP3A4: largest share in lecture.
  • Inducer: faster metabolism, lower level.
  • Inhibitor: slower metabolism, higher level.

High-yield toxicology

  • Paracetamol excess → NAPQI.
  • Antidote: N-acetylcysteine.
  • Isoniazid toxicity → pyridoxine.
  • Chloramphenicol in neonates → gray baby syndrome.

Kinetics

  • First order: constant fraction, linear, nonsaturable, t½ constant.
  • Zero order: constant amount, nonlinear, saturable, t½ variable.
  • Drug A: 100→75→50→25→0 = zero order.
  • Drug B: 100→50→25→12.5 = first order.

Common traps

  • Do not confuse constant fraction with constant amount.
  • Do not place active secretion in the glomerulus; it occurs mainly in the proximal tubule.
  • Do not assume lipid-soluble drug is easily excreted; lipid solubility favors reabsorption.
  • Do not confuse loading dose with maintenance dose: loading dose reaches the target; maintenance dose keeps it there.
  • Do not forget that enzyme inhibition raises plasma drug levels, while enzyme induction lowers them.
