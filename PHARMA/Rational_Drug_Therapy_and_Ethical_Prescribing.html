Rational Drug Therapy & Ethical Prescribing

Big picture

Overview

Rational prescribing combines diagnostic accuracy, pharmacologic knowledge, communication, individualized dosing, cost awareness, and follow-up.

Pages 1–2

Prescribing is an art and a clinical skill

  • Diagnostic skill
  • Knowledge of medicines
  • Understanding of clinical pharmacology
  • Communication skills
  • Appreciation of each drug’s risks and benefits

Why rational therapy matters

  • More than half of medicines are prescribed, dispensed, or sold inappropriately.
  • Half of patients fail to take medicines correctly.
  • About one-third of the world’s population lacks access to essential medicines.
⭐ Must know: Appropriate drug use is essential to good-quality medical care.

WHO concept of rational drug use

Patients should receive medication that is appropriate to their clinical needs, in a dose suited to the individual, for an adequate period, and at the lowest cost to the patient and community.

Right drug+Adequate dose+Sufficient duration+Clinical appropriateness+Lowest cost

Preventing avoidable harm

Ethical Prescribing

Ethical prescribing requires a clear diagnosis, defined objectives, a suitable medication, accurate communication, cost awareness, patient education, and regular monitoring.

⭐ Every order should be: Legible, unambiguous, dated, timed when it is a chart order, and clearly signed.

Prescribing errors

  • Omitted information: “Continue present IVF” without naming the exact fluid.
  • Assumed records: “Resume preoperative meds” without a verified list.
  • Poor writing: illegible handwriting or ambiguous decimals.
  • Missed contraindications: failure to account for comorbid disease.
  • Incomplete medication history: failure to identify other drugs.
  • Physicochemical incompatibility: incompatible preparations mixed or coadministered.

Decimal-point danger

A misplaced or ambiguous decimal may turn:

  • 0.1 into 1
  • 1.0 into 10
⚠️ Exam trap: “Readable” is not enough. The prescription must also be unambiguous.
Four major forms of nonadherence Page 2
  1. Failure to obtain the medicine.
  2. Failure to take as prescribed: wrong dose, frequency, timing, sequence, route, or purpose.
  3. Premature discontinuation: stopping when symptoms improve or when the container becomes empty.
  4. Inappropriate use: including sharing medicines.
✅ If you see: “Patient improved and stopped early” → think premature discontinuation, not failure to obtain the drug.
Legal and socioeconomic considerations Page 2

Drug categories and regulation

  • OTC drugs: intended for lay use in self-limiting conditions.
  • Prescription drugs: require a licensed prescriber.
  • Controlled drugs listed include opioids, hallucinogens, stimulants, depressants, and anabolic steroids.
  • The lecture notes an S-2 license for regulated or controlled drugs.
  • The FDA monitors drug safety through mandatory industry reports and voluntary practitioner reports.

Generic prescribing

  • Physicians must prescribe the generic name.
  • Generic prescribing allows dispensing of a generically equivalent, lower-cost product.
  • Cost analysis should cover the whole treatment duration.
  • The lecture cites Republic Act No. 6675 and emphasizes generic terminology in DOH transactions.
Preferred drug selection

Choosing the P-drug

A P-drug is selected through a repeatable, patient-centered process rather than habit, brand loyalty, or cost alone.

Pages 2–4

Expanded P-drug sequence

  1. Diagnosis
  2. Therapeutic objectives
  3. Non-drug measures
  4. Inventory of effective drug groups
  5. Choose the best P-drug group with ESSC.
  6. Compare individual drugs within that group using ESSC again.
  7. Write the prescription.
  8. Counsel and monitor.
⭐ Must know: A well-chosen P-drug depends on proper problem assessment plus knowledge of the drugs’ pharmacologic properties.

Diagnosis

Base prescribing on the primary diagnosis and relevant secondary diagnoses.

🔬 Lecture example: Fever, cough, and dyspnea in a patient with diabetes → primary diagnosis: pneumonia; secondary diagnosis: type 2 diabetes mellitus.

Treatment goals

Goals may be cure, relief, or prevention.

✅ Pneumonia example: Cure infection, relieve cough, and prevent complications or recurrence.

Treatment strategy is broader than medication

  • Research evidence may be included under Efficacy.

  • When affordable, an available innovator product may be prescribed; otherwise consider the cheapest appropriate option.
Structured comparison

ESSC Guide

ESSC ranks candidate groups and specific drugs according to Efficacy, Safety, Suitability, and Cost.

Pages 3, 5, 7–10

Efficacy

Mechanism, PK, PD, spectrum when relevant, and supporting reports.

Safety

Frequency, seriousness, predictability of adverse effects, and toxicity.

Suitability

Patient-specific contraindications, comorbidities, interactions, adherence, dosage form, and availability.

Cost

Total cost for the entire treatment, not simply the price per unit.

Suitability: patient fit Page 3
  • Previous adverse drug reactions.
  • Vulnerability due to very young age, older age, pregnancy, or comorbidities.
  • Current medications and potential interactions.
  • Likely adherence; once-daily dosing may improve compliance.
  • Taste, dosage form, and age-appropriate preparation.
  • Local availability and practicality.
✅ If you see current medications or drug interactions: Score them under Suitability.

How the scoring works

  1. List candidate drug groups.
  2. Give each ESSC category a score from + to ++++.
  3. Add the scores vertically.
  4. Select the highest-scoring group.
  5. List specific drugs within the chosen group and repeat the comparison.

Minimum counseling information Page 4

Effects

  • Why is the drug needed?
  • Which symptoms should improve?
  • When should the effect begin?
  • What happens if it is taken incorrectly or not taken?

Side effects

  • What may occur?
  • How is it recognized?
  • How long may it last?
  • How serious is it?
  • What action should be taken?

Instructions

  • How and when should it be taken?
  • How long should treatment continue?
  • How should it be stored?
  • What should be done with leftovers?

Warnings and follow-up

  • When should it not be taken?
  • What is the maximum dose?
  • Why should the course be completed?
  • When should the patient return or come earlier?

“Everything clear?”

  • Ask whether the patient understood.
  • Ask the patient to repeat the most important instructions.
  • Invite questions.
⭐ Counseling test: Do not assume understanding. Use the patient’s own repetition to confirm it.

Common traps

  • Do not choose a P-drug before defining the diagnosis and objective.
  • Do not treat cost as the only selection criterion.
  • Do not classify drug interactions under Safety when the question asks whether the drug fits the patient; the lecture places them under Suitability.
  • Do not assume every disease requires medication.
  • Do not finish at the prescription; counseling and monitoring are required.
  • Do not use incomplete orders such as “continue present IVF.”
