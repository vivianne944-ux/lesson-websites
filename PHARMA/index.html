Prescription Writing: Liquids
Lecture map

Build every liquid prescription from dose to delivery

The lecture moves from household-measure oral liquids to compounded mixtures, injectable solutions, dry-solid reconstitution, aerosols, and ophthalmic or otic drops. Across all six, the core task is to translate an ordered dose into a measurable volume and a complete written instruction.

The universal calculation path

pp. 1-3
Find daily dosePatient weight x ordered mg/kg/day, when a weight-based order is given.
Find dose per administrationDaily dose divided by the number of doses per day.
Convert to volumeRelate the ordered amount to the stock concentration.
Find total volumeVolume per dose x doses per day x treatment days.
Choose containersTotal volume divided by bottle, ampule, vial, or respule size; dispense enough whole units.
Total amount needed = dose per intake x total number of doses
Total volume required = volume per dose x doses per day x number of days

Measure, do not guess

For oral liquids, base the computation on the milliliter equivalent of the household measure. The lecture specifically says to instruct the patient to take a teaspoonful, not a teaspoon.

p. 1

Write the usable dosage form

The inscription should identify the preparation and concentration. For ready-made products, the subscription states the number and size of containers to dispense.

pp. 2-3

Complete the label

Frequency, duration, route, timing, and special handling belong in the directions when relevant: round the clock, after meals, shake before use, use a nebulizer, or instill in a named eye or ear.

Available measures in the lecture

  • Small plastic cups may measure 2.5, 5, 7.5, and 10 mL.
  • Syrups and suspensions are commonly available in bottles of 15, 30, up to 250 mL.
  • Infant drops are sold with calibrated droppers.
  • If only one bottle size is available, prescribe that bottle even when the calculated need is smaller.
Lecture discrepancy check

Do not copy figures mechanically. Compare the written order, computation, and sample label: the epinephrine paragraph on page 5 states every 15-30 minutes, while the sample image states every 10 minutes. The practical habit is to reconcile all parts before signing.

Type A

Single-ingredient oral liquids

Two delivery approaches appear first: a measured teaspoonful and drops. Stock solutions use the amount to dispense in the inscription; ready-made preparations require the correct number of bottles.

pp. 1-3

Teaspoonful method

The Paregoric example orders 5 mL every four hours, as needed, for three days. The total amount is:

Total amount = 5 mL per intake x total number of doses

The prescription uses the official name or USP form and includes the quantity in milliliters to be obtained from stock.

Exam cue

Write one teaspoonful in the label. The lecture explicitly rejects “one teaspoon.”

Drop method

The Belladonna Tincture example converts a usual dose of 0.6 mL using 45 drops/mL:

0.6 mL x 45 drops/mL = 27 drops per dose

The amount prescribed may follow convention: 30 mL or 15 mL. The label must instruct the patient to procure the Official Medicine Dropper.

Type B

Multiple-ingredient oral liquids

This section turns a dose formula into a compounded bulk preparation. The inscription follows the lecture’s basis-adjuvant-vehicle order, and each ingredient is scaled to the total number of doses.

pp. 3-5

Compounding sequence

  1. Determine total number of doses.
  2. Compute total bulk: amount of one dose x total doses.
  3. Compute each drug’s total quantity.
  4. Compute any percentage-based syrup volume from total bulk.
  5. Write ingredients in the required order and add vehicle to make the final volume.

Role of the vehicle

The vehicle adds bulk and may act as solvent, flavoring, or coloring agent. The lecture lists:

  • Aqueous: aromatic water, syrup, mucilage; used for water-soluble salts.
  • Hydroalcoholic: elixir; for substances relatively soluble in water and alcohol.
  • Alcoholic: tincture or spirit; for substances soluble in stronger alcohol or as flavor.

The written direction says every four hours, while the displayed calculation uses four doses per day. Preserve the lecture’s shown arithmetic for recall, but verify the intended frequency whenever writing the final order.

Type C

Liquid solutions for parenteral use

Parenteral solution orders identify the official preparation, concentration or amount per container, number of ampules, syringes, or vials, route, timing, and dose volume.

pp. 5-6

What the prescription must show

  • Official name of the preparation.
  • Concentration and/or amount in one ampule or syringe.
  • Number of units to dispense.
  • Dose volume, route, frequency, and special timing such as STAT.

Dose-volume conversion

Desired dose volume = ordered dose / amount on hand x quantity on hand

Midazolam example: (1.5 mg / 5 mg) x 1 mL = 0.3 mL, given by slow intravenous push on arrival at the operating room.

Worked order cues

pp. 5-6

Diphenhydramine

Single STAT dose of 50 mg IM; available as 50 mg/mL ampule. The order therefore uses one mL from one ampule.

Filgrastim

One subcutaneous dose using a 300 mcg in 0.5 mL pre-filled syringe, administered at the arm or abdomen.

Dexamethasone

6 mg IM every 12 hours for four doses. With 5 mg/mL in 1 mL ampules, the lecture computes 1.2 ampules and rounds to 2 ampules per dose.

Lab practical cue

Do not stop at the milligram answer. A parenteral order is operational only after the dose has been converted to the available concentration and the required number of whole containers has been stated.

Type D

Parenteral dry solids: reconstitute, then administer

These orders add a preparation step before dosing. The prescription must identify the dry sterile product, diluent, reconstituted amount, further dilution or infusion when shown, and the administration instruction.

pp. 7-8

Lecture rules

  • Reconstitution means adding a diluent to a dry solid to make it liquid.
  • The lecture’s penicillin example uses an ideal diluent amount of 1.5 mL, with a range of 1-2 mL.
  • If the dry solid forms a solution, start the label with “Dissolve”.
  • “Inject only after a negative skin test” is generally placed for parenteral drugs, especially those likely to cause hypersensitivity, with an emergency exception stated by the lecture.

Penicillin G example

Crystalline Penicillin G Sodium is ordered at 1,000,000 units IV every six hours round the clock for four days.

4 doses/day x 4 days = 16 doses

The sample prescription dispenses 16 vials of 1,000,000 units/vial and directs dissolution in 1.5 mL distilled water before slow IV injection.

Reconstitution patterns in the lecture

pp. 7-8
Two-vial RSV vaccine system
  1. Withdraw the entire adjuvant suspension using a sterile needle and syringe.
  2. Slowly transfer it into the lyophilized antigen vial.
  3. Gently swirl until completely dissolved; do not shake vigorously.
  4. Withdraw 0.5 mL after reconstitution and administer intramuscularly.
Ceftazidime: reconstitute, further dilute, infuse

1 g every 8 hours for three days gives 9 doses and 9 g total. Each 1 g vial is reconstituted with 3 mL sterile water, then further diluted with 50 mL D5W or NSS and infused for 30 minutes.

Ceftriaxone: select the matching vial
20 kg x 50 mg/kg/day = 1000 mg/day; divided into 2 doses = 500 mg/dose

The lecture’s prescription uses two 500 mg vials for 24 hours, with the vial dissolved in 5 mL diluent and injected slowly IV over five minutes.

Pantoprazole: single reconstituted dose

Dispense one 40 mg vial. Dissolve the 40 mg powder in 2 mL sterile NSS and inject intravenously as a single dose.

Common confusion

Reconstituting the vial and further diluting the reconstituted drug are separate steps in the ceftazidime example. Both volumes and the 30-minute infusion belong in the final instruction.

Types E-F

Aerosols and ophthalmic or otic preparations

These small-volume preparations are distinguished by delivery device and anatomical site. The label must make the route unmistakable: nebulize and inhale, instill in the conjunctival sac, or instill in the named ear.

pp. 8-9

Aerosol via nebulizer

The salbutamol sample uses nebules: plastic containers holding drug solution. The contents are placed in a nebulizer; the patient breathes the mist through a tube or mask.

Instructions should state frequency, duration, and indication for use when needed, such as attack, maintenance, or round-the-clock use.

Eye and ear drops

Ophthalmic drops are instilled in the conjunctival sac. If another eye drug is also prescribed, the lecture specifies a 5-minute interval to avoid washout.

State the exact site: right eye, both eyes, or left ear.

Container calculations

pp. 8-9

Budesonide respules

2 administrations/day x 2 days = 4 respules

Select 250 mcg/2 mL because it matches the ordered 250 mcg dose.

Ofloxacin otic drops

3 drops x 4/day = 12 drops/day
12 x 1/20 mL x 7 days = 4.2 mL

A 5 mL bottle covers the course: dispense one bottle.

Prednisolone eye drops

1 drop x 2 eyes x 3/day = 6 drops/day
6 x 1/20 mL x 5 days = 1.5 mL

Select the ophthalmic, not otic, 0.5% solution.

Exam trap

When both ophthalmic and otic preparations exist at the same concentration, concentration alone does not identify the correct product. Match the dosage form to the intended site.

Lab practical guide

Write from the case in a fixed sequence

Use the same checkpoint order every time. It keeps weight-based arithmetic, stock selection, subscription, and label directions aligned.

pp. 1-9

Prescription-writing checklist

lecture-wide
1. Parse the order before calculating
  • Patient weight, if mg/kg/day is used.
  • Total daily dose or dose per administration.
  • Number of divided doses or stated interval.
  • Duration, route, dosage form, and available concentration.
  • Available bottle, vial, ampule, syringe, or respule size.
2. Calculate dose and volume with units
  • Daily dose first, then dose per administration.
  • Convert the dose to mL using the available concentration.
  • Carry mL/dose into total-volume computation.
  • For drops, include the number of sites, drops per site, frequency, and drop factor.
3. Determine whole containers
  • Divide total required volume by container volume.
  • Round up when a partial bottle or vial count results.
  • When only one size is available, dispense that size even if some volume remains.
4. Build the written prescription
  • Patient identifiers and date.
  • Rx symbol followed by drug, dosage form, and concentration.
  • Subscription with number and size of containers.
  • Label with dose, route, frequency, duration, timing, and handling.
  • Prescriber identity, license, and PTR details shown in the samples.
5. Add preparation-specific directions
  • Suspension: shake before use.
  • Dry solid: dissolve in the stated diluent; include further dilution or infusion.
  • Nebule: place contents in nebulizer and inhale mist.
  • Eye/ear: instill the stated number of drops into the named site.
  • Controlled drug example: the lecture notes an Opium License Number for opium.
6. Reconcile all three layers

Final audit: the written case, your arithmetic, and the label must describe the same dose, concentration, schedule, route, and duration.

Stock solution vs ready-made bottle

Stock solution: the quantity in mL is incorporated in the inscription; there may be no separately defined subscription.

Ready-made bottle: know the available concentration and container size, compute total need, then state the number and size of bottles.

p. 2

Solution vs suspension direction

The oral suspension examples include shake bottle well before use. Dry solids that become a solution use dissolve in the reconstitution label.
