  • Hydrophilic ligands act at cell-surface receptors; hydrophobic ligands cross the lipid bilayer to intracellular receptors.
  • Ligand-gated ion channels respond in milliseconds; intracellular receptor responses appear after about 30 minutes or more and last hours to days.

  • A higher therapeutic index and a bigger therapeutic window indicate a safer drug in the lecture framework.
  • Tachyphylaxis is rapid; tolerance develops gradually and requires higher doses for the same response.

Pharmacodynamics & Mechanisms of Drug Action

The lesson proper begins here: definition, receptor-mediated action, coupling, receptor classes, spare receptors, and non-receptor mechanisms.

pp. 1–2

I. Pharmacodynamics

Pharmacodynamics is the study of the biochemical, biologic, and physiological effects of drugs.

  • Biochemical effects: the mechanism of action at the molecular and enzyme level.
  • Physiological effects: observable changes—how the drug affects the body.

Why it matters

  • Provides the basis for the rational therapeutic use of drugs.
  • Supports the design of new and superior chemical agents.
  • Includes Structure–Activity Relationship (SAR) / Biochemorphology: relating chemical structure to biochemical and physiological effects.
  • Aims for greater selectivity and fewer toxic effects.
  • Lecture examples: norepinephrine and epinephrine.

II. Receptor-Mediated Action

A receptor is a locus in a macromolecule that can initiate biological effects and biochemical reactions, or a specific cell molecule with which most drugs interact to produce an effect.

A drug ligand may bind reversibly or irreversibly, depending on the strength and type of binding:

Bond / interactionLecture listing
CovalentStrong interaction associated with irreversible binding in later antagonist discussion.
ElectrostaticListed among possible ligand–receptor interactions.
Hydrogen bondingListed among possible ligand–receptor interactions.
Van der WaalsListed among possible ligand–receptor interactions.
Significance of ReceptorsReceptors largely determine the quantitative relation between dose or concentration and pharmacologic effect, provide selectivity, and mediate actions of agonists and antagonists.

Main Classes of Receptors / Drug Targets

Regulatory proteins

Mediate endogenous chemical signals such as neurotransmitters, autacoids, and hormones.

Enzymes

May be inhibited or activated by drug binding.

  • Dihydrofolate reductase for methotrexate
  • Acetylcholinesterase
  • ACE
  • COX
  • Carbonic anhydrase
  • DNA and RNA polymerases
  • Monoamine oxidase
  • Neuraminidase
  • Na+–K+ ATPase
  • Reverse transcriptase

Transport proteins

  • Na+–K+ ATPase for digitalis
  • Norepinephrine and serotonin transporters for antidepressants
  • Inhibitors of dopamine and GABA reuptake

Structural proteins

Tubulin is the lecture example target for colchicine.

Drug–Receptor Coupling

Coupling is the overall transduction process linking drug occupancy of receptors to the pharmacologic response.

Agonist bindsThe drug combines with the receptor.
Receptor changes shapeOccupancy produces a conformational change.
Signal transductionA cellular response is initiated.
Drug actionThe pharmacologic effect becomes observable.

Possible coupling effects

  • Opening or closing an ion channel
  • Activating second messengers
  • Physically inhibiting a normal cellular function
  • “Turning on” a cellular function

Spare Receptors & Signal Amplification

Some tissues possess more receptors than are necessary to produce a maximal response. The amount of reserve depends on the tissue, the response measured, and the drug’s intrinsic efficacy.

  • The lecture states that occupancy of about 1% of receptors may still produce a maximal response when receptor reserve is vast.
  • Spare receptors do not bind drug once the concentration is already sufficient for maximal effect.
  • They are present when Kd > EC50.
  • Kd is the concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied.
Do Not ConfuseReceptor occupancy and maximal response are not always identical because post-receptor signaling can amplify the response.

Non-Receptor-Mediated Drug Action

1. Physical activity

Adsorption
Adhesion of gas or liquid molecules to a surface. Examples: activated charcoal in poisoning and simethicone as an antiflatulent.
Mass / bulk
Psyllium, bran, and fibrosine use bulk as a stimulus for defecation in constipation.
Osmotic property
Mannitol, an osmotic diuretic, is used in the lecture example for cerebral edema.
Colligative effect
Action depends on the number of molecules and ions in the system. Examples: magnesium sulfate as a purgative, menthol as a demulcent, and tannic acid as an astringent.

2. Chemical activity

Neutralization
Antacids neutralize HCl and buffer gastric acid; they may also bind other drugs and decrease their absorption.
Chelation
Chelating agents bind divalent and heavy metal ions into complexes for excretion. Examples: penicillamine in Wilson’s disease/copper poisoning and dimercaprol (BAL) in heavy metal poisoning.
Radioactivity & radio-opacity
Iodine-131 is used for thyrotoxicosis. Barium sulfate is a contrast medium; radiopaque agents containing iodine absorb x-rays to help image a specific body area.
Altering metabolic processes
A structural analog of a normal body constituent is incorporated into a cellular system and alters its action. The lecture example is sulfonamide acting as a non-functional analog of a naturally occurring metabolite.

Properties of Drugs

The four core terms used to interpret drug binding and dose–response curves: affinity, efficacy, intrinsic activity, and potency.

p. 2

Affinity

The ability or tenacity of a drug to bind to a receptor.

  • Varies in degree.
  • A drug with stronger affinity may displace another drug.
  • May be competitive or non-competitive.

Efficacy

The relationship between receptor occupancy and the ability to initiate a response at the molecular, cellular, tissue, and system levels.

  • The maximal response produced by the drug.
  • The ability of the drug to elicit a pharmacologic response.

Intrinsic Activity

The capacity of a single drug–receptor complex to evoke an effect.

  • The ability to activate the receptor once bound.
  • Represents agonist–receptor coupling that brings about a response.

Potency

The range of doses over which a drug produces increasing responses.

  • EC50 is the concentration or dose required to produce 50% of the drug’s maximal response.
  • Also called the median effective concentration in the lecture.
Exam TrapEfficacy asks, “What is the highest response the drug can produce?” Potency asks, “How much drug is required to produce a specified response?” Intrinsic activity is the activating capacity of one drug–receptor complex.
Lecture Terminology AlertThe lecture uses EC50 in two contexts: the concentration producing 50% of a drug’s maximal response, and the concentration inducing a specified effect in 50% of subjects. Keep the surrounding curve context in mind.

A. Intracellular Receptors

  • The ligand must diffuse into the cell, so it must be very lipid soluble.
  • The lecture notes attachment to a plasma protein such as albumin.
  • Best examples: steroid and thyroid hormones.
  • The activated ligand–receptor complex migrates to the nucleus and regulates gene expression.
  • Responses are delayed but prolonged: about 30 minutes or more, lasting hours to days.

B. Enzyme-Linked Receptors

  • Best exemplified by insulin and epidermal growth factor.
  • Usually have tyrosine kinase activity as part of the receptor structure.
  • Activation causes autophosphorylation of the receptor.
Lecture-Specific PathwayCytokine receptors for erythropoietin, somatotropin, and interferons activate JAKs, which phosphorylate STATs. STATs dimerize, dissociate, cross the nuclear membrane, and modulate gene transcription.

C. Ligand-Gated Ion Channels

The response is very fast; binding and response can be measured in milliseconds.

  • Nicotinic receptor + acetylcholine → influx of sodium → action potential → skeletal muscle contraction.
  • Benzodiazepines enhance GABA receptor stimulation → influx of chloride → cell hyperpolarization.

D. G Protein-Coupled Receptors

  • Single peptide with seven membrane-spanning regions.
  • Also called seven-transmembrane or serpentine receptors.
  • Linked to a G protein and interacts with cellular effectors.
  • Changes concentrations of second messengers, which conduct and amplify signals.
G proteinReceptorsAction

GiM2, α2, D2Inhibition of adenylyl cyclase

Dose–Response Curves

How administered dose relates to receptor exposure, magnitude of response, population response, potency, efficacy, slope, and safety.

p. 3

General Dose–Response Relationship

The effect of a drug depends on the dose administered and how many drug molecules reach the receptors.

What the curve tells us

  • Type of drug
  • Mode of action
  • Potency
  • Therapeutic dose
  • Efficacy and relative drug safety

Main variables

  • Potency: how tightly the drug binds to the receptor in the lecture description.
  • Slope: the linear central part; steeper means a narrower safety margin.
  • Efficacy: the peak response produced.

A. Graded / Quantitative Dose–Response Curve

  • Relates a dose to a quantitatively gradable effect in an individual or given population.
  • Indicates efficacy and potency.
  • Is response-related and gives the maximal response.
  • Expresses an individual’s response to increasing doses.
  • The magnitude of response is proportional to the number of receptors with which the drug effectively interacts.

B. Quantal Dose–Response Curve

  • Shows the proportion of animals or individuals in a population that manifest a stated pharmacologic response at a given dose.
  • Plots percentage of the population responding against dose or log dose.
  • ED50 is the dose producing the response in half the population.
  • Tracks frequency of occurrence—either inhibition or stimulation.
  • The defined effect may be clinically relevant or chosen to preserve experimental-subject safety.
  • May be an all-or-none phenomenon.

Four Parameters of the Graded Curve

Other Uses of the Graded Curve

  • Classify drugs according to mechanism of action.
  • Lecture example: acetylcholine as a cholinergic neurotransmitter acting at cholinergic nerve endings.
  • Determine relative potency of drug analogs using ED50.

Uses of the Quantal Curve

  • Determine median therapeutic dose and toxic dose in a patient population.
  • Identify the margin of safety and therapeutic index.
  • Determine drug selectivity.
  • Indicate potential variability of responsiveness among people.

Drug Safety Determination

Four lecture measures connect therapeutic effect to toxicity: therapeutic index, certain safety factor, standard safety margin, and therapeutic range/window.

pp. 3–4

A. Therapeutic Index (TI)

A relative measure of a drug’s toxicity or safety.

Humans: TI = TD50 / ED50
Animals: TI = LD50 / ED50
  • Animals use lethal dose.
  • TI provides relative median values and does not describe extreme values.
  • A higher TI means the drug is safer to give in the lecture framework.

B. Certain Safety Factor (CSF)

Developed because potentially disastrous overlap can occur at the extremes of dose distributions.

CSF = LD1 / ED99

LD1: lethal dose in 1% of the population.
ED99: effective dose in 99% of the population.

C. Standard Safety Margin (SSM)

Shows the percentage by which the dose can be increased before 1% will die of overdose.

SSM% = [(LD1 − ED99) / ED99] × 100

D. Therapeutic Range / Window

The serum concentration required to achieve therapeutic effects without toxicity.

  • Bigger window → safer drug.
  • Narrow window → less safe drug.

Safety Measures at a Glance

MeasureUses which points?What it communicatesKey limitation / cue
TITD50 or LD50 relative to ED50Relative median safetyDoes not describe extreme values.
CSFLD1 and ED99Safety at the tails of the populationFocuses on 1% lethal vs 99% effective.
SSMLD1 and ED99Percent dose increase before 1% lethalityExpressed as a percentage.

VII. Pharmacogenetics

Pharmacogenetics is the study of the influence of heredity on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic responses to a drug. It includes qualitative and quantitative variations in drug response due to genetics.

Lecture ExampleIndividuals with alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency are discussed as an example of genetic traits affecting responses to environmental and industrial chemicals. The lecture states that alpha-1-antitrypsin protects against neutrophil elastase, involves mutations in SERPINA1, and deficiency may be associated with lung or liver disease.

Individual Variations in Pharmacologic Response

  • Pharmacokinetic variation
  • Pharmacodynamic variation
  • Idiosyncratic reactions

Main causes of variability

  • Age
  • Genetic factors
  • Immunologic factors
  • Pathologic states
  • Drug interactions

Consequences of Pharmacogenetic Variation

  • Altered drug clearance—renal or metabolic
  • Functional overdose
  • Failure to convert a prodrug to its active drug/metabolite
  • Altered pharmacodynamics

Drug Interactions

Interactions may occur inside or outside the body and may enhance or diminish effect, with beneficial or adverse outcomes.

Definition & Core Classification

A drug interaction is any reaction between an object drug—the drug being affected—and a second substrate or interactant.

ClassWhere / how it actsLecture effect
PharmaceuticalOccurs during compounding, combination, or dilutionLoss of activity of the object drug
PharmacokineticAlters absorption, distribution, or elimination of the object drugChanges the amount or persistence of drug reaching its target
PharmacodynamicAlters the object drug’s effect at its site of actionLecture example: additive action of two drugs with similar actions

Major Interaction Patterns

PatternEquationMeaningExamples in lecture
Addition / additive effect1 + 1 = 2Total effect equals the sum of individual effects; does not exceed the two drugs’ summed effect.Paracetamol + ibuprofen; aspirin + ibuprofen
Synergism1 + 1 = 3Total effect is greater than the sum; the lecture states both drugs should have the same effect, such as both being antimicrobial.Beta-lactam + aminoglycoside

Physical Antagonism

Lecture example: charcoal tablets in poisoning.

Chemical Antagonism

Formation of a complex between an effector drug and another compound. Protamine binds heparin to reverse its action.

Functional / Physiologic Antagonism

Two agents with opposing actions antagonize each other. Lecture examples: adrenaline and histamine, described as vasoconstrictor and vasodilator.

Do Not ConfuseAddition equals the sum. Synergism exceeds the sum with both drugs contributing a similar effect. Potentiation emphasizes an enhancer that may lack the target effect by itself.

Posology: Dosage Factors & Variations in Responsiveness

Physiologic, pathologic, genetic, and environmental factors determine dosage needs and explain why repeated exposure can increase or decrease response.

pp. 4–5

A. Factors Influencing Dosage and Response

1. Physiologic factors

  • Age
    • Pediatric: highly sensitive; underdeveloped hepatic metabolism, inefficient enzyme systems, incompletely developed BBB/blood-aqueous barriers, and immature renal tubular support.
    • Geriatric: may require lesser doses because of adverse reactions; decreased body weight/body fat, intestinal motility, mesenteric blood flow, liver and kidney function, and altered mental function.
  • Sex
  • Weight
  • Pregnancy and lactation
  • Nutrition / plasma protein binding
  • Emotional status
Lecture-Specific NoteThe lecture states that extremes of age require dose adjustment and that geriatric patients are given half of the adult dose.
Lecture InconsistencyThe earlier age table states that elderly patients have higher fat content and less water, while the later posology list states decreased body weight and body fat. Both are retained as presented in the lecture.

2. Pathological factors

  • Comorbid illnesses, especially kidney and liver disease
  • Body weight / body surface area

3. Genetic factors

  • Inherited differences in drug handling and response
  • Idiosyncrasy: abnormal, unpredictable, genetically based reaction, usually seen on the first dose.
  • Genetic polymorphism: existence of two or more phenotypes in a population with respect to a drug effect.

Drug age and medium

Cram Sheet

Rapid review of the entire lecture—definitions, mechanisms, formulas, distinctions, examples, warnings, and exam cues.

FINAL TAB · pp. 1–5

Core Definition

  • Pharmacodynamics = biochemical, biologic, and physiologic effects of drugs.
  • Biochemical = molecular/enzyme MOA; physiologic = observable effect on the body.
  • Supports rational use, SAR/biochemorphology, selectivity, and reduced toxicity.

Receptors & Coupling

  • Receptors determine dose–effect relations, selectivity, agonism, and antagonism.
  • Agonist binds → receptor shape changes → signal transduction → response.
  • Coupling may open/close ion channels, activate second messengers, inhibit function, or turn on function.
  • Spare receptors: maximal effect without full occupancy; present when Kd > EC50.

Drug Targets

  • Regulatory proteins: neurotransmitters, autacoids, hormones.
  • Enzymes: DHFR, AChE, ACE, COX, carbonic anhydrase, polymerases, MAO, neuraminidase, Na–K ATPase, reverse transcriptase.
  • Transport proteins: Na–K ATPase, NE/serotonin transporters, dopamine/GABA reuptake systems.
  • Structural protein: tubulin for colchicine.

Non-Receptor Actions

  • Adsorption: charcoal, simethicone.
  • Bulk: psyllium, bran, fibrosine.
  • Osmotic: mannitol.
  • Colligative: MgSO4, menthol, tannic acid.
  • Neutralization: antacids.
  • Chelation: penicillamine, dimercaprol.
  • Radioactivity: I-131; radio-opacity: barium sulfate.
  • Metabolic analog: sulfonamide.

Four Drug Properties

  • Affinity: binding tenacity.
  • Efficacy: maximum response / plateau.
  • Intrinsic activity: activation by one drug–receptor complex.
  • Potency: dose required; x-axis / EC50 or ED50.

Receptor Families

  • Intracellular: lipid-soluble; steroid/thyroid/Vit D; gene expression; ≥30 min; hours–days.
  • Enzyme-linked: insulin/EGF; tyrosine kinase; autophosphorylation.
  • JAK–STAT: cytokine receptor → JAK → STAT phosphorylation → dimerization → nuclear transcription.
  • Ligand-gated: milliseconds; nicotinic Na+ influx; GABA Cl− influx/hyperpolarization.
  • GPCR: seven-transmembrane; Gq activates PLC, Gi inhibits AC, Gs activates AC.

Graded Curve

  • Magnitude of response in an individual / given population.
  • Efficacy = plateau.
  • Potency = x-axis / ED50.
  • Steep slope = narrow therapeutic range.
  • Biologic variability reflects different dose needs.

Agonist Spectrum

  • Full agonist: affinity 1, IA 1.
  • Partial agonist: affinity 1, IA <0.5; submaximal even at 100% occupancy.
  • Antagonist: affinity 1, IA 0.
  • Inverse agonist: affinity 1, IA −1; opposite-direction effect.

Antagonists

  • Competitive: same site, reversible, surmountable, right shift, ↑ED50.
  • Non-competitive: other site, irreversible/covalent in lecture, insurmountable, ↓maximal response.
  • Examples: atropine vs acetylcholine; naloxone vs morphine; phenoxybenzamine.

Quantal Curve

  • Percentage of population showing a defined response.
  • ED50 = response in half the population.
  • May be all-or-none.
  • Uses: median therapeutic/toxic dose, safety margin/TI, selectivity, variability.

Safety Formulas

  • Human TI = TD50/ED50
  • Animal TI = LD50/ED50
  • CSF = LD1/ED99
  • SSM% = [(LD1−ED99)/ED99] ×100
  • Higher TI / wider window = safer in lecture framework.

Pharmacogenetics

  • Heredity affects PK and PD responses.
  • Variability causes: age, genes, immune factors, disease, interactions.
  • Consequences: altered clearance, functional overdose, failed prodrug activation, altered PD, idiosyncrasy.
  • Age: pediatric has underdeveloped liver/kidney and more water; elderly has less renal/hepatic function, less water, more fat.

CYP Differences

  • CYP3A4: 50% of clinically available drug metabolism.
  • CYP2D6 deficiency: Caucasians 1–7%; Asian/Afro-Americans 1–3%; antipsychotic toxicity → dyskinesia.
  • CYP2C19 deficiency: Caucasians 1–3%; Asians 30%; omeprazole effect may promote faster ulcer healing.

Interaction Equations

  • Addition: 1+1=2.
  • Synergism: 1+1=3.
  • Potentiation: enhancer may lack the target effect.
  • Antagonism: 1+1=0.
  • Physical: charcoal; chemical: protamine–heparin; functional: adrenaline–histamine.

Posology Factors

  • Physiologic: age, sex, weight, pregnancy/lactation, nutrition, emotional status.
  • Pathologic: kidney/liver disease, weight/surface area.
  • Genetic: idiosyncrasy and polymorphism.
  • Environmental: route, timing, climate, race, preparation, drug age, medium.

Genetic Examples

  • Acetyltransferase: slow → peripheral neuropathy; rapid → hepatitis.
  • Pseudocholinesterase + succinylcholine → apnea.
  • Malignant hyperthermia + succinylcholine → calcium release, spasm, high temperature.
  • G6PD + oxidants → hemolytic anemia.

Responsiveness

  • Tachyphylaxis: rapid, 1–3 days; ephedrine/salbutamol.
  • Tolerance: gradual, higher dose; opioids.
  • Downregulation: fewer receptors after agonist exposure.
  • Upregulation: more receptors after prolonged blockade.
  • Idiosyncrasy: genetic, unpredictable, often first dose.
  • Hypersensitivity: immune-mediated allergy/autoimmunity.

Exam Cues

  • Plateau → efficacy.
  • Left/right position → potency.
  • Right shift → competitive antagonist.
  • Lower maximum → non-competitive antagonist.
  • Milliseconds → ligand-gated channel.
  • Hours to days → intracellular receptor.
  • Population proportion → quantal curve.
  • Rapid repeated-dose decline → tachyphylaxis.
