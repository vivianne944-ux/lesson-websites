Must KnowPharmacokinetics is what the body does to a drug. Its core parameters are ADME: absorption, distribution, metabolism, and elimination. The lecture’s prescribing reminder is to give the right drug, right dose, right time, and right patient.

The lecture emphasizes that only the free drug is readily distributed and able to produce an effect, while protein-bound drug acts as a reservoir. Drug movement and exposure are then shaped by solubility, ionization, concentration gradients, vascularity, surface area, first-pass metabolism, protein binding, tissue affinity, and physiologic barriers.

Major transport types

Aqueous diffusion, lipid diffusion, special carriers, endocytosis, and exocytosis. Carrier-mediated transport is selective, saturable, and inhibitable; active transport and vesicular transport require energy.

Bioavailability determinants

Extent of absorption, first-pass elimination, and rate of absorption, modified by dosage form, physiology, GIT interactions, and patient status.

Distribution endpoints

Drug may reach a site of action, bind plasma or tissues, enter a storage depot, undergo metabolism, or be excreted.

Pharmacokinetics versus pharmacodynamics

Key DifferentiatorPharmacoKKKinetics = Katawan: what the body does to a drug. PharmacoDDDynamics = Drugs: what the drug does on the body.

Pharmacokinetics describes how the body handles the drug and dictates its fate through ADME.

AbsorptionPassage from the site of administration into circulation.
DistributionTransfer from plasma to tissues and body compartments.
MetabolismBiotransformation into an active or inactive metabolite.
EliminationRemoval of drug; the lecture identifies the kidney as the major excretory organ.

Plasma fate after absorption

After absorption, a drug enters the plasma compartment and becomes either free or bound.

  • Free drug: can distribute to different parts of the body and produce an effect.
  • Bound drug: is less available and serves as a reservoir.
  • Albumin: the largest protein described in the lecture as carrying or transporting drugs.

From plasma, free drug may reach the site of action, enter a tissue depot, be metabolized in the liver, lung, and GIT, or be excreted.

Route of administration: path to systemic circulation

RouteLecture sequenceKey point
OralGut → liver → vein → heart → lungs → aorta → arterial circulationSafest route among the routes discussed; undergoes first-pass effect.
ParenteralVein → right side of heart → lungs → aorta → arterial circulation“Anything that uses needles,” such as IV. The IV route has the highest possibility of toxicity.
InhalationLungs → left side of heart → aorta → arterial circulationShortest route sequence among the three.
Lecture-Specific NoteDuration of route journey: oral > parenteral > inhalation. Regardless of route, the drug must ultimately reach systemic circulation and its target area.

Drug formulation

Drug formulation is the process in which chemical substances, including the active drug, are combined to produce a final medicinal product with a dosage form.

Factors of drug formulation

  • Barriers the drug can pass; absorption requires membrane penetration.
  • Setting: outpatient or hospital use.
  • Urgency of the medical situation.
  • Stability of the drug.
  • First-pass effect: liver metabolism reduces the concentration of active component.

Permeation & Transport Processes

How drugs move into and within the biological environment.

Pages 1–2

Permeation depends on three major conditions

SolubilityLipid solubility helps a drug cross lipid bilayers; water solubility helps dissolution.
Concentration gradientOnly free and unionized forms influence efficient diffusion.
Surface area & vascularityLarger absorptive area and greater blood supply improve absorption.
Clinical CueMost drugs are absorbed in the small intestine because of its large surface area and rich blood supply. The stomach has a pH of 1.5–2.5, gastric emptying time of 3–4 hours, and a thicker mucus layer that makes diffusion more difficult.

Lecture examples of drugs absorbed in the stomach include NSAIDs, aspirin, theophylline, warfarin, and phenytoin. The lecture notes that theophylline, warfarin, and phenytoin have a narrow margin of safety. Delayed gastric emptying delays delivery to the intestine and can delay absorption.

01 · Aqueous diffusion

  • Occurs within interstitial space, cytosol, epithelial tight junctions, and endothelial lining through aqueous pores.
  • Driven by the drug’s concentration gradient.
  • Governed by Fick’s Law of Diffusion.

02 · Lipid diffusion

  • Movement through lipid membranes separating body compartments and between extracellular and intracellular fluid.
  • Described as the most important limiting factor for drug permeation.
  • Governed by Fick’s Law.
  • Partition coefficient: ratio of solubility in an organic solvent to solubility in an aqueous solution.
  • Absorption increases as lipid solubility increases.

03 · Special carriers

  • Used by peptides, amino acids, and glucose.
  • Selective, saturable, and inhibitable.
  • Carry ions through the Na⁺/K⁺ pump, metabolites such as glucose, neurotransmitters through reuptake transporters, and xenobiotics.
  • Operate through active transport or facilitated diffusion.
  • Not governed by Fick’s Law.
Exam TrapThe lecture says special carriers “require energy,” but later review rationales specifically distinguish active transport as ATP-requiring and facilitated diffusion as carrier-mediated without energy. Keep the two mechanisms separate.

04 · Endocytosis & exocytosis

These are opposite, energy-requiring processes.

  • Endocytosis: cell membrane engulfment. Small polar drug–protein complexes are engulfed; large drugs bind receptors and are released after vesicle breakdown.
  • Exocytosis: reverse process responsible for secretion from the cell.
  • Lecture examples: iron bound to transferrin and vitamin B12 bound to intrinsic factor.
High-Yield RecallLUNA → absorbed: Lipophilic, Unionized, Non-polar. HIPE → excreted: Hydrophilic (water-soluble), Ionized, Polar.

TermMeaning in the lecture
C₁Higher concentration
C₂Lower concentration
AreaCross-sectional area of the diffusion path
Permeability coefficientMobility of the drug molecule in the diffusion medium
ThicknessLength of the diffusion path
If You See X, Think YThinner membrane → faster absorption (lung faster than skin). Larger surface area → faster absorption (intestine faster than stomach).

Ionization of weak acids and bases

Drugs exist as weak acids or weak bases. To cross biologic membranes, they should be in their lipid-soluble, uncharged form.

  • Weak acid: lipid-soluble form is protonated.
  • Weak base: lipid-soluble form is unprotonated.
  • pH determines the fraction of ionized versus non-ionized molecules.
Do Not ConfuseProtonated does not always mean lipid soluble. It is lipid soluble for a weak acid, but water soluble for a weak base.

Henderson–Hasselbalch equation

General form
pH = pKa + log(Unprotonated ÷ Protonated)
Weak acid
pH = pKa + log([A] ÷ [HA])
Weak base
pH = pKa + log([B] ÷ [BH])

The equation relates the ratio of protonated to unprotonated forms to the drug’s pKa and the medium’s pH. Only the uncharged species diffuses through lipid membranes.

pH partition is the tendency of weak acids to accumulate in relatively high-pH compartments and weak bases to show the opposite pattern.

  • pH < pKa: greater fraction is protonated; more weak acid is lipid soluble at acidic pH.
  • pH > pKa: more basic drug is lipid soluble at alkaline pH.
FormWeak acidWeak base
UnprotonatedWater soluble; excreted fasterLipid soluble; crosses biologic membranes
ProtonatedLipid soluble; crosses biologic membranesWater soluble

Lecture practice problem

Drug X is a weak acid with pKa 2.5 in the stomach at pH 1.5.

Substitution
1.5 = 2.5 + log(U ÷ P) → U/P = 10⁻¹ = 0.10

The lecture reports approximately 10% unprotonated and 90% protonated. Because Drug X is a weak acid, the protonated form is lipid soluble.

Consequences of pH partition

  • Urinary acidification: accelerates excretion of weak bases and retards excretion of weak acids.
  • Urinary alkalinization: has the opposite effect.
  • Increasing plasma pH: causes weakly acidic drugs to be extracted from the CNS into plasma.
Clinical Cue from LectureFor aspirin poisoning, the lecture recommends alkalinizing the urine to extract the weak acid from CNS and plasma. Listed substances: potassium citrate, sodium bicarbonate, and sodium citrate.

Definition and route categories

Drug absorption is the passage of a drug from its site of administration into the circulation. The route of administration is the major factor influencing absorption and determines how quickly and how much drug reaches the circulation.

CategoryRoutes listed in the lecture
EnteralSublingual, oral, rectal
ParenteralIntravenous, intramuscular, subcutaneous, intrathecal
OtherTransdermal, inhalational, topical
Topical examplesOintments, creams, otic solutions, ophthalmic solutions

01 · Lipid solubility

The more lipid soluble the drug, the faster its absorption.

02 · pH at the site

Depends on whether the drug is a weak acid or weak base. Acidic environments favor absorption of weak acids, and vice versa.

03 · Vascularity

The greater the blood supply at the absorption site, the better the absorption.

04 · Contact time

Longer presence at the absorptive surface improves absorption. Lecture shorthand: longer time = better absorption.

05 · Drug concentration

Higher drug concentration generally makes absorption faster.

06 · Membrane surface area

A larger surface area, as in the small intestine, produces faster absorption.

High-Yield RecapFast absorption is favored by: high lipid solubility, favorable pH, strong vascularity, longer contact time, higher concentration, and larger membrane surface area.

Definition and absolute bioavailability

Bioavailability (f) is the fraction of unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation after administration by any route. It depends strongly on the route: IV administration gives 100% bioavailability.

Bioavailability is reduced by first-pass effect, presystemic redistribution, or incomplete absorption. The lecture determines it using the area under the plasma concentration–time curve (AUC).

Absolute bioavailability
f = (AUCPO ÷ dosePO) ÷ (AUCIV ÷ doseIV)
The lecture also shows the simplified AUCPO ÷ AUCIV form.

AUC represents the body’s exposure to the drug and, under first-order elimination, is proportional to dose and extent of bioavailability.

Lecture graph formula
Bioavailability = (AUC oral ÷ AUC injected) × 100
AUC is used to measure how much drug reaches the bloodstream over time and is used for dosing and identifying potential drug interactions.

Depends on lipophilicity, hydrophilicity, and the presence of P-glycoproteins.

02 · First-pass elimination

Extraction ratio
ER = CLliver ÷ Q

Q is hepatic blood flow. The source prints “normal = 0 L/h for a person weighing 70 kg”; this is retained as a lecture-specific printed value rather than silently altered.

03 · Rate of absorption

Determined by the site of administration and the drug formulation.

Factors affecting bioavailability

Biopharmaceutical factors

Dosage form, excipients, and physicochemical properties influence disintegration and dissolution.

Dissolution orderSolutions > suspensions > capsules > compressed tablets > time-released tablets.

Physiologic factors

  • Gastric acidity: acidic conditions favor weak acids. The lecture’s favorable gastric profile is a small, weakly acidic, highly concentrated drug.
  • Drug market distribution: lecturer note says 75% are weak bases and 25% weak acids.
  • Gastric emptying: normal time is 3–4 hours. Delayed emptying delays small-intestinal absorption.
  • Food: may increase, delay, or decrease absorption depending on drug properties. High-fat meals slow gastric emptying, increase bile secretion, and stimulate splanchnic blood flow.
  • Exercise: low-to-moderate activity shortens emptying; high-intensity exercise delays it as blood is diverted to skeletal muscle.
  • Small bowel motility: faster motility lowers absorption, especially for poorly absorbed drugs.
  • Gut-wall absorption and GIT perfusion: higher absorption and greater vascularity increase bioavailability.

First-pass effect

Also called presystemic metabolism. Orally administered drugs enter portal circulation and may be metabolized by the liver before reaching systemic circulation, reducing bioavailability.

Clinical CueNitroglycerin is given sublingually to bypass extensive first-pass metabolism.

GIT interactions and patient status

  • Food: may delay or enhance absorption.
  • Drug interaction: calcium- and magnesium-containing antacids chelate tetracycline, rendering it ineffective.
  • Bacterial growth is listed as a GIT interaction factor.
  • Patient status: age, nutrition, and gender.

Volume of distribution (Vd)

The apparent volume of biologic fluid into which a drug appears to distribute homogeneously at the concentration found in blood, plasma, or plasma water.

It relates the prescribed dose to the expected target plasma concentration.

Formula
Vd = Total amount of drug (dose) ÷ Plasma concentration
Vd = Dose (Q) ÷ Cp
Lecture example
500 mg ÷ 5 mg/L = 100 L

The lecture states that most drugs have Vd = 0.1–0.6 L/kg.

What changes Vd?

  • Tissue binding lowers plasma concentration and makes apparent Vd larger.
  • Plasma protein binding raises plasma concentration and makes apparent Vd smaller.
Key DifferentiatorLow Vd: drug remains largely vascular. High Vd: drug is bound in tissues and widely distributed.

Drugs with very high Vd have much higher extravascular than vascular concentrations and may require larger loading doses to reach a target plasma concentration.

Distribution and redistribution

Distribution is the reversible transfer of drug from blood to tissues and body compartments. Distribution and elimination occur simultaneously, but distribution is usually faster.

Regional blood flow strongly influences how rapidly tissues are exposed to a drug. Redistribution is movement from more perfused organs to less perfused organs.

Lecture ExampleThiopental is an ultrashort-acting anesthetic that rapidly induces anesthesia, then loses effect as it redistributes to other tissues.
Central compartmentWell-perfused tissues with rapid equilibration: kidney, heart, brain.
Peripheral compartmentLess-perfused tissues with slower equilibration: fat, bone, muscle.

Clinical significance: tissues receive drugs at different rates, influencing onset, intensity, and duration of action.

Single-compartment model

Drug appears to distribute instantaneously and remains mainly in the central compartment.

Multi-compartment model

Drug first distributes from central to peripheral tissues before elimination.

  • Distribution phase: plasma concentration falls as drug enters tissues.
  • Elimination phase: drug is removed from the central compartment.
  • Steady state: dynamic balance between input and elimination during continued dosing; lecturer note describes equal net flux between central and peripheral compartments.

Drug forms in blood

  • Free form: active form.
  • Protein-bound: inactive form in the lecture.
  • Metabolites: usually the degraded form.

Initial distribution

Depends on local blood flow.

Final distribution

Depends on the drug’s affinity for the tissue site. More lipophilic drugs enter the CNS or fat; more hydrophilic drugs remain localized in the vascular compartment.

Clinical significance of plasma protein binding

  • Delays onset of action.
  • Prolongs duration of action.
  • May require a higher dose.
  • Acts as a reservoir.
  • May cause drug–drug interaction; examples: sulfonamides and warfarin.
  • Diseases causing hypoalbuminemia reduce protein binding.
  • This increases the free drug level.
  • Lecture example: chronic renal failure.
  • Free drug is available for metabolism.
Exam TrapAn increase in free-drug plasma levels may result in potential toxicity.

Storage sites

Adipose tissue

Stores lipid-soluble drugs. Thiopental binds in adipose tissue. Fat has a low metabolic rate and is poorly perfused, so storage can prolong drug duration.

Bone

Storage site for toxic substances such as arsenic, mercury, lead, and tetracycline.

Muscle

Drugs form reversible bonds with intracellular structures such as nucleoproteins and phospholipids. Quinacrine binds nucleoproteins.

Organs and cells

Drugs enter cells passively or through active transport. The lecture lists aminoglycosides such as streptomycin; gentamicin is stored in renal proximal tubule cells; aluminum is stored in kidneys, liver, and neurons and can damage them.

Determinants and barriers

Factors Controlling Distribution & Retention

Eight lecture-listed determinants, including blood flow, binding, physicochemical properties, BBB, and placenta.

Page 5

1 · Blood flow to tissues

Faster blood flow / better blood supply → greater drug distribution.

2 · Cardiac output

Increased cardiac output → faster drug distribution.

3 · Plasma protein binding

  • More protein bound → slower distribution.
  • High protein binding plus narrow therapeutic range predisposes to interaction; examples: warfarin and phenytoin.
  • Albumin binds weak acids; α1-acid glycoprotein binds weak bases.

4 · Tissue binding

Competition for plasma binding sites increases the free fraction and enhances effect. The lecture states that increased tissue binding means better distribution.

5 · Lipid content of tissue

The more lipid soluble the drug, the more easily it distributes.

6 · Physicochemical properties

  • Unionized → extensive distribution.
  • Ionized → less distribution because drug becomes trapped; depends on pH and weak acid/base character.
  • Most drug molecular weights are listed as 100–1000.
  • Smaller molecular size → more extensive distribution.

7 · Blood–brain barrier

Better penetration requires a drug to be lipid soluble, low molecular weight, and have a high lipid–water partition coefficient. Tight junctions in brain endothelial cells restrict entry.

8 · Placental barrier

Efflux transporters move drug from fetus back to mother.

High-Yield RecapFor broad distribution, think high blood flow, high cardiac output, low plasma binding, greater tissue binding, lipid solubility, unionized form, and smaller molecular size. For CNS entry, add low molecular weight and high lipid–water partition coefficient.

Cram Sheet

Condensed definitions, equations, sequences, comparisons, traps, and lecture cues.

Entire lecture
Core spinePharmacokinetics = body → drug. Think ADME. The route gets drug to systemic circulation; membrane transport controls entry; absorption and first-pass effect shape bioavailability; blood flow, binding, tissue affinity, and barriers shape distribution.

Definitions & pathways

  • Absorption: site of administration → circulation.
  • Distribution: reversible blood → tissue transfer.
  • Metabolism: biotransformation to active or inactive metabolite; liver is the major metabolic machine.
  • Elimination: major excretory organ is the kidney.
  • Oral path: gut → liver → vein → heart → lungs → aorta → arteries.
  • Parenteral path: vein → right heart → lungs → aorta → arteries.
  • Inhalation path: lungs → left heart → aorta → arteries.
  • Route duration: oral > parenteral > inhalation.

Transport recall

  • Aqueous diffusion: pores and tight junctions; gradient driven; Fick-governed.
  • Lipid diffusion: major limiting factor for permeation; Fick-governed.
  • Special carriers: selective, saturable, inhibitable; not Fick-governed.
  • Active transport: ATP required.
  • Facilitated diffusion: carrier, no ATP in review rationale.
  • Endocytosis/exocytosis: energy-requiring vesicular processes.
  • LUNA: lipophilic, unionized, non-polar → absorbed.
  • HIPE: hydrophilic, ionized, polar → excreted.

Absorption & bioavailability

  • Major factor: route of administration.
  • Faster absorption: more lipid soluble, favorable pH, vascular, concentrated, long-contact, large-area.
  • Small intestine: major absorption site due to large surface and rich blood supply.
  • IV: 100% bioavailability; most rapid; high toxicity possibility.
  • Oral: safest/most convenient; first-pass effect lowers bioavailability.
  • Rectal: less first pass than oral.
  • Transdermal: slow absorption, prolonged action.
  • Dissolution: solutions > suspensions > capsules > compressed tablets > time-release.
  • Gastric emptying: 3–4 h; high-fat meals slow; low/moderate exercise speeds; high-intensity exercise delays.
  • First-pass cue: sublingual nitroglycerin bypasses extensive hepatic first pass.
  • Interaction: Ca/Mg antacids chelate tetracycline.

Distribution & Vd

  • Free drug: active, distributable; high free level may cause toxicity.
  • Bound drug: reservoir; delays onset, prolongs duration, slows distribution.
  • Low Vd: plasma confinement / high plasma binding.
  • High Vd: tissue binding / widespread distribution / larger loading dose may be needed.
  • Redistribution: more perfused → less perfused; thiopental example.
  • Central: rapid equilibration; kidney, heart, brain.
  • Peripheral: slow equilibration; fat, bone, muscle.
  • BBB entry: lipid soluble + low molecular weight + high lipid–water partition coefficient.
  • Placenta: efflux transporters move drug fetus → mother.
