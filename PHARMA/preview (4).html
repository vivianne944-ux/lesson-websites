Whole-Lesson Overview

This lecture presents a complete framework for ethical and rational prescribing: define the problem, set goals, choose treatment, score drug options using ESSC, write a clear prescription, counsel the patient, and monitor outcomes.

Pages 1–11

Core prescribing pathway

Diagnosis→ Treatment goals→ Treatment selection→ Prescription & counseling→ Monitoring

A rational prescription balances clinical effectiveness, patient safety, suitability, cost, and patient choice.

⭐ MUST-KNOW ANCHORS
ESSC = Efficacy, Safety, Suitability, Cost.
P-drug = preferred drug selected through a structured comparison.
Prescription orders must be legible, unambiguous, dated, timed when applicable, and clearly signed.
Counseling ends with confirmation of understanding.

Why rational therapy matters

  • More than half of medicines are prescribed, dispensed, or sold inappropriately.
  • About half of patients fail to take medicines correctly.
  • About one-third of the world lacks access to essential medicines.

Ethical prescribing goals

  • Maximize effectiveness
  • Minimize harm
  • Avoid waste
  • Respect patient choice

Final responsibilities

  • Write correctly.
  • Counsel clearly.
  • Monitor regularly.
  • Keep drug knowledge updated.

Rational & Ethical Prescribing

The lesson proper begins with what makes prescribing rational, ethical, and clinically responsible.

Page 1

Prescribing is an art requiring

  • Diagnostic skills
  • Knowledge of medicines
  • Clinical pharmacology principles
  • Communication skills
  • Appreciation of risks and benefits in a specific patient

WHO definitions

The 1952 definition emphasizes medications appropriate to the patient’s clinical needs, at doses meeting individual requirements, for an adequate period, and at the lowest cost to the patient and community.

The 1985 revision condenses this to the right drug, adequate dose, sufficient duration, appropriate to clinical needs, at the lowest cost.

⭐ ETHICAL PRESCRIBING REQUIRES
Evaluate the problem → set therapeutic objectives → select an appropriate medication → consider cost → give information, warnings, and instructions → monitor regularly.

Prescription-order standards

  • Legible
  • Unambiguous
  • Dated
  • Timed for chart orders
  • Clearly signed

These support optimal communication among the prescriber, pharmacist, and nurse.

Four ethical priorities

  1. Maximize clinical effectiveness
  2. Minimize harms
  3. Avoid wasting scarce healthcare materials
  4. Respect patient choice

Prescribing Errors & Medication Adherence

Many errors arise not from the drug itself, but from incomplete orders, poor communication, missed contraindications, and improper use.

Pages 1–2

Omission of information

  • “Continue present IVF” fails to identify the exact intravenous fluid.
  • “Resume preoperative meds” assumes a complete and accurate medication record is available.
⚠️ CORE ERROR
A prescription should never require the reader to guess what the prescriber intended.

Poor prescription writing

Illegible handwriting and ambiguous decimal placement can create major dosing errors.

  • 0.1 may be misread as 1.
  • 1.0 may be misread as 10.

Why inappropriate prescriptions occur

  • Failure to recognize contraindications caused by comorbid disease.
  • Failure to obtain information about other medications.
  • Failure to recognize physicochemical incompatibilities.
  • The lecture notes that some insulin preparations should not be mixed.
  • Antacids or products with high metal content may reduce intestinal absorption of drugs such as tetracycline.

Fails to obtain medicationPrescription never filledNo therapeutic exposure occurs

✅ ADHERENCE TAKEAWAY
Good prescribing includes a drug choice that the patient can realistically obtain, understand, and use correctly. Adherence is part of suitability, not an afterthought.

Legal, Regulatory & Socioeconomic Factors

Ethical prescribing is shaped by drug classification, professional licensing, safety surveillance, generic-prescribing laws, and treatment cost.

Page 2

Drug classes

OTC drugs may be safely used by laypersons for self-limiting conditions.

Prescription drugs require a licensed physician, especially controlled substances such as:

  • Opioids
  • Hallucinogens
  • Stimulants
  • Depressants
  • Anabolic steroids

The lecture notes that physicians need an S-2 license to prescribe regulated or controlled drugs.

Drug safety surveillance

  • Government regulates labeling and distribution.
  • Licensing boards control who may prescribe.
  • The Philippine National Formulary identifies drugs purchasable by government hospitals and reimbursable by PhilHealth.
  • The FDA monitors drug safety using mandatory company reporting and voluntary practitioner reporting.

Labeled and off-label use

Off-label use means using an approved drug for a condition, dosage form, or dose not included in its approved labeling.

The lecture emphasizes that labeled indications may not include every situation in which a drug may be useful.

Generic prescribing

  • Physicians must prescribe the generic name.
  • This permits dispensing a generically equivalent and cheaper option.
  • The lecture notes that a brand may be listed, but substitution restrictions are governed by law.
  • Cost also depends on quantity dispensed and refills authorized.
⭐ REPUBLIC ACT NO. 6675
The lecture states that doctors may not instruct pharmacists not to substitute the brand or use “dispense only as prescribed.” In DOH facilities, generic names or terminology are to be used in procurement, prescribing, dispensing, and administration.
⚠️ GENERIC-PRODUCT CONCERNS LISTED IN THE LECTURE
The lecture mentions possible concerns such as a high inert-to-active ingredient ratio, poor solubility, and low therapeutic index, while also stating that unsatisfactory generic products are rare.

The P-Drug Selection Process

A well-chosen P-drug depends on correct assessment of the patient’s problem and knowledge of pharmacologic properties.

Pages 2–3

Step-by-step process

  1. Determine the diagnosis
  2. Identify treatment goals
  3. Select the treatment
  4. Prescribe and counsel
  5. Monitor the patient

The expanded P-drug outline adds non-drug measures, an inventory of effective drug groups, and then comparison of specific drugs.

Diagnosis

Prescribing decisions should be based on the primary diagnosis and relevant secondary diagnoses.

🧠 SAMPLE
A diabetic patient with fever, cough, and dyspnea: Primary diagnosis = pneumonia; secondary diagnosis = type 2 diabetes mellitus.

Treatment goals

Goals may focus on cure, relief, or prevention.

For pneumonia: cure the infection, ease coughing, and prevent complications or recurrence.

Treatment selection

Options may include advice and information, non-drug treatment, drug treatment, or referral.

⭐ CORE PRINCIPLE
Not all health problems need drugs, and not all treatment plans should consist entirely of medications.

High-yield recap

Before choosing a drug, clearly define the patient’s problem, the primary objective, and whether a non-drug strategy or referral is sufficient.

ESSC Criteria

ESSC provides the structured comparison used to select a P-drug group and then a specific P-drug.

Pages 3, 5, 7

Efficacy

How well the drug achieves the intended therapeutic objective.

Safety

Frequency, seriousness, predictability, and toxicity of adverse effects.

Suitability

How well the drug fits this specific patient and context.

Cost

Total expense of the full treatment course.

Efficacy details

  • Mechanism of action
  • Pharmacokinetics: onset, duration, bioavailability, distribution, metabolism, excretion
  • Pharmacodynamics: specificity, selectivity, dose–response, therapeutic index
  • Spectrum of activity for antibiotics
  • Journal evidence, when available

Safety details

  • List adverse effects from common to rare.
  • Consider toxicity.
  • Assess how frequent, serious, and predictable harms are.

Suitability details

  • Previous adverse reactions
  • Very young, elderly, pregnant, or comorbid patients
  • Current medications and interactions
  • Adherence likelihood
  • Dose frequency and dosage form
  • Taste and convenience
  • Local availability and practicality

Cost details

  • Compare the total treatment cost.
  • Cheaper alternatives may be preferred if efficacy is similar.
  • Rate options using plus signs from + to ++++.
  • Compare each criterion side by side per drug.

Two-stage selection

  1. Compare drug groups using ESSC.
  2. Select the group with the highest total score.
  3. List specific drugs within that group.
  4. Score the specific drugs again using ESSC.
  5. The highest-scoring specific drug becomes the P-drug.
✅ SYNCHRONOUS NOTE
When drugs within one group share the same mechanism, compare their PK and PD under efficacy instead of repeating the same MOA.
⚠️ COST SCORING NOTE
For lecture scoring, the price of special equipment such as a nebulizer does not need to be included in the ESSC cost column. In actual practice, still ask whether the patient has the required equipment before prescribing the corresponding drug.

Selection is incomplete until the drug is communicated correctly and the patient understands how to use it.

Counseling categoryMinimum information to cover

⭐ “EVERYTHING CLEAR?”
Ask whether everything is understood. Ask the patient to repeat the most important instructions. Ask whether they have questions.

Monitoring

Monitoring should confirm whether the drug is producing the intended effect, causing adverse effects, or requiring adjustment.

The lecture example asks patients with hypertension to bring a blood-pressure log to the next visit.

Keeping drug knowledge current

Sources listed in the lecture include reference books, drug compendia, treatment guidelines, formularies, bulletins, journals, drug information centers, and computerized tools.

Lecture Cases 1–3

The cases demonstrate how diagnosis, objectives, non-drug measures, drug-group inventory, ESSC scoring, and prescription writing fit together.

Pages 4–7

Case 1: Post-inflammatory cough

  • Patient: 52-year-old taxi driver with persistent nighttime cough after a cold; heavy smoker.
  • Diagnosis: post-inflammatory cough.
  • Therapeutic objective: suppress the cough.
  • Non-drug measure: stop smoking.
  • Possible P-drug groups: opioid agonists and antihistamines.

Case 2: Acute gastroenteritis with mild dehydration

  • Patient: 1-year-old with seven watery stools, thirst, dry lips, and slightly sunken eyes.
  • Diagnosis: acute gastroenteritis, mild dehydration, probably viral.
  • Therapeutic objective: rehydrate and prevent further dehydration.
  • Non-drug measure: continue breastfeeding.
  • Drug options: ORS, opioid agonist, zinc sulfate.
✅ COMPLETE DIAGNOSIS
The lecture emphasizes including the illness, hydration status, and etiology when relevant because these guide management.

Case 2 P-drug selection

ORS is the preferred drug group. Among listed ORS products, Glucolyte Plus receives the highest total score because its efficacy is similar to alternatives while cost is lower.

The lecture notes that Pedialyte 45 is more convenient because it is already prepared, whereas Glucolyte and Hydrite require mixing.

Case 3: Stable angina

  • Problem: stable angina pectoris.
  • Primary objective: stop the attack as soon as it begins.
  • Mechanistic objective: reduce preload, contractility, and afterload.
  • Drug groups: nitrates, beta blockers, calcium-channel blockers.
  • Preferred group: nitrates.
  • Preferred specific drug: isosorbide dinitrate.
⭐ SYNCHRONOUS CASE NOTES
Compare criteria side by side. List the primary therapeutic objective first. A symptomatic-relief prescription is generally written for 3–5 days. If the patient can afford an innovator product, it may be prescribed; otherwise consider the cheapest option.

Case summary

  • Patient: 9-year-old girl, previously well.
  • Symptoms: sudden itchy red wheals on arms, legs, and trunk; lesions migrate and come and go.
  • No red flags listed: no fever, respiratory symptoms, or abdominal pain.
  • History: similar episodes with climate change and travel; family history of atopy.
  • Impression: urticaria.

Therapeutic objectives

  1. Resolve acute urticaria
  2. Reduce itching and rash
  3. Prevent trigger exposure through education

Non-drug measures

  • Keep a symptom diary.
  • Identify and reduce exposure to likely triggers.
  • Avoid scratching.
  • Use cool compresses.
  • Wear loose, soft cotton clothing.
  • Avoid rough fabrics.

Drug groups considered

  • First-generation oral antihistamines
  • Second-generation oral antihistamines
  • Oral glucocorticoids

The appendix identifies second-generation antihistamines as the highest-scoring group.

✅ MASTER CORRECTION
Rational prescribing is patient-specific, goal-directed, evidence-informed, cost-conscious, and monitored.
