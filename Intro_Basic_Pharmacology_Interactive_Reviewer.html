Big picture: Pharmacology is the **science of drugs** from identity → development → action → prescribing.

This reviewer follows the lecture sequence: **what a drug is**, how drugs are **named and prepared**, the **branches of pharmacology**, how drugs move through **development and clinical trials**, how **placebo and drug actions** work, and the basic parts of a **prescription**.

High-yield framework p.1–6

  1. Pharmacology = from pharmacos meaning drug and logos meaning study.
  2. Branches classify what you are asking: dose? toxicity? ADME? mechanism? cost? heredity?
  3. Drug development moves from pre-clinical animal testing → FDA safety review → clinical trials → post-marketing surveillance.
  4. Drug action may cure, alleviate symptoms, replenish deficiencies, stimulate, depress, irritate, replace, prevent, or act locally/systemically.
  5. Prescription writing must include correct parts, generic name, legibility, and patient directions.

Core terms: drug, medicine, prototype drug, drug names, and pharmaceutical preparations.

These are the definitions that make the rest of the lecture easier: know whether the question is asking about a chemical substance, a preparation, a class representative, or a name used in prescriptions.

Drug p.1

A chemical substance of known structure that produces a biological effect when administered to a living organism.

Lecture note: it encompasses all, whether good or bad, prohibited or not prohibited, including over-the-counter drugs.

  • Molecular size: often 100–1000 daltons.
  • Shape: should be complementary to the receptor to induce an effect.
  • Chemical nature: majority are either weak acids or weak bases.
If you see: “known structure + biological effect” → think drug.

Medicine p.1

A chemical preparation that usually contains one or more drugs to produce a therapeutic effect.

Trap: A drug is the substance; a medicine is the preparation containing drug/s for therapeutic effect.

Prototype drugs p.1

Prototype drugs typify the most important characteristics of a drug class/group.

  • Omeprazole → proton pump inhibitors.
  • Losartan → angiotensin receptor blockers.
  • Cimetidine → H2 blockers.
  • Propranolol → beta blockers.
If you see: “represents class characteristics” → think prototype drug.

Drug names p.1–2

  • Chemical name = chemical structure of the drug, e.g. (RS)-2-(4-(2-methylpropyl)phenyl)propanoic acid.
  • Nonproprietary / generic name = name understood worldwide, e.g. Ibuprofen.
  • Proprietary name = trade/brand name; may or may not be written in the prescription and is written below the generic name, e.g. Alaxan.
⭐ Must know
All prescriptions should bear the generic name of the drug and should be written legibly to prevent medication errors.

Branches of Pharmacology: learn them as “question types.”

Most branch questions are scenario-based. Ask: is the scenario about dose, toxicity, ADME, mechanism, cost, heredity, population, pregnancy/fetus, or natural crude drugs?

Pharmacy p.2

Allied branch involved in compounding, preparation, collection, standardization, and dispensing of drugs. Also involves plant cultivations used as drugs.

If you see: dispensing/compounding/preparation → Pharmacy.

Pharmacognosy p.2

Study of crude drugs, proper horticulture, harvesting, and uses of materials derived from nature.

Crude drugs are plant or animal drugs consisting of natural substances that have undergone only collection and drying.

If you see: senna/natural substance + collection and drying → Pharmacognosy.

Biochemorphology p.2

Structure Activity Relationship (SAR): how the chemical structure of a drug relates to physiological, biological, and biochemical effects.

Lecture examples: norepinephrine affects α1, α2, β1; epinephrine affects α1, α2, β1, β2; isoproterenol affects β1, β2.

Factors: protein-binding, drug solubility, acidity/alkalinity.

Toxicology p.2

Study of the harmful effects of drugs and other chemicals on humans, animals, and plants.

Lecture statement: there is no safe drug; a drug may cause adverse effect or side effect.

If you see: dose more than necessary, poisoning, harmful effect, hepatic necrosis prevention → Toxicology.

Posology p.2

Deals with the dosage of the drug required to produce a therapeutic response.

Lecture example: amoxicillin recommended dose for humans is 30–50 mg/kg.

If you see: mg/kg computation or recommended dose → Posology.

Molecular Pharmacology p.2

Studies the mechanism of action of a drug at the enzymatic level.

Includes gene therapy and genetic engineering, where the genetic makeup of the cell is changed using biotechnology.

Developmental Pharmacology p.2

Deals with drugs given to the mother as they affect the fetus during natal, prenatal, perinatal, and neonatal periods.

Examples from the lecture: isotretinoin, ACE inhibitors, alcohol, thioamides, PTU, and methimazole.

If you see: pregnancy, fetus, cleft lip, prenatal/neonatal → Developmental Pharmacology.

Pharmacogenetics p.2

Studies the influence of heredity on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic response.

Falls under a bigger banner: pharmacogenomics.

If you see: family history, heredity, inherited response → Pharmacogenetics.

Pharmacogenomics p.2–3

Uses genetic information to guide the choice of drug therapy on an individual basis.

Discovers gene variations associated with good or poor therapeutic response and investigates inherited/acquired genetic differences in drug response and behavior.

If you see: gene variation guiding drug choice → Pharmacogenomics.

Pharmacoepidemiology p.3

Studies drug response in a population at a given time and space, whether adverse or beneficial, using epidemiologic measures.

Also described as the utilization and effects of drugs in large numbers of people.

Pharmacoeconomics p.3

Studies the cost-effectiveness of drug treatment and compares costs/consequences of pharmaceutical products and services.

If you see: generic costs ₱10 vs branded costs ₱20 → Pharmacoeconomics.

Ethnopharmacology p.3

Deals with interethnic differences in response to or metabolism of chemicals and enzyme differences in regions or races.

Also studies medicines from naturally occurring substances like fungi and plants used traditionally by specific groups for medicinal purposes.

Clinical Pharmacology, Pharmacokinetics, and Pharmacodynamics

Clinical Pharmacology p.3

Also called Clinical Therapeutics. It deals with rational development, safe/effective use, and proper evaluation of drugs in humans for prevention, diagnosis, treatment, alleviation, or cure.

Refers to rational drug prescribing using ESSC Criteria: Efficacy, Safety, Suitability, Cost.

Pharmacokinetics p.3

Movement of the drug in the body and how the body acts on the drug; the fate of the drug in the body.

Processes: ADME — absorption, distribution, metabolism/biotransformation, excretion.

Pharmacodynamics p.3

Biochemical and physiological effects of drugs and their mechanism of action.

Includes enzymatic/molecular level, toxicological or adverse effects, clinical indications, side effects, adverse effects, and drug-drug interactions.

Drug manufacture: from pre-clinical testing to post-marketing surveillance.

The core sequence is animal testing → FDA safety review → clinical testing → NDA → Phase IV monitoring. The lecture highlights that drug development takes about 12 years on average.

Animal testing / pre-clinical testing p.4

Tested in at least 2 rodent and 1 non-rodent species.

Duration: 1–3 years, average 18 months.

  • Acute toxicity test: observation for 7–14 days using a sample dosage, from single dose up to lethal level.
  • Subacute / prolonged toxicity test: 2 weeks–3 months.
  • Chronic toxicity test: 6 months–2 years.

FDA safety review and clinical testing p.4

FDA: performs a 30-day safety review.

Clinical testing / clinical trials: includes humans, usually healthy adult volunteers early on; duration is 2–10 years, average 5–6 years.

The lowest effective dose is given to humans because humans can react differently to the drug. Short-term and long-term effects are monitored, including chronic toxicity, reproduction, and teratogenicity.

Clinical trial phases: what each phase is trying to prove

Phase I p.4

Normal healthy volunteers.

Measures initial drug safety, biological effects, metabolism, and pharmacokinetics.

Studies acute effects over a wide range of doses.

Primary goal: establish overall safety.

Phase II p.4

Selected patients needing treatment.

Measures therapeutic efficacy, dosage range, metabolism, and pharmacokinetics.

Determines efficacy at doses sick people can tolerate; close monitoring is needed.

Primary goal: establish activity for a specific group of patients with a specific disease.

Phase III p.4

Large sample of selected patients with the disease.

Measures safety and efficacy; large amount of data collected.

Beneficial actions are explored and compared with other drugs.

Primary goal: confirm safety and effectiveness.

Phase IV p.4

Post-marketing surveillance; drug is available commercially.

Detects toxicity not seen in previous phases; looks for adverse reactions, patterns of utilization, and additional indications.

Primary goal: monitor ongoing safety in large populations and uncontrolled use.
⭐ Must know
Phase I = safety in healthy volunteers. Phase II = activity/efficacy in selected patients. Phase III = confirms safety/effectiveness in large selected patients. Phase IV = commercially available/post-marketing surveillance.

NDA and the long road to approval p.4–5

New Drug Application (NDA) requests approval to market the drug for prescription use. FDA reviews all preclinical and clinical testing results.

Lecture summary: it takes 12 years on average for an experimental drug to travel from the lab to the medicine chest, and only 5,000 compounds that are in preclinical testing make it to human testing.

✅ COVID-19 note
The lecture states that vaccine development was expedited, but no phases were skipped; phases overlapped or occurred in parallel while strict clinical and safety standards were maintained. EUA authorizes unregistered drugs/vaccines for public emergencies.

Placebo and drug action: what the treatment does, how strongly, and why effects vary.

This tab connects placebo response, principles of drug action, types of drug action, potency vs efficacy, and the phases that determine final drug effects.

Placebo — “I shall please” p.5

A substance or treatment with no active therapeutic effect.

Response may involve objective physiologic and biochemical changes as well as changes in the subject’s complaints associated with the disease.

✅ Placebo effect / response
A psychological phenomenon where the recipient improves due to personal expectations rather than the treatment itself.
  • Painkilling and anxiety-reducing drugs infused secretly are less effective than when the patient knows they are receiving them.
  • Patients may perceive colored pills as better than white pills.
  • Injections may be perceived as more effective than oral preparations.
  • Larger tablets may be perceived as faster-acting than small tablets.

Principles of drug action p.5

  • Cure diseases: curing is usually not 100% guaranteed; one can treat but not necessarily cure.
  • Alleviate symptoms: make the patient more comfortable.
  • Replenish deficiencies: replace what was lost due to disease.
If you see: “treats but may not cure” → principle of curing/treating disease; “makes comfortable” → alleviate symptoms; “replace what is missing” → replenish deficiencies.
Fundamental drug actions

Stimulation p.5

Enhances or increases functionality of specialized tissues.

Example: epinephrine increases cardiac muscle activity.

Depression p.5

Diminishes functional activity of specialized tissues.

Examples: depressants, sedatives, hypnotics, opioids, analgesics; acetylcholine slows heart rate but contracts smooth muscles; beta blockers decrease cardiac muscle contraction.

Irritation p.5

Either stimulates or diminishes non-specialized tissue functions. Irritants may lead to too much stimulation or depression.

Examples: corrosives and NSAIDs irritate gastric mucosa; erythromycin may cause gastric irritation and is recommended after meals.

⚠️ Exam trap
NSAID-related gastric mucosal irritation is categorized under irritation, not depression, cathartic action, or antacid effect.
Other drug actions and specific effects

Other drug actions p.5

  • Replacement therapy: diarrhea → oral rehydration solution; vitamin, iron, or hormone deficiency.
  • Anti-infective: antimicrobial agents including antibacterial, antifungal, antiviral, cytotoxic agents.
  • Anesthetic: temporary block of nerve conduction; local or general anesthesia.
  • Prophylactic: prevents untoward occurrences/illnesses, e.g. isoniazid for tuberculosis, doxycycline for leptospirosis.
  • Local: topical action on the area itself, e.g. ophthalmic/otic solutions, creams, ointments, mupirocin, hydrocortisone.
  • Systemic: systemic effect even if applied to skin, sprayed, or injected, e.g. nitroglycerin patch, scopolamine patch, hydrocortisone with long-term exposure.

Actions in relation to specific effects p.5–6

  • Cathartic: increases bowel movement; stronger than laxative; evacuates entire colon, usually watery unformed stool.
  • Laxative: for constipated patients; increases passage of stool and eases passage of formed fecal matter. Strength: Laxative < Purgative < Cathartic.
  • Hypnotic: induces sleep, e.g. benzodiazepines, general anesthesia, diphenhydramine.
  • Diuretic: increases urination for edema, congestive heart failure, or hypertension.
  • Antacid: buffering action that neutralizes HCl.
  • Vasodilator: relaxes vascular smooth muscles, e.g. calcium channel blockers such as amlodipine.

Potency vs efficacy p.6

Potency = amount/strength of a drug required to produce the desired effect or action. It can be absolute or relative.

Efficacy = ability of the drug to elicit its maximum inherent physiologic effect or effectiveness.

If you see: “strength required to produce effect” → potency. “maximum effect/effectiveness” → efficacy.

What happens in each drug activity phase?
  • Pharmaceutical phase: disintegration of dosage form, generally water-soluble, and dissolution of active ingredients, lipid-soluble.
  • Pharmacokinetic phase: bioavailability, defined as the fraction of unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation after administration by any route.
  • Pharmacodynamic phase: drug-drug interaction, individual sensitivity, and pathological conditions.
⭐ Must know
Tablet disintegration belongs to the pharmaceutical phase, not pharmacodynamics.

Prescription writing: the written order + instructions to pharmacist + directions to patient.

The lecture defines prescription as a physician’s written order for medicinal agents with instructions for preparation and directions for use. The exam-favorite distinction is superscription vs inscription vs subscription vs signatura.

Prescription definition p.6

A written order, especially by a physician, for the preparation and administration of a medicine, therapeutic regimen, assistive/corrective device, or other treatment.

Also described as a physician’s written order for one or more medicinal agents, together with instructions to the pharmacist for preparation and directions to the patient for use.

Required identity and patient details p.6

  • Identity of prescriber: name, license classification, address, office telephone number.
  • Date.
  • Name and address of patient.
  • Age is written if the patient is a child or senior citizen.

Rx core parts p.6

  • Superscription: R/ or Rx, from recipe, meaning “take thou of.”
  • Inscription / Transcription: body of the prescription order.
  • Subscription: directions to the pharmacist.
  • Signatura: from signa, meaning “mark” or “label”; directions to the patient; preceded by Label.
Inscription order for multiple ingredients

Basis

Principal drug giving the prescription its chief action.

Adjuvant

Aids or increases the action of the basis.

Corrective

Modifies or corrects undesirable effects of the basis or adjuvant.

Vehicle

Solvent of the solution used to increase bulk or dilute the mixture.

If you see: “solvent to increase bulk” → Vehicle. “body of the prescription order” → Inscription / Transcription.
⭐ Must know
The prescriber’s signature is followed by initials M.D., PRC license number, PTR number, and Opium License/S2 number if the prescribed drug is regulated or prohibited. Other parts may include refill information, childproof-container waiver, and additional warning labels such as “may cause drowsiness” or “do not drink alcohol.” p.6
⚠️ Exam trap
The review item states the true Rx writing statement as: the label should include dose, frequency, and duration of treatment. Do not choose “brand name should always be written,” “PRC may or may not be written,” or “Rx means mark.” p.7–8

Definitions you must not mix up

  • Pharmacology = science/study of drugs.
  • Drug = chemical substance of known structure that produces a biological effect.
  • Medicine = chemical preparation containing one or more drugs for therapeutic effect.
  • Prototype = drug that typifies a class.
  • Generic name = worldwide nonproprietary name; must appear on prescriptions.
  • Proprietary name = trade/brand name; may or may not be written below generic name.

Branch cue map

  • Crude natural drug, collection/drying → Pharmacognosy.
  • Dosage computation / mg/kg → Posology.
  • Harmful effects / overdose → Toxicology.
  • ADME / body handles drug → Pharmacokinetics.
  • Mechanism / receptor / physiologic effect → Pharmacodynamics.
  • Heredity affects response → Pharmacogenetics.
  • Gene-guided therapy choice → Pharmacogenomics.
  • Cost comparison → Pharmacoeconomics.
  • ESSC rational prescribing → Clinical Pharmacology.
  • Pregnancy/fetal/neonatal effects → Developmental Pharmacology.

Clinical trials in one glance

  • Pre-clinical: 2 rodent + 1 non-rodent; acute, subacute, chronic toxicity.
  • FDA: 30-day safety review.
  • Phase I: healthy volunteers; overall safety.
  • Phase II: selected patients; activity/efficacy and dosage range.
  • Phase III: large selected patients; confirm safety/effectiveness.
  • NDA: approval to market for prescription use.
  • Phase IV: post-marketing; commercially available; detects toxicity not previously seen.
  • Total: about 12 years from lab to medicine chest.

Drug action quick hits

  • Stimulation: increases specialized tissue function; epinephrine increases cardiac muscle activity.
  • Depression: diminishes specialized tissue function; sedatives, hypnotics, opioids, beta blockers.
  • Irritation: non-specialized tissue stimulation/depression; NSAIDs irritate gastric mucosa.
  • Replacement: replace what is deficient.
  • Prophylactic: prevent illness/untoward event.
  • Local: action only at applied area.
  • Systemic: systemic effect even if applied, sprayed, or injected.
  • Laxative < Purgative < Cathartic.

Potency, efficacy, and activity phases

  • Potency = amount/strength required to produce effect.
  • Efficacy = maximum inherent physiologic effect/effectiveness.
  • Pharmaceutical phase = disintegration and dissolution.
  • Pharmacokinetic phase = bioavailability.
  • Pharmacodynamic phase = interactions, individual sensitivity, pathologic conditions.
  • Effects = toxic, therapeutic, or subtherapeutic.

Prescription must-know

  • Superscription = R/ or Rx, “recipe,” “take thou of.”
  • Inscription / Transcription = body of prescription order.
  • Subscription = directions to pharmacist.
  • Signatura = directions to patient; preceded by “Label.”
  • Basis = principal drug.
  • Adjuvant = aids/increases basis action.
  • Corrective = modifies undesirable effects.
  • Vehicle = solvent to increase bulk/dilute mixture.
  • True Rx writing statement: label includes dose, frequency, duration.
