Big picture

Medical history and physical examination are synergistic, not separate. History gives the subjective information from the patient, while physical examination gives the objective findings through evaluation by touch, listening, and proper positioning. Together, they serve as the guidepost for clinical decision-making. Pages 1, 11

Learning objectives

  • Conduct a medical history interview using the correct approach and technique.
  • Perform a complete symptom analysis.
  • Compose the medical history in the prescribed format.
  • Construct a schematic clinical temporal profile.

High-yield summary

⭐ Must know
The lecture repeatedly emphasizes that sequence and timing change the diagnostic meaning. Fever → abdominal pain → jaundice suggests a different thought process from abdominal pain → jaundice → fever, even when the components are similar. Pages 4–5
✅ Key point
The temporal profile is a graphic representation of the HPI. It shows how symptoms overlap in time and gives a “forest” view of the illness course plus the “individual trees” or components. Pages 10–11

The Interview

The interview is not just question-asking. It requires rapport, mindfulness, topic tracking, and empathic cue recognition so the physician can obtain useful medical data without losing the patient’s trust. Pages 1–3

Approach before the interview

  • Review the medical board and set goals for the interview.
  • Use self-reflection: “Am I too near?”, “Am I using the proper non-verbal cues?”, “Is my voice too loud?”
  • Review clinical behavior and appearance. Professionalism is described as unconditional positive regard.
  • Adjust the environment and be mindful when taking notes.
⚠️ Pitfall
Patients may get distracted if they see you taking notes while interviewing them. Balance documentation with patient presence. Page 1

Technique: science + art

History taking is like both science and art: science because it must be learned continuously and appropriately; art because it relies on nonverbal cues and experience.

  • Conduct sensitive questions in privacy.
  • Do not limit note-taking when follow-up questions are needed.
  • Remove personal biases; patients judge professionalism.
✅ Key point
For sensitive questions, it is acceptable to ask whether the patient would like to transfer to a more private area. Page 1

Sequence of the interview

1 Greet + rapport→2 Invite story→3 Agenda→4 Clarify + test hypotheses→5 Shared understanding→6 Negotiate plan→7 Follow-up + close

Before asking medical questions, make it personal: “Kamusta po kayo?”, ask who is with them, and observe what is beside the patient. Establishing rapport sets the expectation for how the patient provides information.

⭐ Must know
SPIKES is used for disclosing bad news: Setting, Perception, Invitation, Knowledge, Empathetic responses, Summary and strategy. Page 2

Skills with ongoing influence

Rapport building & relationship maintenance
Warm greeting, eye contact with patient and bedside person, brief non-medical interaction, checking on important life events. Pitfall: trust may not develop if the patient does not feel connection. Avoid by small talk and brief check-in.
Mindful practice
Observe the patient’s and physician’s own thought processes. Avoid cognitive shortcuts and physician dominance of the agenda. Stay present and critically curious.
Topic tracking
Maintain focus on a mutually agreed topic using summarizing, process transparency, and goal alignment. This prevents disorganization and rushing through issues.
Acknowledging social/emotional cues
Empathic acknowledgement can focus discussion or invite further exploration. Example: “You appear worried, do you have other concerns?”

Relationship → communication → efficiency

The lecture figure links ongoing skills to interview quality and efficiency: rapport, mindfulness, topic tracking, and empathic cue responses help establish focus, elicit the patient’s perspective, and cocreate a plan.

Exploring the patient’s perspective

  • Feelings, fears, or concerns about the problem.
  • Ideas about nature and cause of the problem.
  • Effect of the problem on life and function.
  • Expectations of disease, clinician, or healthcare based on prior experiences.
✅ Key point
Use symptom analysis to process information and think about the most likely organ system involved. Page 3

Type of questions

  • Neutral/open-ended: “Tell me more about…”
  • Simple direct: can speed up the interview but may overwhelm the patient.
  • Leading: establishes bias, e.g., “Isn’t it true that…”
  • Loaded: usually avoided, but may be used to observe reaction.
⚠️ Trap
Do not start with leading or overly direct questions when the patient’s story is still forming. Start broad, then focus. Page 3

Symptom Analysis

Symptom analysis is used to formulate a diagnostic hypothesis. The differential diagnosis process is continuous, so every reassessment asks: “Is this the only problem?” Pages 4–5, 8–9

Interview approach: broad → focused

  1. Move from open-ended to focused questions.
  2. Use questions that elicit a graded response.
  3. Ask a series of questions one at a time.
  4. Offer multiple choices when needed.
  5. Clarify what the patient means.
  6. Use echoing.
⭐ Must know
The funnel starts with open-ended questions to hear the story, then specific questions to elicit the seven symptom features, then yes/no or pertinent positives/negatives from ROS.

Questioning skills

Non-focusing

Silence, non-verbal encouragement, and neutral utterances encourage the patient to talk without directing them too much.

Focusing

Echoing, open-ended requests, and summarizing/paraphrasing direct focus toward a symptom or topic already mentioned.

✅ Key point
Summarizing/paraphrasing checks the accuracy of understanding and can refocus the interview. Page 4

PQRSTU for characterizing symptoms

  • P — Provocative/Palliative: What makes pain worse or better?
  • Q — Quality/Quantity: What does it feel like? How bad is it?
  • R — Region/Radiation: Where is it? Does it move elsewhere?
  • T — Treatment: Any meds, home treatment, consults, or changes since last consult?
  • U — Understanding: What does the patient think is causing it?
✅ Key point
Ask severity in relation to function: “Was it so severe that you couldn’t stand up?” or “Did it make you miss work?” Page 4

Onset, manner, and timing

  • Use day of admission/consultation as reference date.
  • Ask for temporal order and relationships between events.
  • Manner gives a clue to tempo: gradual or sudden.
  • Ask precipitating/predisposing factors: what made it worse, what was the patient doing, where were they, what made it better?
⭐ If you see X → think Y
Fever → abdominal pain → jaundice was contrasted with abdominal pain → jaundice → fever. Same symptoms, different timing, different diagnostic direction. Pages 4–5

HPI phases and symptom analysis checklist

Open-ended neutral story→Phase 2
Detailed symptom analysis→Phase 3

⚠️ Trap
Phase 1 should not begin with direct closed questions. It should let the patient speak freely through open-ended neutral questions. Pages 8, 12
Six-point HPI checklist
  1. Are all components present?
  2. Correct sequence?
  3. Correct temporal relationships between problems and signs/symptoms?
  4. Analysis of symptoms of all components?
  5. Any time holes?
  6. Thorough review of systems?

The lecture states this can have a predictive diagnostic value of 85% or higher if done thoroughly and accurately.

Skilled Interviewing + Special Situations

The physician controls the interaction while staying neutral, nonjudgmental, and responsive to patient cues. Pages 5–7

Skilled interviewing techniques

Active listening
Listen with intent, observe nonverbal cues, connect to the patient’s emotional state, and encourage the patient to expand feelings.
Adaptive questioning
Use directed questioning from general to specific, graded response questions, one question at a time, multiple choices, and clarification.
Non-verbal communication
Hand gestures help encourage fuller answers and show active listening.
Empathetic responses
Put yourself in the patient’s shoes, but do not assume the meaning of their feelings. Ask: “How did you feel about that?”
Validation
Validate the patient’s response and feelings as legitimate and understandable. Example: “That accident must have been scary.”

Reassurance, facilitation, summarization

⚠️ Reassurance trap
Do not tell a stage IV cancer patient “Gagaling ka, huwag kang mag-alala.” First identify and acknowledge feelings; false hope is a risk. Page 5
  • Facilitation: guide the patient to the next expected questions; use “Mm-hmm,” “Go on,” “I’m listening.”
  • Summarization: shows you listened, organizes reasoning, and lets the patient correct misunderstandings.
  • Echoing: repeating the patient’s words encourages elaboration.
  • Highlighting transitions: tells the patient when you shift topics.

Sensitive topics

⭐ Single most important rule
Never be judgmental. Page 6

Sexual history

Establish relationship first, explain that questions are private/personal, ask if relatives may hear, move to a private area if needed, use specific understandable language.

Mental health

Be sensitive to reports of mood changes or symptoms.

Alcohol/drug abuse

Ask routinely about current/past use and family history. Remember CAGE: Cutting down, Annoyance, Guilty feelings, Eye-openers.

Family violence

Stay discreet; if detected, tell senior/consultant and do not directly confront the family. Spend part of the encounter alone if abuse is suspected.

Death and dying

Give opportunities to talk and listen receptively; if they remain social only, respect preferences. Palliative care handles this sensitive topic.

Organization of the Medical History

The prescribed sequence is: Patient Profile → Source & Reliability → Chief Complaint → HPI → Past Health Maintenance → Family History → ROS → Temporal Profile Diagram. Pages 7–10

Patient profile

The profile reveals the individual as a whole: personality, mental make-up, reaction to environment/illness, identifying data, family members, environmental history, daily routine, diet, medications, and hobbies/habits.

✅ Why it matters
It helps determine stimuli in the environment, factors influencing diagnosis/therapy, and clues to the illness cause, especially when financial capability limits testing. Page 7

Source & reliability

Source may be the patient or relatives. Reliability can be scored Poor, Fair, or Good; a percentage can also be used.

⚠️ Trap
It is false that only the patient can be a reliable source. Relatives/family may be sources, especially when patient reliability is reduced. Pages 8, 12

Chief complaint

  • Primary reason for consultation.
  • Brief statement, restricted to a symptom or two.
  • Use patient’s own words as nearly as possible.
  • Includes duration.
  • Avoid diagnostic terms/diseases.

Example: “masakit po ang tuhod ko, tatlong linggo na” → “Knee pain, 3 weeks.”

Habits

Smoking: calculate pack years.

✅ Formula
Pack years = packs smoked/day × years of smoking
Example: 3 sticks/day for 5 years → 3/20 × 5 = 0.75 pack years. Page 8

Alcohol: ask beer/wine/spirits, amount, and last drink.

History of Present Illness (HPI)

1. Restate CC with elaboration

Use profile context: e.g., older adult taking medication or security guard working nights with dizziness.

2. History from onset

Chronologic story from first appearance; may use medical terms here.

3. Current status

What happened afterwards? Did the patient consult or take meds?

4. Significant positives/negatives

Summarize all significant positive and negative findings, including symptoms revealed in ROS.

⭐ Must know
Use a separate paragraph for each chronological period. In each paragraph, analyze symptoms completely and note positives/negatives related to those symptoms. Pages 8–9, 12

Past health maintenance

  • General state of health, past illnesses/injuries/accidents/childhood diseases.
  • Hospitalizations: medical, surgical, obstetric, psychiatric.
  • Immunizations and reactions.
  • Current medications; adherence/compliance.
  • Allergies: sensitivities and severity.
  • OB-Gyne: pregnancies, deliveries, menstrual history, birth control, sexual function.

GPFPAL Gravida, Parity, Full-term, Preterm, Aborted, Living.

Family history

  • Present status of parents and siblings: age, health status, cause and age at death if applicable.
  • Similar illness or symptom in the family.
  • Common hereditary tendency diseases: diabetes mellitus, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, allergies, cerebrovascular disease, mental illness, malignancies.

ROS, Temporal Profile, and Medical Data

The ROS catches symptoms not elicited earlier; the temporal profile converts the HPI into a visual course of illness. Pages 10–11

Review of Systems (ROS)

ROS is a head-to-toe survey to uncover symptoms not elicited earlier. It is a systematic review by organ system and may detect symptoms related or unrelated to the HPI.

⚠️ Trap
Symptoms found during ROS should not automatically be omitted if they seem unrelated. They may be analyzed and included in HPI Phase 4 because they may be relevant or suggest another condition. Pages 8, 10, 12

Temporal profile diagram

  • Follows after ROS and before PE record.
  • Represents illness components across the course of present illness.
  • Shows how symptoms overlap.
  • Severity/intensity is shown by an upward stroke.

Temporal profile guidelines

  1. Use 1–2 symptom lines, maximum 3.
  2. If symptoms have similar profiles, use one line to represent all.
  3. Lines may be color-coded or represented by symbols.
  4. Separate diagrams for more than one problem/illness.
  5. Height, shape, slope, and sharpness depict severity, acuteness, and tempo.

7-day timeline with 3 components

The example uses RUQ pain → jaundice → fever. Any curve touching the baseline/clinical horizon is interpreted as absent. Symptom lines are curves, not sharp angles.

Example: pressure over left chest “like an elephant is sitting there,” going to left neck and arm.Example: height, weight, BMI, BP, HR, RR, temperature and examination description.

Comprehensive assessment

Appropriate for new patients in office or hospital. Provides fundamental personalized knowledge, strengthens clinician-patient relationship, helps identify/rule out physical causes, provides baseline, creates health promotion platform, and develops PE skills.

Focused assessment

Appropriate for established patients, especially routine or urgent care visits. Addresses focused concerns, assesses symptoms restricted to a specific body system, and applies relevant examination methods thoroughly.

Common traps

  • “Only patient is reliable source” → False. Relatives can be sources.
  • “Allergies/immunizations under patient profile” → False. Put under Past Health Maintenance.
  • “Omit unrelated ROS symptoms from HPI” → False. Analyze/include if relevant or suggest another condition.
  • “Phase 1 uses direct closed questions” → False. Phase 1 uses open-ended neutral questions.

If you see X → think Y

  • Time gaps in HPI → ask what happened between periods.
  • Talkative patient → courteously interrupt and redirect.
  • Angry patient → do not engage; step back and ask if it is a bad time.
  • Sensitive topic → ensure privacy, orient patient, never judge.
  • Symptoms same but sequence different → diagnostic direction may change.

Special situations quick cues

  • Silent: ask if you upset them; observe nonverbal cues.
  • Confusing: relative help; redirect to disease/context.
  • Talkative: polite interruption.
  • Angry: do not engage; step back.
  • Low literacy: avoid fancy terms; assess reading.

Final exam brain-dump

History + PE are synergistic. History is subjective; PE is objective. Start interview with rapport, move from open-ended to focused, characterize symptoms with PQRSTU/CLITAA, build HPI chronologically, check for time holes, use ROS to reveal missed symptoms, and draw a temporal profile to show overlap, sequence, severity, acuteness, and tempo.
