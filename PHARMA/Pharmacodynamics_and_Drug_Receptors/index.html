Pharmacodynamics and Drug Receptors

Mechanisms, receptor signaling, dose-response relationships, safety, variability, and drug interactions

Big picture

Overview

Pharmacodynamics explains what a drug does to the body: its molecular action, observable effects, clinical uses, adverse effects, receptor interactions, and dose-response behavior.

Pages 1–2

Pharmacodynamics

The study of the biochemical, biologic, and physiological effects of drugs.

  • Biochemical: mechanism of action at the molecular or enzymatic level.
  • Physiological: observable changes produced in the body.
✅ Includes: mechanism of action, clinical indications, adverse or side effects, and drug-drug interactions.

Why it matters

  1. Rational therapeutic use of drugs.
  2. Design of improved chemical agents.

The lecture connects this to structure-activity relationship (SAR), where chemical structure is related to biochemical and physiological effects.

How drugs produce effects

Mechanisms of Drug Action

Drug action may depend on a receptor target or on a physical, chemical, or metabolic property of the drug itself.

Pages 2–4

Main receptor target classes

  • Regulatory proteins — mediate endogenous neurotransmitter, autacoid, and hormone signals.
  • Enzymes — may be activated or inhibited by a drug.
  • Transport proteins — include reuptake and membrane transport systems.
  • Structural proteins — e.g., tubulin for colchicine.

Lecture target examples

  • Dihydrofolate reductase — methotrexate
  • Acetylcholinesterase
  • ACE, COX, carbonic anhydrase
  • DNA/RNA polymerases, MAO, neuraminidase
  • Na+-K+ ATPase — digitalis
  • NE and serotonin transporters — antidepressants

Drug-receptor coupling

Coupling is the transduction process linking receptor occupancy by an agonist to the pharmacologic response.

Drug binds receptor→Receptor changes shape→Signal transduction→Cellular response
Ion channel

Spare receptors

Some tissues possess more receptors than are necessary to generate a maximal response.

  • Present when Kd > EC50.
  • Kd is the concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied.
  • Maximum response may occur with only a small fraction occupied.
  • Lecture example: insulin, where about 1% receptor occupancy may produce an effect.
⚠️ Exam trap: Maximum effect does not always require occupation of all receptors.
Nonreceptor-mediated physical actions Pages 3–4

Adsorption

Adhesion of gas or liquid molecules to a surface. Examples: activated charcoal and simethicone.

Mass or bulk

Bulk laxatives such as psyllium, bran, and fibrosine stimulate defecation through their mass.

Osmotic property

Mannitol is listed as an osmotic diuretic used for cerebral edema.

Colligative effect

Depends on the number of molecules and ions in a system. Examples include magnesium sulfate, menthol, and tannic acid.

Nonreceptor-mediated chemical and metabolic actions Page 4

Chemical interactions

  • Neutralization: antacids neutralize gastric HCl.
  • Chelation: complexes metals for excretion; examples include penicillamine and dimercaprol.
  • Radioactivity: radioactive iodine for thyrotoxicosis.
  • Radio-opacity: barium sulfate and iodinated contrast agents.

Altered metabolic processes

A structural analog may be incorporated into a cellular system and alter normal function.

  • Sulfonamide as a structural analog example.
  • Antineoplastic drugs as antimetabolites.

Ligand-gated ion channels

Drug binding directly regulates an ion channel. Responses are extremely fast and diminish within milliseconds.

  • ACh at nicotinic receptors → Na+ influx → action potential → skeletal muscle contraction.
  • Benzodiazepines enhance GABA receptor stimulation → Cl- influx → hyperpolarization.

G protein-coupled receptors

The largest receptor class. A single peptide crosses the membrane seven times, earning the terms seven-transmembrane or serpentine receptor.

G proteins interact with intracellular portions and alter second-messenger concentrations.

Gq
M1, M3, α1 → phospholipase C activation.
Gi
M2, α2, D2 → adenylyl cyclase inhibition.
Gs
β1, β2, D1 → adenylyl cyclase activation.
Second messengers
Conduct and amplify receptor signals.

Enzyme-linked receptors

Membrane receptors with intrinsic or associated tyrosine kinase activity.

  • Insulin and epidermal growth factor receptors.
  • Activated receptors may undergo autophosphorylation.
  • Cytokine receptors can recruit JAK, which phosphorylates STAT.

JAK-STAT sequence

Ligand binds→Receptor dimerizes→JAK activates→STAT phosphorylates→STAT dimer enters nucleus

Intracellular receptors

  • Ligand must be very lipid-soluble and diffuse through the membrane.
  • Examples: steroids, vitamin D, thyroid hormone.
  • Activated ligand-receptor complex migrates to the nucleus and regulates gene expression.
  • Onset is delayed; duration extends for hours to days.
⭐ If you see steroid hormone: Think intracellular/DNA-coupled receptor and altered gene transcription.

Binding and response

Drug Properties and Receptor Behavior

Affinity describes binding; intrinsic activity describes receptor activation after binding; efficacy describes the maximal response; potency describes how much drug is needed.

Pages 4–6

Affinity

Ability or tenacity of a drug to bind a receptor.

Efficacy

Ability to elicit a response and the maximum response or ceiling effect.

Intrinsic activity

Ability to activate a receptor once bound.

Potency

Amount or dose required to produce a specified response, represented by EC50/ED50.

⚠️ Common confusion: Potency is not the same as efficacy. A lower dose can be more potent while both drugs still achieve the same maximum effect.

Lecture case: 5 mg versus 50 mg

Two antihypertensive drugs both reduce blood pressure. Drug A works at 5 mg; Drug B at 50 mg.

  • Same efficacy: both can achieve the desired blood-pressure reduction.
  • Drug A is more potent: a lower dose produces the effect.

Competitive antagonist

  • Binds the same site as agonist.
  • Does not activate receptor.
  • Also called surmountable antagonism.
  • Shifts dose-response curve to the right and raises ED50.
  • Examples: ACh vs atropine; morphine vs naloxone.

Noncompetitive antagonist

  • Binds a site other than the agonist-binding site.
  • Often irreversible and insurmountable.
  • Lecture example: phenoxybenzamine.
⭐ If you see rightward curve shift with same achievable effect: Think competitive antagonism and increased ED50.
Quantifying drug action

Dose-Response Curves

Dose-response curves show how response changes with dose and provide information about efficacy, potency, slope, safety, and biologic variability.

Pages 4–7, 15

What a dose-response curve tells you

Potency
Horizontal position and ED50.
Efficacy
Maximum height or plateau.
Slope
How much response changes with dose.
Relative safety
Steeper slope means narrower margin of safety in the lecture.

Graded dose-response curve

  • Shows a quantitatively gradable effect in an individual or selected population.
  • Response magnitude increases continuously with dose until the system’s maximum.
  • Used to determine efficacy, potency, slope, and biologic variability.
  • Graded ED50 = dose producing half-maximal response.

Quantal dose-response curve

  • Shows the percentage of a population displaying a stated response.
  • Often an all-or-none outcome.
  • Quantal ED50 = dose producing the response in 50% of the population.
  • Used for safety, selectivity, and population variability.

Four parameters of the graded curve Pages 5–6

Efficacy

Maximum desirable response; reflected by the plateau.

Potency

Relative amount of drug required for a specified response. Lower ED50 indicates greater potency.

Slope

Change in response relative to dose. A steep slope means a small dose change produces a large response change.

Biologic variability

Normal differences in required dose among individuals due to factors such as age, sex, diet, and concurrent drugs.

Margin of safety

Drug Safety and Pharmacogenetics

Safety is estimated using median and extreme-dose measures, while pharmacogenetics explains inherited variation in pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic responses.

Pages 7–8

Therapeutic index

Humans: TI = TD50 / ED50
Animals: TI = LD50 / ED50

Higher TI indicates a safer drug, but TI provides median values and does not show extreme overlap.

Certain safety factor

CSF = LD1 / ED99

CSF > 1 indicates safety; CSF < 1 indicates an unsafe overlap.

Standard safety margin

SSM = ((LD1 - ED99) / ED99) × 100

Percentage by which dose can be increased before 1% of subjects die from overdose; used more in industry.

⭐ Therapeutic window: Serum concentration required to produce therapeutic effects without toxicity. A wider range is safer.

Pharmacogenetics

The study of hereditary influence on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic responses, including qualitative and quantitative genetic variation in drug response.

  • Pharmacokinetic variation: absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion.
  • Pharmacodynamic variation: receptors, mechanism, interactions, adverse effects.
  • Idiosyncratic reaction: unexpected qualitative response related to genetic factors.

Important inherited examples

  • Succinylcholine apnea from pseudocholinesterase deficiency.
  • G6PD deficiency with oxidant-induced hemolysis.
  • Malignant hyperthermia linked to inherited ryanodine receptor abnormality.
  • Slow acetylation of INH → peripheral neuropathy.
  • Rapid acetylation of INH → hepatotoxicity.

Consequences of genetic variation

  1. Altered renal or metabolic clearance.
  2. Functional overdose.
  3. Failure to convert a prodrug to active drug or metabolite.
  4. Altered pharmacodynamics.
  5. Idiosyncratic or unusual reactions.

Response modifiers

Drug Interactions and Posology

Posology is the study of dose and effect. Drug response varies with physiology, disease, environment, genetics, repeated exposure, and interacting drugs.

Pages 8–10

Pharmaceutical interaction

Occurs during compounding, combination, or dilution and may cause loss of object-drug activity.

Pharmacokinetic interaction

Changes absorption, distribution, or elimination of the object drug.

Pharmacodynamic interaction

Changes drug effect at the site of action, such as additive actions of similar drugs.

Factors influencing dose and response Pages 8–9
Physiologic
Age, weight/surface area, sex, pregnancy/lactation, nutrition, emotion.
Pathologic
Kidney and liver disease.
Environmental
Time, place, climate, route, race, preparation, drug age, medium.
Genetic
Idiosyncrasy, polymorphism, acetylator status, enzyme deficiency.

Tachyphylaxis vs tolerance

Tachyphylaxis: rapid decrease in response after repeated doses over a short period; examples include ephedrine, salbutamol, and nasal decongestants.

Tolerance: gradual need for higher doses during long-term use; lecture example: opioids.

Receptor regulation

Downregulation: sustained agonist exposure decreases receptor number.

Upregulation: prolonged receptor blockade increases receptor availability.

Other forms of antagonism Page 10

Physical antagonism

Physical removal or adsorption, such as charcoal in poisoning.

Chemical antagonism

Complex formation or chemical reaction, such as chelation, deferoxamine for iron, and protamine binding heparin.

Functional antagonism

Two agonists at different receptors produce opposite physiological effects, such as epinephrine versus histamine.

Pharmacologic antagonism

Competitive/reversible or noncompetitive antagonism at receptors.

✅ Clinical significance of antagonism: Correct adverse effects, treat poisoning, and avoid combinations that reduce efficacy.

Cram Sheet

Condensed definitions, receptor patterns, curve rules, formulas, and “if you see X → think Y” cues.

Pages 1–15

Four core properties

  • Affinity: bind receptor.
  • Intrinsic activity: activate receptor once bound.
  • Efficacy: maximum response.
  • Potency: dose required; lower ED50 = greater potency.

Agonist values

  • Full agonist: affinity 1, IA 1.
  • Partial agonist: affinity 1, IA between 0 and 1.
  • Antagonist: affinity 1, IA 0.
  • Inverse agonist: affinity 1, IA -1.

Receptor speed map

  • Ion channel: milliseconds.
  • GPCR: seven-transmembrane, second messengers.
  • Enzyme-linked: tyrosine kinase/JAK-STAT.
  • Intracellular: lipid-soluble ligand, transcription, hours to days.

GPCR map

  • Gq: M1, M3, α1.
  • Gi: M2, α2, D2.
  • Gs: β1, β2, D1.

Dose-response rules

  • Graded: magnitude in one subject.
  • Quantal: proportion of population.
  • Left shift: more potent.
  • Higher plateau: greater efficacy.
  • Competitive antagonist: right shift, ↑ ED50.

Common traps

  • Do not call a more potent drug more efficacious unless its maximum response is also higher.
  • Partial agonists may have full affinity; their reduced effect comes from lower intrinsic activity.
  • Antagonists can bind strongly even though intrinsic activity is zero.
  • Graded ED50 is half-maximal response; quantal ED50 is response in half the population.
  • Intracellular receptors are slow because they alter gene expression.
  • Competitive antagonism is surmountable and shifts the curve right.

The source contains conflicting shorthand for antagonism across lecture versions: 1 + 1 = 0 and 1 + 0 = 0. The main teaching point is that antagonism reduces or abolishes the effect.
