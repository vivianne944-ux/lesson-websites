Whole-Lesson Overview

A roadmap of the complete adult physical examination—from preparation to system examination.

Pages 1–15
Framework 01

Prepare the examiner, patient, and setting

The lecture begins with professional demeanor, informed consent, patient comfort, lighting, equipment, privacy, hygiene, and infection-control precautions.

Framework 02

Choose an orderly sequence

A comprehensive examination should move broad to specific, usually head to toe or anterior to posterior, while minimizing unnecessary position changes.

Framework 03

Observe before and throughout

The general survey starts the moment the clinician encounters the patient and continues while history-taking and examination refine the clinical impression.

⭐ WHOLE-LESSON MUST KNOW
IPaPeA—inspection, palpation, percussion, auscultation—is the usual sequence. The abdomen differs: IAPePa—inspection, auscultation, percussion, palpation—because percussion and palpation may change auscultatory findings.

Rapid anchors

  • Standard precautions: apply to all patients in any setting.
  • Transmission-based precautions: added when certain infectious agents require extra measures.
  • Right-sided examination: the standard position described in the lecture.
  • Cardinal vital signs: blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, temperature.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: fall of at least 20 mmHg systolic or 10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing.

Major measurement anchors

  • BMI: weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared.
  • Waist risk thresholds: ≥35 in (~89 cm) in women; ≥40 in (~102 cm) in men.
  • Blood pressure: palpatory estimate plus 30 mmHg before auscultatory measurement.
  • Motor strength: graded 0–5.
  • Orientation: time, place, person.
✅ OVERVIEW PRINCIPLE
The clinician should be thorough without wasting time, systematic but flexible and gentle, and attentive to the patient’s verbal and nonverbal cues throughout the encounter.

Prepare Before You Touch the Patient

p. 1

Six preparation tasks

  1. Reflect on your approach so you are calm and focused.
  2. Adjust lighting and environment for visibility and comfort.
  3. Check equipment for completeness.
  4. Make the patient comfortable in an appropriate position.
  5. Observe standard and universal precautions and check isolation requirements.
  6. Choose the examination sequence so the examination is systematic and orderly.

Professional demeanor

  • Be straightforward: explain each step and obtain informed consent.
  • Be calm, organized, and competent to reassure the patient.
  • Warn the patient before a maneuver that may cause discomfort.
  • Maximize time by examining systematically.
  • Be selective while maintaining a comprehensive approach.
🧠 PATIENT-CENTERED CUE
Patients may feel anxious, vulnerable, physically exposed, or apprehensive about findings. A skillful clinician notices a wince or worried glance, shares information, and reassures while continuing the examination.
Lighting and environment p. 2

Good lighting helps reveal subtle physical signs. A quiet environment supports auscultation and clear communication.

Tangential lighting

Direct light at a low grazing angle to reveal surface undulations and pulsations. The lecture highlights its use for the JVP, thyroid gland, and apical impulse.

Perpendicular lighting

Light shines directly down, reducing shadows and flattening the appearance. It can show clear surface findings but makes depth and texture harder to perceive.

⭐ MUST KNOW
If you need to evaluate the apical impulse, JVP, or other superficial pulsations → use tangential lighting.
Core examination equipment pp. 2–3
  • Ophthalmoscope
  • Otoscope; pneumatic otoscopy may be used in children
  • White penlight for accurate tissue color and fine detail
  • Thermometer
  • Analog watch with a second hand
  • Ruler for JVP
  • Flexible tape measure in centimeters
  • Tongue depressor
  • Stethoscope: well-fitting ear tips; thick-walled tubing as short as feasible; bell and diaphragm
  • Sphygmomanometer: standard cuff 12 × 23 cm, up to 28 cm
  • Vaginal specula, gloves, lubricant
  • Reflex hammer
  • Two test tubes for hot and cold sensation, optional
  • Tuning forks: 128 Hz for vibration; 512 Hz for auditory examination
  • Safety pin, cotton, visual acuity card, writing device
⚠️ PITFALL
Do not use a yellow penlight when accurate assessment of tissue color is important; the lecture favors white light for diagnostic visibility.
Privacy, draping, and comfort p. 3
  • Close doors and draw curtains.
  • Use a gown or draw sheet so only the area being examined is exposed.
  • Describe the plan and keep the patient informed.
  • Give courteous, clear instructions.
  • Adjust for the patient’s feelings and physical comfort.
  • Share general impressions and explain what comes next.
  • Wash hands, clean equipment, and dispose of waste materials properly.
✅ KEY POINT
Privacy, clear explanation, and thoughtful draping are part of examination quality—not optional extras.
⭐ CORE DISTINCTION
Standard precautions apply to all patients. Transmission-based precautions are a second tier used in addition to standard precautions when extra measures are needed.

Standard Precautions

pp. 3–4

Underlying principle

Blood, body fluids, secretions, excretions except sweat, non-intact skin, and mucous membranes may contain transmissible infectious agents. These precautions apply regardless of diagnosis or suspected infection status.

Core practices

  • Hand hygiene
  • Appropriate PPE
  • Respiratory hygiene and cough etiquette
  • Patient placement
  • Equipment and environmental cleaning
  • Safe handling of laundry
  • Safe injection practices
  • Proper waste disposal

Transmission-Based Precautions

p. 4

Contact precautions

  • Appropriate patient placement and PPE
  • Limit transport to medically necessary purposes
  • Use disposable or dedicated equipment, such as a blood pressure cuff
  • Prioritize frequent cleaning and disinfection
  • The lecture emphasizes gloves, goggles, and face masks for blood-borne pathogen exposure

Airborne precautions

  • Put a mask on the patient
  • Place in an AIIR
  • Restrict susceptible personnel
  • Use PPE appropriately
  • Limit transport
  • Immunize susceptible persons after unprotected contact when applicable
  • Lecture cue: keep six feet away and use mask, face shield, gown, and gloves

Droplet precautions

  • Put a mask on the patient
  • Use a single room if possible
  • Don a mask upon entry
  • Limit transport
  • Lecture cue: keep six feet away and use mask, face shield, gown, and gloves

Reverse isolation

  • Protects a patient with a weakened immune system
  • Use dedicated equipment
  • Perform hand hygiene before and after contact
  • Barrier precautions are not automatically required without suspected or confirmed infection unless standard precautions or source control indicate them
  • Avoid dust-dispersing cleaning, carpeting, upholstered furnishings, flowers, and potted plants

Universal Precautions

p. 4

Designed to prevent transmission of HIV, hepatitis B, and other blood-borne pathogens during first aid or healthcare. Potentially infectious materials include blood and body fluids containing visible blood, semen, vaginal secretions, and cerebrospinal, synovial, pleural, peritoneal, pericardial, and amniotic fluids.

Protective barriers include gloves, gowns, aprons, masks, and protective eyewear. Safe injection and sharps practices are essential, and needle-stick or sharp injuries should be reported immediately to the health service.

🧠 CLINICAL REASONING CUE
Even without a posted sign, the lecture expects you to connect the patient’s case with the precautions needed before beginning the physical examination.

Comprehensive assessment

  • Appropriate for new patients in office, clinic, or hospital
  • Builds personalized foundational knowledge
  • Strengthens the clinician-patient relationship
  • Helps identify or rule out physical causes
  • Creates a baseline for future assessments
  • Supports health promotion, education, and counseling
  • Develops essential examination skills

Focused assessment

  • Appropriate for established patients
  • Addresses focused concerns or symptoms
  • Uses examination methods relevant to the affected body system
  • Assesses the concern as thoroughly and carefully as possible
✅ RECOMMENDED ORDER
Move from broad to more specific systems and from head to toe or anterior to posterior.
Examining from the patient’s right side p. 5
  • JVP estimates are described as more reliable.
  • The palpating hand rests more comfortably on the apical impulse.
  • The right kidney is more frequently palpable than the left.
  • Examination tables often support a right-handed approach.
  • Left-handed students are encouraged to adopt right-sided positioning while still using the left hand for percussion or instruments.
Adapting for a patient at bedrest p. 5
  1. Examine the head, neck, and anterior chest with the patient supine.
  2. Roll the patient to each side to listen to the lungs, examine the back, and inspect the skin.
  3. Return the patient to supine and complete the remaining examination.

Cardinal Techniques

pp. 5–6
Usual sequence: IPaPeA = Inspection → Palpation → Percussion → Auscultation
Abdomen: IAPePa = Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation

Inspection

Close observation of appearance, behavior, movement, facial expression, mood, body habitus, skin findings, eye movement, pharyngeal color, thoracic symmetry, JVP height, abdominal contour, edema, and gait.

Palpation

Uses the palmar fingers or fingertips to assess elevation, depression, warmth, tenderness, lymph nodes, pulses, organ and mass contours, and crepitus. The hypothenar side may be used to feel fremitus.

Percussion

A plexor finger strikes the distal pleximeter finger to generate a sound and tactile vibration. Notes include resonance, dullness, tympany, and flatness.

Auscultation

Uses the diaphragm and bell to assess heart, lung, and bowel sounds by location, timing, duration, pitch, and intensity, and to detect bruits or vascular turbulence.

⭐ GENERAL SURVEY GOAL
Describe the patient’s distinguishing features so clearly that colleagues could spot the patient in a crowd of strangers.

General Appearance

pp. 6–7

State of health & consciousness

Judge whether the patient appears acutely or chronically ill, frail, fit, or robust. Determine whether the patient is awake, alert, and responsive.

Distress

Look for cardiorespiratory distress, pain behavior, anxiety, depression, tachypnea, or cardiorespiratory labor.

Skin & visible lesions

Observe scars, plaques, nevi, pallor, cyanosis, jaundice, rashes, bruises, or mottling.

Dress, grooming, hygiene

Assess suitability, cleanliness, footwear, jewelry, piercings, hair, nails, makeup, and whether appearance is appropriate for age, lifestyle, and occupation.

Expression & eye contact

Observe at rest, during conversation, and during the examination. Eye contact may be natural, sustained, quickly averted, or absent.

Odor, posture, gait, movement

Note body or breath odor, preferred posture, assistance needs, involuntary movement, immobility, balance, limp, fear of falling, and movement disorders.

Lecture-supported appearance clues p. 6
  • Excess clothing may reflect cold intolerance, rashes, needle marks, masking anorexia, or lifestyle preferences.
  • Holes or slippers may suggest painful foot conditions; run-down shoes may contribute to pain, calluses, falls, and infection.
  • Hair, nail polish, and nail biting may provide clues to illness duration, stress, mood, lifestyle, or self-regard.
  • Patients with left-sided heart failure may prefer sitting upright; patients with COPD may lean forward with arms braced.
  • Anxious patients may be agitated and restless; patients in pain may avoid movement.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP
Never assume that alcohol odor fully explains altered mental status or neurologic findings; the lecture warns that other serious and treatable causes may coexist.

Why BMI is used

It is a quick, non-invasive screening estimate of weight status and an initial prompt for further assessment of metabolic health and lifestyle.

Limitations

BMI is not a perfect measure of body fat, especially in specific populations such as athletes and older adults. Interpretation should consider muscle mass.

Waist Circumference

p. 7

  • Other anthropometric measures include head and abdominal circumference, skinfolds, and waist-to-hip ratio.

Blood Pressure Technique

STEP 3
Auscultate

STEP 4

Phase 1 onset = systolic; Phase 5 disappearance = diastolic.

Cuff details

  • Lower cuff border about 2.5 cm above the antecubital crease
  • Snug enough to permit insertion of two fingers
  • Arm slightly flexed
  • Ensure the arm is free of clothing, dialysis fistula, brachial cut-down scars, or lymphedema

Patient preparation

  • Avoid smoking or caffeine for 30 minutes before measurement
  • Sit quietly for at least 5 minutes with feet on the floor
  • Use a quiet, comfortably warm room
  • Confirm a viable brachial pulse and support the arm at heart level
⚠️ PITFALL / AUSCULTATORY GAP
The palpatory estimate plus 30 mmHg prevents unnecessarily high cuff pressure and helps avoid errors from an auscultatory gap.

Orthostatic Hypotension

pp. 7–8
Supine after 3–10 min rest → stand → repeat within 3 min
Positive: SBP ↓ ≥20 mmHg OR DBP ↓ ≥10 mmHg

The lecture lists drugs, moderate-to-severe blood loss, prolonged bed rest, and diseases of the autonomic nervous system as causes. Measure both blood pressure and heart rate in the two positions.

Heart rate

Use the radial pulse or listen to the apical pulse at the cardiac apex. Count for one full minute.

Respiratory rate

Count visually or listen subtly over the trachea for one minute. Observe rate, rhythm, depth, and effort. The lecture gives a normal value of 20 breaths/min in a quiet, regular pattern.

Temperature

Routes include oral, rectal, axillary, tympanic, and temporal artery. Core temperature is about 37°C (98.6°F) and fluctuates about 1°C daily.

🧠 CLINICAL CUE
Do not alert the patient that you are counting respirations, because awareness may change the breathing pattern. Prolonged expiration is noted in the lecture as common in COPD.

Skin Examination

p. 8

Inspect and palpate the skin

  • Color
  • Moisture
  • Temperature
  • Mobility and turgor—the lecture uses good versus poor turgor as a hydration cue.
  • Lesions—note whether margins are irregular or well delineated.
  • Texture

Nails and hair

  • Inspect and palpate the fingernails and toenails; document color, shape, and lesions.
  • Inspect and palpate the hair; note quantity, distribution, and texture.
  • Check specifically for alopecia.

Do not stop at “rash” or “lesion.” Build the description from primary lesion → number → size → shape → color → texture → location → configuration.

HEENT: Head and Eyes

p. 9

Head examination

  • Hair: quantity, distribution, and texture.
  • Scalp: inspect and palpate for lumps or lesions.
  • Skull: assess size and contour.
  • Face: assess symmetry and facial expression.
  • Skin: note color, texture, and hair distribution.

Eye examination sequence

  1. Check visual acuity and screen the visual fields.
  2. Note the position and alignment of the eyes.
  3. Observe the eyelids; inspect the sclera and conjunctiva of each eye.
  4. Use oblique lighting to inspect the cornea, iris, and lens.
  5. Compare the pupils and test their reaction to light; the lecture links this to CN II.
  6. Assess extraocular movements—CN III, IV, and VI.
  7. Use the ophthalmoscope to inspect the ocular fundi.
🧠 CLINICAL REASONING CUE
The lecture emphasizes that assessment of extraocular movements and the ocular fundi can help identify possible cranial-nerve involvement or damage.

HEENT: Ears, Nose, Sinuses, Mouth & Pharynx

p. 9

Ears

  • Inspect the auricles, canals, and eardrums with an otoscope.
  • Look for obstruction, determine whether the eardrum is intact, and check for cerumen.
  • Check auditory acuity.
  • When indicated, perform Weber for lateralization and Rinne to compare air and bone conduction when evaluating conductive versus sensorineural hearing loss.

Nose and sinuses

  • Examine the external nose using a light or otoscope and a nasal speculum.
  • Inspect the nasal mucosa, septum, and turbinates.
  • Palpate the frontal and maxillary sinuses for tenderness, especially when congestion or sinusitis is present.
  • The lecture figure identifies the frontal, ethmoid, sphenoid, and maxillary sinuses.
Mouth and pharynx examination p. 9
  • Wear a mask first.
  • Use a penlight and tongue depressor.
  • Inspect the lips, oral mucosa, gums, teeth, tongue, palate, tonsils, and pharynx.
  • The lecture notes that cranial nerves may be assessed during this portion: CN IX and X for the gag reflex, CN XII for tongue movement, and CN VII via the chorda tympani for taste sensation.
🔬 HEENT CRANIAL-NERVE ANCHORS
Pupillary light reaction → CN II. Extraocular movements → CN III, IV, VI. Gag reflex → CN IX and X. Tongue movement → CN XII. Taste via chorda tympani → CN VII.

Neck and Back

p. 9

Neck sequence

  • Inspect and palpate the cervical lymph nodes bilaterally; look for lymphadenopathy.
  • Note masses or unusual pulsations.
  • Ask the patient to swallow; the thyroid normally follows the trachea.
  • Feel for tracheal deviation.
  • Observe the sound and effort of breathing.
  • Inspect and palpate the thyroid gland.

Back

  • Inspect and palpate the spine and muscles of the back.
  • Compare shoulder height for symmetry; asymmetry may indicate an underlying musculoskeletal issue.
⭐ CHEST ORDER
Inspection → Palpation → Percussion → Auscultation (IPaPeA). Use the diaphragm directly on the skin; clothing may create friction and added sounds.

Set Up and Position the Patient

pp. 9–10

General setup

  • Listen over both the posterior and anterior chest and lungs.
  • Identify normal breath sounds and any adventitious or added sounds.
  • When indicated, assess transmitted voice sounds.
  • For a patient lying on the back, keep the arms somewhat abducted for comfort.

When breathing is difficult

Raise the head of the examining table or bed to increase respiratory excursion and ease breathing.

If the patient cannot tolerate a flat position → modify the position before continuing.

Detailed anterior sequence p. 10
  • Begin by inspecting and palpating the spine and muscles of the upper back.
  • Inspect, palpate, and percuss the anterior chest in an orderly side-to-side pattern.
  • Identify the level of diaphragmatic dullness on each side.
  • The heart normally creates an area of dullness to the left of the sternum from the 3rd to 5th interspaces.
  • Auscultate breath sounds, identify added sounds, and assess transmitted voice sounds when indicated.

Posterior Chest and Lungs

p. 10

Posterior sequence

  1. Inspect and palpate the spine and upper-back muscles.
  2. Inspect, palpate, and percuss the posterior chest.
  3. Identify the level of diaphragmatic dullness on each side.
  4. Listen to breath sounds and added sounds; assess transmitted voice sounds when indicated.

Comparison pattern

Examine corresponding points in sequence—left to right and downward, then vice versa—rather than finishing one entire side before the other.

Adventitious or Added Breath Sounds

pp. 10, 15

Crackles or rales

  • Discontinuous, intermittent, nonmusical, and brief—described in the figure as “dots in time.”
  • Fine crackles: soft, high-pitched, and very brief.
  • Coarse crackles: somewhat louder, lower in pitch, and brief.
  • The lecture associates crackles with abnormalities of the lung parenchyma—pneumonia, interstitial lung disease, pulmonary fibrosis, atelectasis, and heart failure—or airways such as bronchitis and bronchiectasis.

Wheezes and rhonchi

  • Continuous, sinusoidal, musical, and prolonged—“dashes in time.”
  • Wheezes: relatively high-pitched with a hissing or shrill quality.
  • Rhonchi: relatively low-pitched with a snoring quality.
  • Wheezes arise in narrowed airways in asthma, COPD, and bronchitis. The lecture notes that “rhonchi” may describe secretions in large airways and may change with coughing.

“99” louder and clearer → abnormal bronchophony. “Ee” becomes “ay” → abnormal egophony. Whispered “1-2-3” becomes clearer → abnormal whispered pectoriloquy.

Breasts, Axillae, and Epitrochlear Nodes

p. 11
🧠 BEFORE EXAMINING THE BREAST
Notify the patient that the examination is about to begin and ask about any observed lumps or other breast problems.

Woman’s breast examination

  1. Inspect with the arms relaxed.
  2. Repeat with the arms elevated.
  3. Repeat with the hands pressed on the hips.
  4. Inspect skin color and contour, thickening and unusually prominent pores, breast size and symmetry, and nipple characteristics.
  5. Palpate tissue consistency, tenderness, nodules, lumps, or masses.

The lecture states that the ideal timing is 5–7 days after menstruation because the breasts tend to become more swollen and nodular premenstrually.

Men and both sexes

  • For a man, inspect the nipple and areola for nodules, swelling, or ulceration.
  • Palpate the areola and breast tissue for nodules.
  • For both sexes, inspect the skin of both axillae for rash, infection, or unusual pigmentation.
  • Palpate the axillary and epitrochlear nodes for enlargement.

Cardiovascular System

p. 11
⭐ CARDIOVASCULAR ORDER
Inspection → Palpation → Percussion → Auscultation (IPaPeA).

Position, inspection, and palpation

  • Ask the patient to lie down.
  • Use the left lateral decubitus position so selected heart sounds or possible murmurs can be heard better.
  • Inspect and palpate the precordium.
  • Describe the apical impulse by location, diameter, amplitude, and duration.
  • Palpate the point of maximal impulse (PMI).

Auscultation

  • Listen at each auscultatory area with the diaphragm.
  • Use the bell at the apex and lower sternal border.
  • Identify the first and second heart sounds—S1 and S2.
  • Listen for physiologic splitting of S2.
  • Listen for abnormal heart sounds or murmurs, including in left lateral decubitus.

Detailed abdominal examination pp. 11–12
  • Ask the patient to flex the hips and knees to relax the abdomen; the lecture identifies this as the best position for the abdominal examination.
  • Palpate lightly, then deeply.
  • Assess the liver and spleen first by percussion and then by palpation.
  • Percuss the liver for dullness to identify its borders and approximate its size.
  • Try to feel the kidneys, and palpate the aorta and its pulsations.
  • When kidney infection is suspected, percuss posteriorly over the costovertebral angles for tenderness.
⚠️ AORTIC PALPATION WARNING
The lecture specifically warns to be careful and not to palpate when an abdominal aortic aneurysm is suspected.
✅ DO NOT CONFUSE
Chest and cardiovascular systems → IPaPeA. Abdomen → IAPePa. The differentiator is that abdominal auscultation occurs before percussion and palpation.
✅ POSITION-FIRST FRAMEWORK
While the patient is supine, assess the peripheral vascular, musculoskeletal, and nervous systems. Reassess selected components when the patient stands.

Lower Extremities: Patient Supine

p. 12

Peripheral vascular system

  • Palpate femoral pulses and, when indicated, popliteal pulses; the lecture states that these are best assessed supine.
  • Palpate the inguinal lymph nodes.
  • Inspect for edema, discoloration, or ulcers.
  • Palpate for pitting edema.

Musculoskeletal system

  • Note deformities or enlarged joints.
  • When indicated, palpate joints, assess range of motion, and perform the relevant joint maneuvers.
  • Grade lower-extremity strength using the lecture’s 0–5 motor scale.

Nervous system

  • Assess muscle bulk, tone, and strength.
  • Assess proprioception and reflexes.
  • Observe for abnormal movements.

Lower Extremities: Patient Standing

p. 12

Vascular and musculoskeletal

  • Inspect for varicose veins.
  • For the ankle-brachial index, obtain lower-extremity pressure and divide ankle systolic pressure by brachial systolic pressure.
  • The lecture uses ABI to determine blood-flow insufficiency to the lower extremities.
  • Examine the alignment and range of motion of the spine.
  • Examine alignment of the legs and feet.

Neurologic and additional standing maneuvers

  • Observe the patient’s gait.
  • Ask the patient to walk heel-to-toe, walk on tiptoes, walk on the heels, hop in place, and perform shallow knee bends.
  • Perform a Romberg test and check for pronator drift.

Mask the patient, use a single room when possible, don a mask upon entry, and limit transport. The lecture likewise states a six-foot distance plus mask, face shield, gown, and gloves.

1. Before the Examination

pp. 1–3

Six preparation tasks

  1. Reflect on your approach and professional demeanor.
  2. Adjust lighting and environment.
  3. Check equipment.
  4. Make the patient comfortable.
  5. Observe standard and universal precautions.
  6. Choose an orderly sequence.

Professional behavior

  • Be straightforward: explain each step and ask consent.
  • Be calm, organized, and competent.
  • Warn before discomfort and respond to anxiety or vulnerability.
  • Maximize time and be selective without losing comprehensiveness.
  • Be thorough, systematic, flexible, gentle, and willing to cause needed discomfort.

Set the stage

Good lighting reveals subtle signs; a quiet environment supports auscultation and communication. Tangential lighting is used for the JVP, thyroid, and apical impulse; perpendicular lighting reduces shadows but flattens depth and texture.

Comfort, dignity, and closure

Close doors or curtains, drape so only the examined area is exposed, keep the patient informed, give clear instructions, state general impressions and next steps, wash hands, clean equipment, and discard waste properly.

🔬 WAIST CUES
When BMI is ≥35 kg/m², measure just above the hips. The lecture’s risk thresholds are ≥35 inches (~89 cm) in women and ≥40 inches (~102 cm) in men.
Blood pressure: exact sequence pp. 7–8
  1. Seat or position the patient properly; keep the room quiet and warm, the arm bare, and the brachial artery at heart level.
  2. Place the cuff snugly with the bladder centered over the brachial artery; lower edge about 2.5 cm above the antecubital crease.
  3. Palpate the radial pulse, inflate until it disappears, note the pressure, and add 30 mmHg.
  4. Deflate fully and wait 15–30 seconds.
  5. Place the bell over the brachial artery, reinflate, and slowly deflate.
  6. Korotkoff phase 1 = systolic pressure; phase 5 disappearance = diastolic pressure.

Orthostatic hypotension

Measure BP and heart rate supine after 3–10 minutes of rest, then within 3 minutes of standing. Diagnostic drop: SBP ≥20 mmHg or DBP ≥10 mmHg.

Other vital signs

  • Heart rate: radial or apical pulse for one minute.
  • Respiratory rate: discreet visual inspection or tracheal listening for one minute; note rate, rhythm, depth, and effort.
  • Temperature: oral, rectal, axillary, tympanic, or temporal artery; core temperature is about 37°C and fluctuates about 1°C daily.

11. Final Common Traps

Entire lecture
⚠️ TRAP 1
Do not begin with the first body region before preparing the patient, setting, equipment, consent, privacy, and precautions.
⚠️ TRAP 2
Do not confuse standard, transmission-based, universal, and reverse-isolation purposes.
⚠️ TRAP 3
Do not use the usual IPaPeA order for the abdomen.
⚠️ TRAP 4
Do not inflate the BP cuff to an arbitrary value; use palpatory systolic estimate + 30 mmHg.
⚠️ TRAP 5
Do not count respirations in a way that alerts the patient and changes the breathing pattern.
⚠️ TRAP 6
Do not listen to lung sounds through clothing because friction can create added sounds.
⚠️ TRAP 7
Do not confuse flat versus raised lesions or the 1-cm cutoff for macule/patch, papule/plaque, and vesicle/bulla.

Do not assess pitting edema standing; the lecture identifies the supine position.
