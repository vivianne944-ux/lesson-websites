Overview

The lecture organizes liquid prescriptions by dosage form, route, concentration, computation, and dispensing unit. The recurring goal is to write the correct form and calculate the exact amount the patient needs.

Pages 1–10
Core objective

Write the right form, then prove the quantity

The session emphasizes correctness of form and precision in dosage. Every prescription therefore has two linked tasks: identify how the drug is supplied, then calculate how much must be dispensed.

⭐ Must know
Do not stop after computing the dose. Convert it into the correct volume, bottle count, ampule count, vial count, respule count, or drop instruction.
Big picture

Six liquid prescription groups

  1. Single-ingredient oral liquid
  2. Multiple-ingredient oral liquid
  3. Parenteral solution
  4. Parenteral solution/suspension from dry solids
  5. Aerosol
  6. Ophthalmic or otic preparation

Single-Ingredient Oral Liquids

Three recurring patterns: a household measure, drops, and a ready-made stock solution supplied in bottles.

Pages 1–3
Pattern 1

Teaspoonful prescription

The lecture uses Paregoric (4% camphorated opium tincture) at 5 mL every four hours as needed for three days. Because it is a stock solution, the quantity to dispense is incorporated into the inscription rather than written as a separate compounded formula.

Total amount of drug = dose per intake × total number of doses
⭐ Must know
For patient convenience, the dose may be written as a household measure, but the computation must use its mL equivalent.
Pattern 2

Drops and the official medicine dropper

The official medicine dropper is described as 3 mm in external diameter at the delivery end and, when held vertically, delivers 20 drops of water.

Belladonna example: 0.6 mL × 45 drops/mL = 27 drops

The number of drops per mL is influenced by the dropper diameter, surface tension, and specific gravity.

🔬 Lab cue
If the prescription gives a dose in drops, check whether the lecture supplies a specific drops/mL value for that liquid.

Multiple-Ingredient Oral Liquids

Compute the total bulk first, then multiply each ingredient’s dose by the total number of doses. The inscription follows the order shown in the lecture.

Pages 3–5

Order of ingredients and total bulk

BasisFirst item in the lecture’s ordering
AdjuvantSecond item in the lecture’s ordering
VehicleFinal item; usually followed by “to make”
Total doses = frequency/day × daysTotal bulk = amount of one dose × total dosesTotal amount of each drug = dose/intake × total doses

In the cough-mixture example, each 5 mL dose contains dextromethorphan hydrobromide 10 mg and ammonium chloride 0.3 g in Terpin Hydrate Elixir.

✅ Key point
The final ingredient is followed by “to make”, meaning the excipient is added until the stated final bulk or volume is reached.

Vehicles in the lecture

A vehicle may add bulk, act as a solvent, or provide flavoring or coloring. The lecture notes that it may sometimes exert corrective or therapeutic action.

p. 4

Official vs extemporaneous orders

Extemporaneous or magistral: compounded according to the physician’s formula.

Official: calls for a preparation with a definite formula in the pharmacopeia or National Formulary.

⚠️ Trade-off noted by the lecture
Official and ready-made formulas are convenient, but the individual ingredient doses may be less adjustable than in an extemporaneous formula.

Extemporaneous, 1 month

  • 1 dose/day × 30 days = 30 doses
  • 5 mL × 30 = 150 mL total bulk
  • Isoniazid: 200 mg × 30 = 6 g
  • Pyridoxine HCl: 10 mg × 30 = 0.3 g
  • Strawberry syrup: 20% of 150 mL = 30 mL

Ready-made, 6 months

  • 1 dose/day × 180 days = 180 doses
  • 5 mL × 180 = 900 mL
  • 900 mL ÷ 120 mL/bottle = 7.5 → 8 bottles

Parenteral Solutions

State the official preparation, its concentration or amount per ampule/syringe, the number to dispense, and the exact injection instruction.

Pages 5–6

Core writing pattern

  • Use the official name of the preparation.
  • Write the concentration and/or amount in one ampule after the name.
  • Follow it with the number to be dispensed.
  • State dose, route, interval, indication if present, and maximum number of doses.
⚠️ Source mismatch
The paragraph states every 15–30 minutes, while the sample label in Figure 15 reads every 10 minutes.

Desired-dose conversion

Desired volume = (ordered dose ÷ amount on hand) × quantity
🔬 Worked cue
Midazolam: (1.5 mg ÷ 5 mg) × 1 mL = 0.3 mL.

The lecture’s example writes the result as a slow intravenous push on arrival at the operating room and instructs that the prescription be written in triplicate.

Dry Solids for Parenteral Use

Reconstitution adds a diluent to a dry solid to create a liquid. The prescription must state how to dissolve, dilute, and administer the preparation.

Pages 7–8

Language that signals reconstitution

Reconstitution is defined in the lecture as adding a diluent to a dry solid ingredient to make it a liquid. When the reconstituted product is a solution, the label should begin with “Dissolve”.

⭐ Must know
For the crystalline penicillin example, the ideal diluent volume is 1.5 mL, with a stated range of 1–2 mL.
✅ Lecture instruction
“Inject only after a negative skin test” is generally placed for parenteral drugs likely to cause hypersensitivity, especially antimicrobials; the lecture notes an exception in life-threatening or emergency situations.

Two-vial vaccine reconstitution sequence

  1. Withdraw the entire adjuvant suspension with a sterile needle and syringe while slightly tilting the vial.
  2. Slowly transfer it into the vial containing the lyophilized antigen powder.
  3. Gently swirl until dissolved; do not shake vigorously.
  4. Withdraw 0.5 mL and administer intramuscularly.
🔬 Recognition cue
If the box contains one liquid vial and one powder vial intended to be mixed, the label must preserve the transfer → dissolve → withdraw final dose sequence.
Practice Exercise 26, p. 7

Aerosol, Ophthalmic, and Otic Liquids

These forms are recognized by their delivery device and site-specific label: nebulizer, conjunctival sac, eye, or ear.

Pages 8–9

Aerosol solutions in nebules or respules

A nebulizer converts the solution into a mist inhaled through a tube or mask. The lecture emphasizes that the label must specify frequency, duration, and, when needed, indication for use.

Budesonide: 250 mcg/2 mL every 12 h for 2 days2 administrations/day × 2 days = 4 respules
🔬 Recognition cue
If the preparation is supplied as one dose per plastic container, count containers/administration × total administrations.

Ophthalmic preparations

The drug is placed in a plastic container and instilled into the conjunctival sac. The gatifloxacin example uses 1 drop in the right eye every hour for 5 days.

⭐ Must know
When another eye medication is also prescribed, the lecture instructs a 5-minute interval to avoid washout of the previously instilled drug.

Otic drops: convert drops to volume

3 drops × 4 times/day = 12 drops/day12 drops × 1/20 mL = 0.6 mL/day0.6 mL/day × 7 days = 4.2 mL4.2 mL ÷ 5 mL/bottle = 0.84 → 1 bottle

Both eyes changes the drop count

1 drop × 2 eyes × 3 times/day = 6 drops/day6 drops × 1/20 mL = 0.3 mL/day0.3 mL/day × 5 days = 1.5 mL

Lab Practical Guide

Use this as a writing sequence: identify the form, compute the dose, convert to the supplied preparation, and make the label complete enough to administer safely.

Synthesized from pp. 1–9

1. Recognize the supply

  • Bottle: oral stock solution, suspension, ophthalmic, otic
  • Ampule: ready-to-inject parenteral solution
  • Pre-filled syringe: fixed parenteral dose
  • Vial with powder: reconstitution required
  • Nebule/respule: aerosol via nebulizer

2. Compute in the right order

  1. Weight × mg/kg/day
  2. Divide by doses/day
  3. Convert mg/dose to mL/dose
  4. Multiply by total doses
  5. Divide by container size
  6. Round up when needed

3. Complete the label

Across the lecture examples, the label specifies the relevant combination of:

  • dose or volume
  • route or site
  • frequency
  • round-the-clock or as-needed use
  • duration
  • special preparation steps such as shake, dissolve, dilute, swirl, or use a nebulizer

Short-answer writing template

R/ [Drug + dosage form] [Concentration / strength] Dispense No. [number] [bottle(s) / ampule(s) / vial(s) / respule(s)] Label: [Preparation instruction, if needed]. [Give / take / instill / inject] [dose or volume] [route/site] every [interval] [round the clock / as needed] for [duration].

Adapt the wording to the dosage form shown in the lecture. Stock products emphasize concentration and container count; compounded liquids list ingredients and the final volume “to make.”

Final check before signing

  • Is the dose in mg, mcg, units, mL, or drops?
  • Did you use the correct concentration?
  • Did you account for all scheduled doses?
  • Did you round the bottle/vial count up?
  • Is the route correct: oral, IM, IV, SC, inhalation, eye, or ear?
  • For eye/ear drugs, did you specify side or both sides?
  • Does the label include frequency and duration?
⚠️ Trans-check habit
Some worked examples contain printed mismatches between the case text, arithmetic line, or sample label. Verify the stated schedule, route, and final total against each other.
