Tab 1 · Whole-Lesson Orientation

Overview

This lecture frames prescribing as a logical, patient-centered process: define the problem, decide what treatment should accomplish, select the best option using ESSC, write the prescription clearly, counsel the patient, and monitor the result.

Step 01

Diagnosis

Identify the primary diagnosis and relevant secondary diagnoses.

Step 02

Goals

State whether the objective is cure, relief, or prevention.

Step 03

Selection

Choose advice, non-drug care, a P-drug, or referral.

Step 04

Prescribe & Counsel

Write clearly and explain effects, side effects, instructions, and warnings.

Step 05

Monitor

Check whether goals were achieved and whether harm occurred.

Core Definition

Rational drug therapy

The WHO definition emphasizes the right medication, an adequate dose, a sufficient duration, appropriateness to the patient’s clinical needs, and the lowest cost to the patient and community. p. 1

Ethical Aim

Four simultaneous duties

  • Maximize clinical effectiveness
  • Minimize harms
  • Avoid wasting scarce resources
  • Respect patient choice

Tab 2 · Lesson Proper Begins

Foundations of Rational Drug Therapy

Prescribing is presented as an art grounded in clinical reasoning, not an isolated writing task.

Prescribing Requires

Five integrated capabilities

  • Diagnostic skills
  • Knowledge of medicines
  • Understanding of clinical pharmacology principles
  • Communication skills
  • Appreciation of each drug’s risks and benefits for a specific patient
Why It Matters

Problems rational therapy tries to correct

  • More than 50% of medicines are prescribed, dispensed, or sold inappropriately.
  • 50% of patients fail to take medicines correctly.
  • About one-third of the world’s population lacks access to essential medicines.

Lecture statistics. p. 1

Clinical Relevance

Appropriate drug use is part of quality care

The lecture treats rational drug use as an essential element in achieving good-quality health and medical care—not as an optional refinement.

WHO formulation, 1952

Patients should receive medicines appropriate to their clinical needs, in doses meeting their individual requirements, for an adequate period, and at the lowest cost to them and their community.

WHO revised formulation, 1985

Right drug + adequate dose + sufficient duration + appropriateness to the patient’s clinical needs + lowest cost.

Exam Trap

“Rational” does not simply mean “effective”

A drug can be pharmacologically effective yet still be irrational if it is unsafe, unsuitable, unnecessarily expensive, incorrectly dosed, poorly explained, or inadequately monitored.

Tab 3 · Rational or Ethical Prescribing

Ethical Prescribing and Prescribing Errors

Ethical prescribing converts clinical knowledge into a safe, clear, patient-specific plan.

Core considerations

  1. Evaluate and define the patient’s problem or diagnosis.
  2. Determine the therapeutic objectives.
  3. Select an appropriate medication.
  4. Consider drug cost.
  5. Give information, warnings, and instructions.
  6. Monitor the patient regularly.

Every prescription order should be

LegibleUnambiguousDated Timed for chart ordersClearly signed

These features support communication among the prescriber, pharmacist, and nurse.

Prescribing Error · Omission

Vague continuation orders are unsafe

“Continue present IVF” fails to identify the exact intravenous fluid. “Resume preoperative meds” assumes a complete and accurate medication record is available.

Prescribing Error · Writing

Illegibility and decimal ambiguity

A misplaced or unclear decimal can turn 0.1 into 1 or 1.0 into 10.

Prescribing Error · Clinical Assessment

Failure to detect contraindications, other drugs, or incompatibilities

  • Failure to recognize contraindications caused by the patient’s other diseases
  • Failure to obtain a complete medication history
  • Failure to recognize possible physicochemical incompatibilities

Lecture examples include certain insulin preparations that should not be mixed and intestinal absorption of drugs such as tetracycline being compromised by simultaneous administration of antacids or products high in “methyl content” as stated in the trans.

Do Not Confuse

Safety versus Suitability

Safety focuses on the drug’s adverse effects and toxicity. Suitability asks whether that drug fits this particular patient, including comorbidities, current medicines, dosage form, dosing convenience, adherence, and availability.

Tab 4 · Special Attention

Adherence, Legal, and Socioeconomic Factors

Rational prescribing fails when the patient cannot obtain, understand, tolerate, afford, or correctly use the treatment.

Compliance / Adherence

Four major types of noncompliance

  1. Failure to obtain the medication.
  2. Failure to take it as prescribed because of wrong dose, frequency, timing or sequence, route, or purpose.
  1. Premature discontinuation after symptom improvement or when the bottle becomes empty.
  2. Inappropriate use, including sharing medications.
Legal Factor

OTC versus prescription drugs

OTC drugs may be safely administered by a layperson for self-limiting conditions. Prescription drugs require a licensed physician, especially controlled substances such as opioids, hallucinogens, stimulants, depressants, and anabolic steroids.

Controlled or regulated drugs → physician must obtain an S-2 license.
Drug Safety Surveillance

Regulatory roles

  • Government controls drug labeling and distribution.
  • Licensing boards determine who may prescribe.
  • The FDA monitors drug safety through mandatory company reporting and voluntary healthcare-practitioner reporting.
  • The Philippine National Formulary guides medicines purchasable by government hospitals and reimbursable by PhilHealth, as stated in the lecture.
Labeled vs Unlabeled Use

Indications may not cover every possible use

Off-label use means using an approved drug for a disease it is not approved to treat, in a different dosage form, or at a different dose than approved.

Generic Prescribing

Generic name is required

Physicians must prescribe the generic name, allowing the pharmacist to supply a generically equivalent product and offer the cheapest available option. The prescriber may indicate a brand, but the lecture emphasizes the generic-name requirement.

Republic Act No. 6675

Generic terminology in DOH facilities

The lecture states that DOH facilities and personnel should use generic names or terminology in procurement, prescribing, dispensing, and administration, and that doctors are not allowed to direct pharmacists not to substitute a brand by writing “dispense only as prescribed.” p. 2

Cost is broader than unit price

  • Consider the total treatment cost, not merely the price of one dose.
  • The prescriber influences cost through the quantity dispensed, refill authorization, and frequency of filling.
  • The lecture notes potential generic-product concerns such as a high ratio of inert to active ingredients, poor solubility, and a low therapeutic index.
  • Bioequivalence information and prices should be available from the FDA.
Tab 5 · Choosing the P-Drug

The Rational Prescribing Workflow

A well-chosen P-drug depends on proper assessment of the patient’s problem and knowledge of the pharmacological properties of the available drugs.

Expanded P-drug selection outline

  1. Diagnosis
  2. Therapeutic objectives
  3. Non-drug measures
  4. Inventory of effective drug groups, then specific drugs
  5. Write the prescription for the patient
  6. Counsel the patient on appropriate use
A · Diagnosis

Base decisions on the primary diagnosis

Include relevant secondary diagnoses. Lecture example: a diabetic patient with fever, cough, and dyspnea has pneumonia as the primary diagnosis and type 2 diabetes mellitus as a secondary diagnosis.

B · Goals

Cure, relief, or prevention

For pneumonia, goals may be to cure the infection, ease coughing, and prevent complications or recurrence.

C · Selection

Treatment is not always a drug

Options include advice and information, non-drug treatment, drug treatment, or referral.

Important Warning

Do not medicalize every problem

Not all health problems need drug treatment, and not all personal treatment plans consist of medicines.

Synchronous Lecture Note

Write therapeutic objectives in priority order

The primary therapeutic objective should be listed first because it is the first goal the group should answer. The lecture notes that objectives often begin with phrases such as “to decide on,” “to cure,” “to prevent,” or “to refer.”

Tab 6 · P-Drug Selection

ESSC: Efficacy, Safety, Suitability, Cost

First score drug groups. Then list and score specific drugs within the highest-scoring group.

E · Efficacy

Will the drug achieve the objective?

  • Mechanism of action
  • PK: onset, duration, bioavailability, tissue distribution, metabolism, excretion
  • PD: specificity/selectivity, dose response, therapeutic index
  • Spectrum of activity for antibiotics
  • Supporting journal reports, when available
S · Safety

What harm can occur?

  • Known adverse effects
  • Their frequency, seriousness, and predictability
  • Possibility of toxicity
S · Suitability

Does the drug fit this patient?

  • Previous adverse drug reactions
  • Age, pregnancy, comorbidities, and vulnerability to adverse effects
  • Current medicines and possible interactions
  • Likely adherence, taste, dosage form, frequency, and dose
  • Availability and practicality
C · Cost

What is the total treatment cost?

  • Score the whole course, not just one unit.
  • The lower total price usually earns a higher rating.
  • Compare innovator drugs with cheaper alternatives having similar efficacy.
Scoring scale used in the lecture: + to ++++ for each criterion → compare vertically, criterion by criterion → add totals.

Scoring Rule

Compare each criterion side by side per drug. Do not score one complete drug in isolation before comparing the same criterion across the alternatives.

Lecture-Specific Note

When drugs in the same P-drug group share the same mechanism, the lecture permits using their PK and PD differences under Efficacy. Research studies may also be included under Efficacy.

Equipment and Cost

For drugs requiring special equipment, such as nebules needing a nebulizer, the equipment price does not need to be included in the ESSC cost score. In practice, still ask whether the patient has the required equipment.

Tab 7 · From Choice to Use

Prescription Writing and Patient Counseling

Selection is incomplete until the treatment is written clearly and the patient knows how to use it.

Prescription components

  1. Name, age, and date
  2. Generic name of the drug, with brand name when included
  3. Dispense number
  4. Label / instructions
  5. Signature and professional identifiers shown in the lecture examples

Counseling DomainQuestions the Prescriber Should Address
EffectsWhy is the drug needed? Which symptoms will disappear and which will not? When should the effect start? What happens if it is taken incorrectly or not at all?
Side effectsWhich may occur? How are they recognized? How long might they continue? How serious are they? What action should be taken?
InstructionsHow, when, and for how long should the drug be taken? How should it be stored? What should be done with leftover medicine?
WarningsWhen should the drug not be taken? What is the maximum dose? Why must the full course be completed?
Future consultationsWhen should the patient return, return earlier, or not return? What information should be brought to the next appointment?
“Everything Clear?”

Confirm understanding rather than assuming it

  • Ask whether everything is understood.
  • Ask the patient to repeat the most important information.
  • Ask whether there are questions.
Lecture Note

For medicines prescribed for symptomatic relief, the lecture notes a duration of 3–5 days.

Tab 9 · Sample Case 1

Post-Inflammatory Cough

The lecture applies the rational process to a 52-year-old taxi driver with a persistent nighttime cough after a cold.

Case Clues

Patient summary

  • Sore throat and cough beginning 2 weeks after a cold
  • Sneezing has stopped, but cough persists, especially at night
  • Heavy smoker repeatedly advised to stop
  • Examination: throat inflammation; otherwise nothing special
Rational Plan

Diagnosis to inventory

  • Diagnosis: Post-inflammatory cough
  • Therapeutic objective: Suppress the cough
  • Non-drug measure: Stop smoking
  • Possible P-drug groups: Opioid agonists and antihistamines
Clinical Cue

The case demonstrates that the rational plan includes both a symptom-focused objective and a relevant non-drug intervention.

Tab 10 · Sample Case 2

Acute Gastroenteritis with Mild Dehydration

This case shows how the diagnosis, hydration status, presumed cause, non-drug care, and ESSC scoring guide the choice of oral rehydration solution.

Patient and diagnosis

A 1-year-old boy has 7 watery stools, without blood, mucus, foul odor, fever, vomiting, or abdominal pain. He is active but thirsty, with dry lips and slightly sunken eyes.

Complete diagnosis: Acute gastroenteritis, mild dehydration, probably viral.

The lecture emphasizes including the illness, hydration status, and etiology when these help guide management.

Therapeutic plan

  • Objective: Rehydrate and prevent further dehydration
  • Non-drug measure: Continue breastfeeding
  • Possible drugs: ORS, opioid agonists, zinc sulfate

Tab 11 · Sample Case 3

Stable Angina and Nitrate Selection

The lecture compares nitrates, beta blockers, and calcium-channel blockers, then compares specific nitrates.

Problem definition

A 60-year-old man has several episodes of suffocating chest pain during physical labor that stop quickly with rest. Examination shows BP 130/85, pulse 78/min, normal body weight, and a right carotid murmur.

Diagnosis: Stable angina pectoris

Therapeutic objective and inventory

Primary objective: Stop an attack as soon as it starts by decreasing preload, contractility, and afterload.

Effective groups: Nitrates, beta blockers, calcium-channel blockers.

Common Confusions
  • Drug group selection comes before specific-drug selection.
  • Safety is not the same as Suitability.
  • Rational treatment may be non-drug advice or referral.
  • Lowest unit price is not always the lowest total treatment cost.
Final High-Yield Recap

The best prescription is not merely pharmacologically active. It must be effective, safe, suitable, affordable, clearly written, properly explained, and monitored.
