Core idea: history and examination work together

Medical history contains the patient’s subjective information. The physical examination supplies objective findings through examination, listening, touch, and proper positioning. Together, they guide clinical decision-making.

✅ Key point
The lecture treats history and physical examination as synergistic, not separate entities.

Four learning targets

  1. Use the correct approach and technique.
  2. Perform complete symptom analysis.
  3. Write the medical history in the prescribed format.
  4. Construct a temporal profile diagram.

Lesson proper begins here

Approach to the Interview

Prepare yourself, the environment, and the patient before detailed questioning begins.

Pages 1–2

Before the first question

  • Review the medical board.
  • Set goals for the interview.
  • Practice self-reflection: consider how expectations and reactions may affect what you hear and how you behave.
  • Review your clinical behavior and appearance.
  • Improve or adjust the environment.
  • Plan how notes will be taken without disrupting rapport.
“Am I too near?” “Am I using the proper non-verbal cues?” “Is the loudness of my voice offensive?”

Professionalism, privacy, and note-taking

The lecture describes professionalism as “unconditional positive regard.” Be mindful of tone, language, and nonverbal cues. Personal biases must be removed because the attitudes of physician and patient can color the outcome.

⚠️ Rapport pitfall
Patients may become distracted when they see the interviewer continuously taking notes. A practical team approach is for one student to interview while another records, then reverse roles on the next session.

Note-taking remains a supportive role: it can preserve forgotten questions and identify points that need follow-up. Sensitive questions should be asked in privacy; it is acceptable to offer a more private area or a bed farther from others.

Interviewing is both science and art

Science

The method must be learned, practiced continuously, and applied appropriately.

Art

The interviewer uses nonverbal cues and develops judgment through experience.

✅ Interview stance
Be collaborative, open-ended, neutral, and prepared to form a concrete plan from the medical information obtained.

Seven-step sequence of the interview

Greet the patient and establish rapport. Introduce yourself and your role. Observe the surroundings and personalize the interaction before formal questions.
Invite the patient’s story.
Establish the agenda. Explain what will be asked and what examination will follow.
Expand and clarify the story; generate and test diagnostic hypotheses.
Create a shared understanding of the problem.
Negotiate a plan: further evaluation, treatment, education, self-management support, and prevention.
Plan follow-up and close the interview.
Suggested opening: “Good morning, I am ___, a medical student, and I am here to ask you about your medical history.”

Make the opening personal

After introducing yourself, the lecture suggests brief questions such as “Kamusta po kayo?” and asking who is at the bedside. Even before formal history-taking, recognize the patient as a person and clarify the relationship of companions.

Skills with ongoing influence

Rapport, Mindfulness, Topic Tracking & Empathy

These skills influence the interview from beginning to end rather than appearing at only one step.

Pages 2–3, 12–13

Rapport building and relationship maintenance

  • Use a warm greeting with a smile.
  • Maintain eye contact with both the patient and the bedside companion.
  • Begin with a brief non-medical interaction.
  • Check on an important life event or how the patient has been since the last encounter.
⚠️ Pitfall
Trust may not develop when patients feel no connection with the physician. Use small talk or a brief “check-in” to re-establish the relationship.

Mindful practice

Observe the patient’s and your own thought processes. Remain present and critically curious.

  • Avoid cognitive shortcuts, including overuse of yes/no questions.
  • Do not let the physician’s agenda dominate the patient’s story.
  • Monitor your own preoccupations so you do not miss clues about important thoughts or feelings.
A physician notices that a patient withdraws after being lectured about non-compliance and realizes the patient’s own views about treatment were never explored.

Topic tracking: maintain a mutually agreed focus

Summarize. Ask the patient to verify and correct what you have understood.
Use process transparency. Name what is happening in the interaction, such as the patient introducing another concern.
Align goals. Decide together whether to stay with the current topic or shift focus.
⚠️ Organization pitfall
Without topic tracking, the interview may become disorganized, incomplete, rushed, or may shift topics without agreement. Step back like a third-party observer and reassess.

Acknowledge social and emotional cues

Empathic acknowledgment can focus the discussion or invite deeper exploration. It decreases the patient’s feeling of being ignored and can improve care.

“You appear worried. Do you have other concerns?”
⚠️ Do not overpromise
Do not create expectations that you cannot fulfill. You may say that concerns can be raised with the resident or consultant, but do not promise a specific outcome.

Patient Perspective & Questioning

Understand what the illness means to the patient, then move from broad narrative to specific clarification.

Pages 3–4

Explore four dimensions of the patient’s perspective

Feelings
Fear, worry, or other concerns about the problem.
Ideas
What the patient thinks the nature or cause of the problem is, including environmental influences.
Function
How the problem affects life and daily functioning.
Expectations
What the patient expects from the disease, clinician, or healthcare, often shaped by prior personal or family experiences.
🧠 Clinical reasoning cue
Process the information through symptom analysis and think about the most likely organ system involved, while keeping the patient’s beliefs and context visible.

Interview approach: broad to narrow

  1. Move from open-ended to focused questions.
  2. Use questions that elicit a graded response.
  3. Ask a series of questions one at a time.
  4. Offer multiple choices when helpful.
  5. Clarify what the patient means.
  6. Use echoing.
⭐ Must know
Start by hearing the story in the patient’s own words. Then obtain the seven symptom features. Finally, use yes/no questions for pertinent positives and negatives.

Non-focusing versus focusing questioning skills

Non-focusing

  • Silence: listen quietly; silence is acceptable but should not be prolonged without follow-through.
  • Nonverbal encouragement: show attentive listening through facial expression and gesture.
  • Neutral utterances: “Okay… Ano pa po?... Sige pa po…”

Focusing

  • Direct the patient toward a symptom or topic already mentioned.
  • Echoing: repeat a word or phrase to prompt elaboration.
  • Open-ended requests: directly invite more information.
  • Summarizing/paraphrasing: verify accuracy or refocus.
Core analytic method

Symptom Analysis

Characterize each important symptom, preserve timing, and continuously test diagnostic possibilities.

Pages 4–5, 9

The seven attributes of a symptom

Location
Where is it? Does it radiate?
Quality
What is it like?
Quantity / severity
How bad is it? For pain, use a 1–10 rating.
Timing
When did it start? How long does it last? How often does it occur?
Setting
Environmental factors, activities, emotions, or other circumstances contributing to illness.
Remitting / exacerbating factors
What makes it better or worse?
Associated manifestations
What else accompanies the symptom?

PQRSTU mnemonic as presented in the lecture

P — Provocative / Palliative
What worsens it? What makes it better?
Q — Quality / Quantity
What does it feel like? How bad is it? Compare with prior episodes and functional effect.
R — Region / Radiation
Where is it? Does it move or occur elsewhere?
T — Treatment
What was taken or tried? Was consultation sought? What happened afterward?
U — Understanding
What does the patient think is causing the symptom?

Purpose and continuous differential diagnosis

  • Use symptom analysis to formulate a diagnostic hypothesis.
  • Reassess whether this is the patient’s only problem each time you return.
  • Keep alternatives ready when the initial assessment is not supported by diagnostic workup.
“If plan A doesn’t happen, how do we go about plan B?”

Onset: date, manner, and contributing factors

  • Use the day of admission or consultation as the reference date.
  • State onset relative to that date: minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, or years prior.
  • Record the temporal order and relationship of events.
  • Describe the manner as gradual or sudden; this gives a clue to the tempo of the pathologic process.
  • Identify precipitating and predisposing factors that precede or coincide with onset.
⭐ Timing changes interpretation
The lecture contrasts fever → abdominal pain → jaundice with abdominal pain → jaundice → fever. The components are similar, but the sequence differs.

After the cardinal features: inquire within the same body system

After obtaining the relevant cardinal features, perform a focused ROS for that system to determine which related symptoms are present and which are absent.

CLITAA and course since onset

Characteristic — CLITAA

  1. Character: quantity, quality, consistency, appearance
  2. Location and radiation
  3. Intensity or severity
  4. Timing: continuous/intermittent, duration, temporal relationship
  5. Aggravating and relieving factors
  6. Associated symptoms

Course since onset

  1. Incidence: single acute attack, recurrent acute attacks, or daily occurrences
  2. Effect of therapy
  3. Progress

End-of-tab recap

For every major symptom, establish onset → full characteristics → associated manifestations → course since onset, while preserving chronology and testing related symptoms in the same system.
Techniques of skilled interviewing

Listening, Clarifying, Responding & Transitioning

Use verbal and nonverbal methods to obtain accurate details while keeping the patient comfortable and the interview organized.

Pages 5–6

Active listening

  • Listen with intent.
  • Observe gestures and other nonverbal cues.
  • Connect with the patient’s emotional state.
  • Use verbal and nonverbal encouragement so the patient can expand on feelings.

Adaptive questioning options

  • General to specific: “Tell me more about your chest discomfort” → “What else?” → “Where?” → “Show me.”
  • Graded response: ask how many steps can be climbed before shortness of breath rather than only whether it occurs.
  • Ask questions one at a time.
  • Offer multiple choices when the patient cannot recall.
  • Clarify exact meaning: “What do you mean by the flu?”

Nonverbal communication

Hand gestures can encourage fuller answers and show that the interviewer is actively listening.

Empathic responses

Put yourself in the patient’s position, but do not assume you already know what the patient’s feelings mean. Ask directly how the patient felt or feels now.

Validation

Recognize the patient’s response and feelings as legitimate and understandable.

“That accident must have been scary.”

Reassurance: timing matters

Patients need reassurance, but first identify and acknowledge their feelings. For late-stage or end-stage illness, do not offer false reassurance.

⚠️ Do not say
Do not tell a patient with known stage IV cancer that everything will be all right or “Gagaling ka, huwag kang mag-alala.”

Facilitation

  • Guide the patient to the next expected questions.
  • Express commitment to an ongoing relationship.
  • Help the patient feel that care will continue.
  • Encourage speech with “Mm-hmm,” “Go on,” or “I’m listening.”

Summarization

Shows that you have listened, clarifies what is known and unknown, organizes clinical reasoning, and allows correction.

“So tama po ba, nasa right knee niyo iyong masakit?” “Mayroon po bang iba?”

Echoing

Repeat the patient’s own word or phrase to encourage elaboration.

Highlighting transitions

Tell the patient when you are changing direction so the shift is clear and reassuring.

Transition example

“We’ll talk about your ankle pain later. We’ll discuss first what is happening to your headache.”

The interviewer controls the interaction without being abrupt, preventing one topic from consuming the entire interview.

Adapting without judgment

Specific Interview Situations

Remain neutral and adapt the method to communication, capacity, behavior, and literacy needs.

Page 6
The silent patient
  • Direct questions to a bedside relative when needed.
  • Consider whether silence reflects the way questions are being asked.
  • Ask whether you have done something upsetting and make the patient comfortable.
  • Watch closely for nonverbal cues.
The confusing patient
  • Ask a relative for help.
  • If there is flight of ideas, bring the patient back to the disease in question.
  • Use skilled interviewing to redirect focus.
  • For multiple confusing symptoms, focus on context, the patient’s perspective, and psychosocial assessment.
Impaired decision-making capacity
  • Determine whether the patient has decision-making capacity.
  • Find a surrogate informant or decision maker, such as a spouse or family member.
  • For intubated or otherwise limited patients, family can help provide the history.
The talkative patient
  • Interrupt courteously, not rudely.
  • Stay with the problem at hand.
  • Uncontrolled talk may cause important information to be missed because one problem dominates.
The angry or disruptive patient
  • Do not engage in confrontation; step back and assess the reason for anger.
  • Avoid being strong or controlling.
  • Accept angry feelings without becoming angry in return.
  • Ask whether it is a bad time or whether you should return later.
Language barrier
  • An interpreter is often needed.
  • Family interpretation may threaten confidentiality, distort meaning, or be incomplete.
  • Use simple questions; fully open-ended questions may not be feasible.
Low literacy
  • Be patient and avoid complex medical terminology.
  • Assess ability to read before providing written examples.
  • Explore whether difficulty is due to language, a learning disorder, poor vision, or education level.
  • Low health literacy may contribute to poor health.
Deaf or hard-of-hearing
  • Ask relatives for assistance when appropriate.
  • Do not shout.
  • Determine the patient’s preferred communication method.
Blind patient
  • Proceed with the interview.
  • Guide the patient during the physical examination.
  • Nonverbal communication remains important for trust despite visual disability.
Limited intelligence
  • For severe intellectual limitation, turn to family or caregivers for history while first showing interest in the patient.
  • Pay attention to school record and ability to function independently.
Patient seeking personal advice
  • Remain professional.
  • Reserve responses until after discussing signs and symptoms.
  • The lecture instructs: never give advice regarding personal problems.
Special communication skills

Sensitive Topics

The single overriding rule is to remain nonjudgmental, protect privacy, and respond with empathy.

Pages 6–7
⚠️ Single most important rule
Never be judgmental.

Sexual history

  • Establish a good relationship before sensitive questioning.
  • Clarify who is at the bedside and how close that person is to the patient.
  • Explain that upcoming questions are private or sensitive.
  • Ask whether the patient permits the companion to remain; if not, ask the companion to leave.
  • Use a private setting and do not assume a companion can answer.
  • Reassure the patient that these questions are asked routinely.
  • Use an orienting sentence explaining why the questions are necessary.
  • Use specific, understandable language rather than vague euphemisms.
“To assess your risk for various diseases, I need to ask you some questions about your sexual health and practices.”

Mental health

Be sensitive to reports of mood changes or symptoms.

Alcohol and drug abuse

  • Questions naturally follow caffeine and cigarette use.
  • Ask about current and past use, patterns, and family history.
C — Cutting down
A — Annoyance when criticized
G — Guilty feelings
E — Eye-openers

Family violence and abuse

  • Stay discreet.
  • If violence or abuse is suspected, tell a senior or consultant; do not directly confront the family.
  • After disclosure, use empathic validation and nonjudgmental responses.
  • Spend part of the encounter alone with the patient when physical or sexual abuse is suspected.
  • If the patient resists, do not force the situation.
  • Ask parents about discipline to screen for child abuse.
“Are you in a relationship where you have been hit or threatened?”

Death and dying

  • Dying patients may not want to discuss illness at every encounter or with every person.
  • Offer opportunities to talk and listen receptively.
  • If the patient remains at a social level, respect that preference.
  • Palliative care handles this sensitive area.

Patient profile: what it reveals

The profile reveals the individual as a whole—personality, mental make-up, reaction to the environment and illness, daily routine, habits, resources, and identifying information.

🧠 Why it matters
The profile may reveal environmental causes, factors that influence diagnostic or therapeutic planning, and practical limits such as financial resources. It can guide selection of the most specific and sensitive tests or imaging.

20 cigarettes = 1 pack

For alcohol, ask the type, amount, and timing of the last drink.

Home life and sexuality

  • Support systems and household contacts
  • Partners and risk for STIs
  • Sexual concerns

Source and reliability

The source may be the patient or relatives. Grade reliability as poor, fair, or good; a percentage may also be used.

Chief complaint: rules

  • The primary reason for consultation and a measure of the patient’s estimate of illness.
  • Keep it a brief statement, usually one symptom or at most two.
  • Use the patient’s own words as nearly as possible, translating Filipino into English for documentation.
  • State a concrete complaint, not a treatment or circumstance such as “admitted for chemotherapy.”
  • Include duration.
  • Avoid diagnostic terms or diseases.

Appropriate

“Knee pain, 3 weeks.”

Not appropriate

“Admitted for chemotherapy” without identifying the symptom or reason for treatment.

Chronology plus complete symptom analysis

Building the History of Present Illness

The HPI converts the patient’s story into an organized, chronological clinical account.

Pages 8–9

Four components of the written HPI

Restate and elaborate the chief complaint. Integrate relevant patient-profile context when useful.
History from the time of onset. Describe the beginning of the present problem.
Full current status and course of events. What happened afterward? What did the patient do? Was consultation sought? Were medications taken?
Summarize all significant positive and negative information.

Paragraphing and reference date

⭐ Must know
Use a separate paragraph for each chronological period. In each paragraph, analyze all symptoms completely and keep related positive and negative findings close to the symptom being described.
  • Place other significant positives and negatives in the final paragraph.
  • Use the day of consultation or admission as the time reference.
  • State onset and progression as minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, or years prior.

Four phases of building the HPI

Obtain the patient’s own account. Use open-ended neutral questions and avoid bias.
Analyze symptoms in detail. Use closed-neutral and selected direct questions in chronological order.
Test diagnostic possibilities. Ask about other symptoms or events usually associated with the suspected problem.
Use the ROS to reveal additional symptoms. Analyze symptoms first discovered there, including remote or apparently unrelated findings.

Phase 1 prompts

“Can you tell me when and how your problem started?”
“Tell me about your problem.”
“What other symptoms did you notice since you became ill?”
⚠️ Phase 1 trap
Do not begin with direct, closed questions. Start with open-ended neutral questions so the patient can speak freely.
Sample patient record: four-paragraph structure
Opening / elaborated complaint: episodic vomiting of bright red blood during the last 3 hours, associated with mid-epigastric pain.
Three years prior: gradual burning mid-epigastric pain precipitated by hunger and work stress, without radiation; initially mild, later moderate and sometimes waking the patient. Hunger and sometimes eating aggravated it; spicy food worsened it. A black-tarry stool episode with weakness occurred six months prior, and upper GI radiography showed an ulcer. Pain became more intense but was relieved by antacids and food, then recurred one week prior with greater frequency and intensity and only slight relief.
Three hours prior: with persistent epigastric pain, the patient suddenly vomited bright-red blood three times, about 1/2 to 1 cup each, 30 minutes to 1 hour apart; the last episode was 15 minutes before ER arrival. No melena at that time, but weakness and slight giddiness on standing were present.
Significant positives and negatives: no history of liver disease, jaundice, bleeding tendency, alcohol or aspirin ingestion; occasional smoking and recent work stress; no prior weakness, easy fatigability, exertional dyspnea, or weight loss.

The six-point checklist for HPI

The lecture states that a thorough and accurate checklist can have a predictive diagnostic value of 85% or higher.

  1. Are all components present—signs and symptoms?
  2. Is the sequence correct?
  3. Are the temporal relationships between problems and symptoms correct?
  4. Have all symptoms and components been analyzed?
  5. Are there any time holes?
  6. Was there a thorough review for symptoms that complete the HPI?
⚠️ Time-hole trap
Do not jump from an onset eight months ago directly to admission. Account for what happened in the intervening periods: gone, better, worse, recurrent, treated, or unchanged.

Review of Systems

  • A head-to-toe survey by organ system.
  • Uncovers symptoms not elicited earlier.
  • Checks for overlooked symptoms of the present illness.
  • Detects symptoms of other disease unrelated to the HPI.
  • Helps plan later action after the current complaint is managed.
⭐ Must know
Symptoms first revealed during ROS should not automatically be omitted. They may complete the HPI or identify another condition requiring attention.

Focused review for HPI versus formal ROS

The HPI checklist includes a symptom review used to complete the current illness and identify involved systems. The lecture notes that this is different from the formal Review of Systems section, which is a systematic head-to-toe survey and also detects unrelated problems.

Graphic representation of the HPI

Temporal Profile Diagram

Show when components appear, how they overlap, and how their intensity changes through the illness.

Pages 10–11

Purpose and placement

  • Follows the Review of Systems and comes before the physical examination record.
  • Represents components across the time frame of the present illness.
  • Shows the extent to which components overlap.
  • Severity or intensity is represented with an upward stroke.
🧠 Forest and trees
The temporal profile shows the overall course—the “forest”—and each symptom or event—the “individual trees.”

Five construction guidelines

  1. Use 1–2 symptom lines, with a maximum of 3, when possible. A cluttered diagram defeats the purpose.
  2. If several symptoms share a similar profile, use one line to represent them.
  3. Lines may be color-coded or represented with different symbols.
  4. Use separate diagrams for separate problems or illnesses in the HPI.
  5. Curve height, shape, slope, and sharpness depict severity, acuteness, and tempo.

Temporal pattern library

Gradual onset, progressively worsening
Acute onset, unrelenting
Intermittent
Continuous, remittent
Acute episodes: remittent + periodic
Recurrent

Total episode repeats at intervals of hours or days; the lecture gives afternoon fever every 2–3 days as an example.

Periodic episode

Occurs in clusters of days, weeks, or months, with a symptom-free period longer than the symptomatic period.

Gradual, continuous, waxing and waning

Seven-day timeline with three components

  • Example sequence: RUQ pain → jaundice → fever.
  • A symptom curve touching the baseline “0” or clinical horizon is interpreted as absent.
  • Symptom lines are curves, not sharp angles.
  • The overlap and sequence may distinguish causes even when the same components are present.

On and off, progressively worsening to 2 days prior to admission and continuing to admission.
Jaundice

Fever
Noted 2 days prior and lasting until admission.
Data type and assessment scope

Medical Data & Assessment Types

Separate what the patient reports from what the clinician detects, then match the scope of assessment to the situation.

Page 11

Subjective versus objective data

Subjective data

What the patient tells you.

Includes symptoms and history from chief complaint through review of systems.

A 54-year-old hairdresser reports left chest pressure “like an elephant is sitting there,” radiating to the left neck and arm.

Objective data

What you detect.

Includes physical examination, laboratory, and test findings.

Older overweight woman; height 5’4”, weight 150 lb, BMI 26, BP 160/80, HR 96 regular, RR 24, temperature 97.5°F.

Comprehensive assessment

  • Appropriate for new patients in the office or hospital.
  • Provides fundamental and personalized knowledge.
  • Strengthens the clinician–patient relationship.
  • Helps identify or rule out physical causes of concerns.
  • Provides a baseline for future assessment.
  • Creates a platform for health promotion, education, and counseling.
  • Develops proficiency in physical examination skills.

Focused assessment

  • Appropriate for established patients, especially routine or urgent care visits.
  • Addresses focused concerns or symptoms.
  • Restricts symptom review to a specific body system.
  • Uses examination methods relevant to the problem as thoroughly and carefully as possible.

Lecture key points

  • Use the appropriate approach and technique to obtain history.
  • Communication and listening skills are essential.
  • Complete history-taking includes symptom analysis and continuous reassessment of the differential diagnosis.
  • The temporal profile is the graphic representation of the HPI in the prescribed medical-information format.
Do not confuse

Whole-Lesson Comparisons

Lecture-supported distinctions that are easy to mix up during interviews, documentation, and exams.

Pages 1–11

History versus physical examination

Medical history

Subjective information from the patient or informant.

Physical examination

Objective findings obtained through examination techniques and positioning.

Chief complaint versus HPI

Chief complaint

Brief, concrete symptom statement, usually one or two symptoms, in the patient’s words as nearly as possible, with duration; avoid diagnoses.

HPI

Chronological elaboration from onset through current status, including complete symptom analysis and significant positives and negatives.

Focused symptom review versus formal ROS

Focused review within HPI

Targets symptoms in the same system or tests a suspected diagnosis.

Formal ROS

Systematic head-to-toe survey that also detects unrelated symptoms or disease.

Neutral, direct, leading, loaded

  • Neutral/open-ended: invites narrative without bias.
  • Direct: faster and specific, but may overwhelm.
  • Leading: establishes bias.
  • Loaded: usually avoided; may be used to observe reaction.

Empathy, validation, reassurance

  • Empathy: connect with and explore the patient’s emotional experience without assuming its meaning.
  • Validation: recognize feelings as legitimate and understandable.
  • Reassurance: support the patient, but do not use premature or false reassurance—especially in late-stage disease.

Intermittent, recurrent, periodic

  • Intermittent: alternately occurs and ceases within the course.
  • Recurrent: the total episode repeats at intervals such as hours or days.
  • Periodic: clustered episodes over days/weeks/months with a symptom-free period longer than the symptomatic period.

Comprehensive versus focused assessment

Comprehensive

New patients; broad baseline, relationship building, prevention and counseling.

Focused

Established patients or urgent/routine visits; specific concern or system.

Similar components, different sequence

Fever → abdominal pain → jaundice

The lecture says this sequence should prompt consideration of viral hepatitis.

Abdominal pain → jaundice → fever

The lecture says this sequence should prompt consideration of gallbladder stone with cholecystitis.

⭐ Key differentiator
The symptoms may be the same, but their temporal order changes the diagnostic interpretation.

5. Skilled interviewing

  • Active listening and nonverbal observation

6. Safety and sensitivity traps

  • Never be judgmental.
  • Protect privacy for sexual history and abuse screening.
  • Do not confront an angry patient or suspected abusive family directly.
  • Do not shout at hard-of-hearing patients.
  • Do not use false reassurance in late-stage disease.
  • Do not overpromise outcomes you cannot deliver.
