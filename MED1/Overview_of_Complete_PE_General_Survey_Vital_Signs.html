Overview

A whole-lesson orientation only: why physical examination matters, how to prepare, and how the complete adult examination flows from first impression to focused system assessment.

WHOLE LECTURE · PP. 1–8

The lecture’s central idea

Before laboratory results, imaging, or an ECG are available, the clinician’s earliest diagnostic tool is the ability to look carefully, obtain vital signs correctly, and examine systematically. In the first minutes, the clinician must decide whether the patient is sick or not sick, stable or unstable, able to wait, or in need of immediate escalation.

Must Know
Physical examination is more than a checklist. It supports early recognition of illness, differential diagnosis, and the next appropriate step in management. Technology confirms what the clinical examination first helps you suspect.

Whole-lesson must-knows

  • Patient dignity and comfort are prioritized throughout the encounter.
  • Hand hygiene is the single most important measure to prevent spread of infection.
  • Tangential lighting reveals subtle contours, depressions, and pulsations better than diffuse lighting.
  • Korotkoff sounds are relatively low-pitched and are generally better heard with the bell.
  • Orthostatic hypotension: fall in systolic BP ≥20 mmHg or diastolic BP ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing.
  • Posterior chest gives access to most lung fields, especially the lower lobes.
  • PMI: left 5th intercostal space at the midclavicular line.

Clinical Approach

The examination starts with the clinician’s presence: professional demeanor, clear communication, careful observation, and respect for the patient as a whole person.

PAGE 1

Why the examination matters

In a crowded emergency setting, the clinician may initially have no laboratory data, CT scan, ultrasound, or ECG. The general survey and vital signs can immediately answer:

  • Does the patient look sick or not sick?
  • Is the patient stable or unstable?
  • Can the patient wait, or is immediate action needed?
  • Should a senior or resident be called right away?
Clinical Cue
Look for altered mental status, rapid breathing, hypotension, tachycardia, and a toxic appearance. The lecture emphasizes that these early observations may determine the patient’s outcome.

Master the normal sequence first

A normal, systematic examination is the foundation for detecting subtle abnormalities. The goal is not merely to memorize values or pass an examination, but to build:

  • Clinical reasoning
  • The habit of careful observation
  • A reliable, repeatable head-to-toe sequence
  • The ability to notice what others may miss

Use the same logical order every time. When the sequence becomes second nature, the examiner is less likely to overlook findings.

Reflect on your approach to the patient

Introduce yourself clearly

Greet the patient respectfully and identify your role as a medical student, clinical clerk, or intern. This establishes trust and reduces uncertainty.

Remain calm, confident, and organized

Even when inexperienced, a composed approach reassures the patient and helps the examination proceed smoothly. The lecture cautions against making beginner anxiety obvious.

Explain extra time or unfamiliar maneuvers

Beginners may take longer. Explain this beforehand so prolonged auscultation or repeated inspection does not make the patient think something is wrong.

Be systematic but flexible

Forgetting an area is common. Examine it later, even if out of sequence. Patients value a thorough examination more than a perfectly memorized order.

Observe the whole patient continuously

Watch how the patient looks, moves, speaks, and responds. Notice wincing, worried glances, unusual posture, or other nonverbal distress.

Skilled Clinician
Be thorough without wasting time, systematic but flexible, and gentle while willing to cause necessary temporary discomfort when clinically required. Explain why a potentially uncomfortable maneuver is needed.
Caution for Beginners
Avoid prematurely interpreting or discussing examination findings. The early goal is to master technique. Questions or uncertain findings should be discussed with a resident or consultant before disclosure.

Responding to patient anxiety

Patients and relatives may feel anxious because the patient is vulnerable, physically exposed, concerned about pain, or worried about what the clinician may find. Professionalism, respect, empathy, explanations, and reassurance can make a major difference.

When an alarming abnormality is seen

Do not show disgust, alarm, or another negative reaction when encountering a serious or disturbing finding such as an ominous mass or deep ulceration. Maintain composure, protect dignity, and seek appropriate supervision.

Doctor–Patient Relationship
The examination is not only a way to gather information. It is also an opportunity to strengthen trust through genuine attention, appropriate reassurance, and concern for the patient’s health.

Setup & Comfort

Prepare the environment, equipment, positioning, consent, privacy, and draping before moving into the hands-on examination.

PAGES 1–3

Lighting and environment

  • Use good lighting for visual inspection.
  • Maintain a quiet environment for auscultation.
  • Adjust the bed to a convenient examination height, then return it to a low, safe position.
  • Ask the patient to move closer, turn, or change position when it improves access.
  • When ideal conditions are not available, make the best adjustment possible; for example, politely request that the television volume be lowered and thank the patient afterward.

Stethoscope characteristics

  • Ear tips should fit snugly and painlessly. Select the correct size, align the earpieces with the ear canals, and adjust the metal spring to comfortable tightness.
  • Use thick-walled tubing that is as short as feasible: about 30 cm (12 in) if possible and not longer than 38 cm (15 in).
  • The stethoscope should have a bell and diaphragm with a reliable changeover mechanism.
Lighting Recall
Diffuse/perpendicular = general inspection.
Tangential = subtle surface contour, elevation, depression, or pulsation.

Protecting privacy and comfort

Principles of draping

  • Expose only the body area currently being examined.
  • Keep the rest of the body covered with a gown or drape.
  • Explain each step before uncovering sensitive areas.
  • Re-drape immediately after each body region.

Draping examples from the lecture

  • Posterior thorax: untie the gown in back while keeping the patient otherwise covered.
  • Breasts: uncover one breast at a time; re-drape before moving to the other side.
  • Abdomen: expose only the abdomen; cover the chest and place the drape at the inguinal level.
  • Inguinal/perineal: explain the reason and obtain permission before proceeding.

Infection Control

Standard and universal precautions protect patients and healthcare workers; transmission-based precautions add targeted measures for specific routes of spread.

PAGES 3–4

Standard precautions

Assume that all blood, body fluids, secretions, excretions except sweat, nonintact skin, and mucous membranes may contain transmissible agents. These precautions apply to all patients in all healthcare settings, regardless of known infection status.

  • Proper hand hygiene
  • Personal protective equipment: gloves, gowns, mouth/nose protection, eye protection
  • Safe injection practices
  • Safe handling of contaminated equipment and surfaces
  • Respiratory hygiene and cough etiquette
  • Patient isolation criteria
  • Safe handling of equipment, toys, surfaces, and laundry

Universal precautions

Designed to prevent parenteral, mucous membrane, and noncontact exposure of healthcare workers to bloodborne pathogens, including HIV and HBV.

Potentially infectious fluids: blood; blood-contaminated body fluids; semen; vaginal secretions; and sterile body fluids such as cerebrospinal, synovial, pleural, pericardial, and amniotic fluid.

  • Use gloves, gowns, aprons, masks, and protective eyewear as appropriate.
  • Follow safe injection and sharp-instrument practices.
  • Report needlestick, sharp injury, or exposure immediately to the appropriate health service.
  • HBV vaccination is strongly recommended for healthcare workers at risk of blood exposure.

CDC recommendations for hand hygiene

Hand hygiene is the single most important measure to prevent the spread of infection.

  • Before touching a patient, even when gloves are worn
  • Before leaving the care area after touching the patient or immediate environment
  • After contact with blood, body fluids, excretions, or wound dressings
  • Before an aseptic task such as placing an IV or preparing an injection
  • When moving from a contaminated body site to a clean body site
  • After glove removal
Method
Use soap and water when hands are visibly soiled or after caring for suspected/known infectious diarrhea such as Clostridium difficile or norovirus. Otherwise, an alcohol-based hand rub is preferred because it is fast and convenient.

Proper hand hygiene reduces transmission of multidrug-resistant organisms, particularly MRSA and VRE.

Exposure Warning
Any needlestick, sharp-instrument injury, or exposure to potentially infected material must be reported immediately for appropriate evaluation and treatment.

Techniques & Sequence

Choose the scope, sequence, and patient position before beginning. Use the cardinal techniques in an organized, comfortable, and efficient head-to-toe flow.

Goals of the examination sequence

  1. Maximize patient comfort.
  2. Avoid unnecessary position changes.
  3. Enhance clinical efficiency.

Move generally from head to toe using the same logical order each time. Position changes should occur only when necessary so that the examination remains organized and important findings are not overlooked.

Specific positions

  • Lithotomy: female pelvic and rectal examination; supine with hips flexed, abducted, externally rotated, and knees flexed.
  • Left lateral decubitus: male prostate or rectal examination.
Figure 2. Suggested sequence and positioning. P. 5

Examine from the patient’s right side

The right side is the standard position because it is easier and more consistent.

  • Jugular venous pressure estimates are more reliable.
  • The palpating hand rests more comfortably on the apical impulse.
  • The right kidney is more often palpable than the left.
  • Examination tables often accommodate a right-handed approach.

Left-handed students are encouraged to adopt the right-sided position while still using the left hand for percussion or instruments when useful.

Patient at bedrest

When the patient cannot sit or stand, modify the sequence:

  1. Examine the head, neck, and anterior chest with the patient supine.
  2. Roll the patient to each side to listen to the lungs, examine the back, and inspect the skin.
  3. Return the patient to supine and complete the remaining examination.

General Survey

Observation begins at first contact and continues through the history and examination. The goal is to describe the patient’s distinguishing features clearly and clinically.

PAGES 5–6

What to observe

  • General state of health, height, weight, build, and sexual development
  • Posture, motor activity, and gait
  • Dress, grooming, and personal hygiene
  • Body or breath odors
  • Facial expression, manner, affect, and reaction to people and the environment
  • Speech and level of awareness or consciousness
Goal
Describe the patient so clearly that colleagues could spot the patient in a crowd of strangers.

General appearance checklist

  • Apparent state of health: acutely or chronically ill, frail, fit, or robust
  • Level of consciousness
  • Signs of distress: chest clutching, pallor, diaphoresis, labored breathing, wheezing, cough, wincing, guarding, grimacing, or unusual posture
  • Skin color and obvious lesions
  • Dress, grooming, and hygiene: suitability, cleanliness, jewelry, body piercings
  • Facial expression and odors
  • Posture, gait, motor activity, height, and weight

Body mass index

BMI is an estimate of nutritional status and helps identify patients who are underweight, overweight, or obese. The lecture notes that examination questions may use either WHO or Asia-Pacific classification, with Asia-Pacific often more applicable in clinical settings.

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m²) or Weight (lb) × 700 ÷ Height² (in²) The lecture figure notes that some organizations use 704.5; the variation is described as negligible.
Lecture-Specific Note
The lecture states that Asians are at higher risk of obesity-related metabolic disease at lower BMI because of a tendency to accumulate more visceral fat, supporting lower Asia-Pacific obesity thresholds.

Vital Signs

Vital signs provide critical initial information and often determine the tempo and direction of evaluation. Always record the units and recheck abnormal values yourself.

PAGE 6

Blood pressure technique

Position correctly

Place the cuff and arm at heart level.

Estimate systolic pressure by palpation

Then inflate the cuff to 30 mmHg above the estimated value.

Place the bell over the brachial artery

Korotkoff sounds are relatively low-pitched and are generally better heard with the bell.

Deflate slowly

Deflate at about 2–3 mmHg per second.

Identify systolic and diastolic pressure

At least two consecutive tapping sounds mark systolic pressure. The disappearance point gives the best estimate of diastolic pressure.

Figure 6. Korotkoff sounds. P. 6

What the sounds represent

  • Systolic pressure: first appearance of at least two consecutive tapping sounds; the artery is compressed and turbulent blood flow is audible.
  • Diastolic pressure: disappearance of the sounds, usually a few mmHg below muffling; the artery is no longer compressed and free flow is inaudible.

Orthostatic hypotension

Measure blood pressure and heart rate in two positions:

  1. Supine after resting for 3–10 minutes.
  2. Standing, reassessed within 3 minutes.
SBP fall ≥20 mmHg OR DBP fall ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes

Useful in patients with dizziness, lightheadedness, or fainting upon standing.

Heart rate

  • Radial pulse is commonly used.
  • Count for 1 minute by radial palpation or apical auscultation.
  • If regular and apparently normal, count 30 seconds × 2.
  • If unusually fast or slow, count for 60 seconds.
  • The lecture gives the usual normal range as 60 to 90 to 100 beats/min.

Respiratory rate

  • Count by visual inspection or by subtly listening over the trachea.
  • Count quietly so the patient does not consciously alter breathing.
  • One technique is to keep palpating the radial pulse while counting respirations.

Body temperature

Choose the site based on the patient and available equipment. Sites listed in the lecture:

  • Oral
  • Rectal
  • Axillary
  • Tympanic membrane
  • Temporal
At the Start of the Encounter
If nurses have already recorded the vital signs, review them promptly. When values are abnormal, retake them yourself during the visit.

Skin & HEENT

Continue observation while moving through skin, hair, nails, head, eyes, ears, nose, sinuses, mouth, and pharynx.

The skin can provide clues to local and systemic disease. Continue assessing it as other body regions are exposed and examined.

Head

Examine the hair, scalp, skull, and face. Look for symmetry, deformities, tenderness, masses, abnormal hair distribution, and alopecia.

Eyes

  • Check visual acuity and screen visual fields.
  • Note position and alignment of the eyes.
  • Inspect eyelids, sclera, and conjunctiva.
  • Use oblique lighting for cornea, iris, and lens.
  • Compare pupils and test light reactions.
  • Assess extraocular movements and inspect the fundi with an ophthalmoscope.
  • Darken the room for ophthalmoscopy to promote pupillary dilation and improve the retinal view.

Ears and hearing tests

  • Inspect auricles, canals, and tympanic membranes.
  • Check auditory acuity.
  • If diminished, use Weber for lateralization and Rinne to compare air and bone conduction.

Nose and sinuses

  • Examine the external nose.
  • Use a light and nasal speculum to inspect the nasal mucosa, septum, and turbinates.
  • Palpate the frontal and maxillary sinuses for tenderness.
Clinical Cue
Frontal or maxillary sinus tenderness may suggest inflammation or infection such as sinusitis.

Mouth and pharynx

  • Inspect the lips, oral mucosa, gums, teeth, tongue, palate, tonsils, and pharynx.
  • Look for ulcers, exudates, or other abnormalities.
  • Cranial-nerve assessment may be incorporated here, including tongue movement, palate elevation, and gag reflex.

Neck, Back & Lungs

Move behind the sitting patient for the thyroid, posterior thorax, and lungs, then complete the anterior chest using systematic inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation.

PAGE 7

Neck

  • Inspect and palpate cervical lymph nodes.
  • Note masses or unusual pulsations.
  • Assess for tracheal deviation.
  • Observe the sound and effort of breathing.
  • Inspect and palpate the thyroid gland for enlargement, lobules, or tenderness.

Back

  • Inspect and palpate the spine and back muscles.
  • Look for spinal deformity, tenderness, or muscle spasm.
  • Compare shoulder height for symmetry, which may indicate an underlying musculoskeletal issue.

Posterior chest and lungs

The posterior chest gives access to most lung fields, especially the lower lobes, which occupy much of the back.

  1. Inspect and palpate the upper back and assess symmetry and expansion.
  2. Inspect, palpate, and percuss the posterior chest.
  3. Identify diaphragmatic dullness on each side.
  4. Auscultate breath sounds, added sounds, and transmitted voice sounds when indicated.
  5. Move in a left-right ladder pattern down the chest and back up, using 7 posterior auscultatory points.
Clinical Cue
Crackles are associated with pneumonia in the lecture.
Clinical Cue
Wheezes are associated with asthma or COPD in the lecture.
Figure 8. Posterior chest auscultation pattern. P. 7

Anterior chest and lungs

  1. Inspect, palpate, and percuss the anterior chest.
  2. Assess chest symmetry, respiratory effort, expansion, and tenderness.
  3. Identify the level of diaphragmatic dullness on each side.
  4. Listen for breath sounds, adventitious sounds, and transmitted voice sounds when indicated.

Use a ladder pattern with 6 anterior auscultatory points. The anterior chest mainly assesses the upper and middle lobes; lower-lung pathology is generally best heard posteriorly.

Figure 9. Anterior chest auscultation pattern. P. 7

Breast, Nodes & Cardiovascular

Examine breast and nodal regions with proper exposure, then assess jugular venous pulsation, carotids, precordium, PMI, heart sounds, and murmurs in the appropriate positions.

PAGE 7

Breasts, axillae, and epitrochlear nodes

  • For a woman, inspect the breasts with arms relaxed, elevated, and pressed on the hips.
  • Expose one breast at a time. For example, while examining the left breast, the patient may raise the left arm while the other side remains draped.
  • In either sex, inspect the axillae and palpate the axillary nodes.
  • Palpate the epitrochlear nodes, which may enlarge in systemic or hematologic disease.
Figure 10. Axillary and epitrochlear nodes. P. 7

Cardiovascular examination: positioning and sequence

Head of bed ~30°

Adjust as needed to see jugular venous pulsations.

Left lateral position

Listen at the apex for an S3 or mitral stenosis.

Sit, lean forward, exhale

Listen for the murmur of aortic regurgitation.

  • Observe jugular venous pulsations and measure jugular venous pressure relative to the sternal angle.
  • Inspect and palpate carotid pulsations; listen for carotid bruits.
  • Inspect and palpate the precordium.

Apical impulse / PMI

Note the location, diameter, amplitude, and duration of the apical impulse.

PMI = Left 5th intercostal space at the midclavicular lineThis is where the left ventricle comes closest to the chest wall during systole.
  • A displaced PMI may suggest left ventricular hypertrophy or cardiomegaly.
  • If difficult to palpate, place the patient in the left lateral decubitus position to bring the left ventricle closer to the chest wall.
Figure 11. Left lateral decubitus position. P. 7

Cardiac auscultation

Use the diaphragm at the four valve areas remembered as “APTM” or “A Patient Trusts Me”:

  1. Aortic
  2. Pulmonic
  3. Tricuspid
  4. Mitral

Then listen at the apex and lower sternal border with the bell. Identify S1, S2, physiologic splitting of S2, extra sounds, and murmurs.

Figure 12. Precordial auscultatory areas. P. 7

Abdomen & Lower Extremities

Return the bed to flat for the abdominal examination, then assess peripheral vascular, musculoskeletal, and nervous systems with the patient supine and standing.

PAGE 8

Abdomen

  1. Lower the head of bed; the patient is supine.
  2. Use IAPP: Inspect → Auscultate → Percuss → Palpate.
  3. Palpate lightly, then deeply.
  4. Assess liver and spleen by percussion and then palpation.
  5. Try to feel the kidneys; palpate the aorta and its pulsations.
  6. If kidney infection is suspected, percuss the costovertebral angles.
Kidney Punch Test
A positive tenderness response may suggest kidney infection such as pyelonephritis.
Exam Trap
Do not use routine IPPA for the abdomen. Auscultation comes before percussion and palpation because manipulating the abdominal contents may interfere with auscultatory or percussion findings.

  • Obtain a Pap smear.
  • Palpate the uterus and adnexa bimanually.
  • Perform rectal examination if indicated.

FINAL REVIEW

First principles

  • PE begins when you meet the patient.
  • Ask: sick/not sick, stable/unstable, wait/act, call senior?
  • Master the normal sequence first.
  • Be thorough, systematic, flexible, gentle.
  • Do not prematurely interpret findings as a beginner.

Prepare & protect

  • Introduce → explain → consent.
  • Good lighting + quiet environment.
  • Expose one body area at a time.
  • Lower bed, raise rails, clean equipment after.
  • Hand hygiene = most important infection-control measure.

Techniques

  • IPPA for routine PE.
  • IAPP for abdomen.
  • Resonance → air-filled lung.
  • Dullness → solid organ/mass.
  • Diaphragm → high pitch; bell → low pitch.

Infection precautions

  • Standard: all potential sources, all patients.
  • Universal: bloodborne pathogens.
  • Contact: gloves + gown.
  • Airborne: TB/measles/chickenpox; N95 + negative pressure.
  • Droplet: influenza/meningococcal/pertussis; surgical mask.
  • Reverse isolation: protect immunocompromised; no plants/flowers; strict hand hygiene.
  • Soap/water when visibly soiled or infectious diarrhea; otherwise alcohol rub.

General survey

  • Health state + consciousness + distress
  • Color/lesions + grooming/hygiene
  • Expression + odor
  • Posture + gait + motor activity
  • Height + weight + BMI + waist

HEENT cues

  • Dark room for ophthalmoscopy.
  • Weber: conductive → affected; sensorineural → normal ear.
  • Rinne: normal AC>BC; conductive BC>AC.
  • Sinus tenderness may suggest sinusitis.
  • Oral exam can integrate tongue/palate/gag cranial nerves.

Lungs & heart

  • Posterior chest → most fields/lower lobes.
  • Crackles → pneumonia; wheezes → asthma/COPD.
  • APTM: Aortic, Pulmonic, Tricuspid, Mitral.
  • Left lateral → PMI/S3/mitral stenosis.
  • Forward + exhale → aortic regurgitation.

Abdomen & legs

  • Supine, bed flat; IAPP; light then deep palpation.
  • Liver/spleen: percussion then palpation.
  • CVA percussion; positive kidney punch may suggest pyelonephritis.
  • Legs: vascular + musculoskeletal + neurologic systems.
  • Standing: varicosities, alignment, gait tasks, Romberg, pronator drift.

Nervous & additional exams

  • Five neuro segments: mental status, cranial nerves, motor, sensory, reflexes.
  • Motor cerebellar: RAM, finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, gait.
  • Sensory: pain, temperature, light touch, vibration, discrimination.
  • Male rectal: left side or standing bent forward.
  • Female pelvic: lithotomy; speculum seated, bimanual standing; chaperone when needed.
