Pharmacodynamic Foundations

Definition, therapeutic basis, receptor significance, coupling, and spare receptors.

pp. 1–2
Definition

What pharmacodynamics studies

Pharmacodynamics is the study of the biochemical, biologic, and physiological effects of drugs.

  • Biochemical effect: mechanism of action at the molecular and enzymatic level.
  • Physiological effect: the observable change produced in the body.

Its scope includes mechanism of action, clinical indications, adverse or side effects, and drug-drug interactions.

Why it matters

Therapeutic and design basis

  1. Rational therapeutic use of a drug.
  2. Design of new and superior chemical agents.

This includes the structure-activity relationship (SAR) or biochemorphology: relating chemical structure to biochemical and physiological effects. The lecture links this to creating more selective drugs with fewer toxic effects, with norepinephrine and epinephrine given as examples.

Receptor-mediated action

Receptors: specific macromolecular loci

A receptor is a specific cell molecule or locus in a macromolecule with which a drug interacts to initiate biochemical reactions and produce a biological response. Drug-ligand binding may be reversible or irreversible, depending on the strength and type of binding.

CovalentElectrostaticHydrogen bondingVan der Waals

Quantitative relation

Receptors largely determine the relation between dose or concentration and pharmacologic effect.

Selectivity

Receptors are responsible for the selectivity of drug action.

Agonism and antagonism

They mediate the actions of pharmacologic agonists and antagonists.

Drug-receptor coupling

Coupling is the overall transduction process linking agonist occupancy of a receptor to a pharmacologic response. Drug binding changes receptor shape, leading to a cellular signal and drug action.

  1. Open or close an ion channel
  2. Activate biochemical second messengers, such as calcium
  3. Physically inhibit a normal cellular function
  4. Turn on a cellular function
Spare receptors

Maximal response without full receptor occupancy

Some tissues contain more receptors than are necessary to produce a maximal response. Their presence depends on the tissue, the measured response, and the drug’s intrinsic efficacy.

Kd > EC50Lecture criterion supporting spare receptors; Kd is the concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied.

The lecture notes that even 1% receptor occupancy may produce a maximum response because of a large reserve. Insulin is the stated example.

Key differentiator
Spare receptors explain why maximal response can occur before all receptors are occupied.

Ligand-gated channels

Fast, direct control of ion flow

  • Nicotinic receptor stimulation by acetylcholine causes sodium influx, an action potential, and skeletal muscle contraction.
  • Benzodiazepines enhance GABA receptor stimulation, producing chloride influx and cell hyperpolarization.
  • The response diminishes within milliseconds.
G protein-coupled receptors

Seven-pass “serpentine” receptors

GPCRs are the largest receptor class. They cross the membrane seven times and interact with intracellular G proteins, which influence cellular effectors and second messengers.

Enzyme-linked receptors

Tyrosine kinase and autophosphorylation

Insulin and epidermal growth factor are the best examples given. These receptors usually have tyrosine kinase activity as part of their structure, followed by autophosphorylation of the activated receptor.

The lecture also lists growth hormone receptors and atrial natriuretic peptide receptors under receptor-enzymes.

Must know
Enzyme-linked receptor: extracellular ligand binding → activation of cytoplasmic enzymatic activity or an associated kinase.
Cytokine receptors

JAK–STAT sequence

  1. Ligand activates a membrane-spanning cytokine receptor.
  2. Associated Janus kinases (JAKs) are activated.
  3. JAKs phosphorylate STAT molecules.
  4. STATs dimerize, dissociate, cross the nuclear membrane, and modulate gene transcription.

Lecture examples include receptors for erythropoietin, somatotropin, and interferons.

Intracellular receptors

Lipid-soluble ligand → gene regulation

  • The ligand must be very lipid soluble and diffuse into the cell.
  • It may be attached to a plasma protein such as albumin.
  • The activated ligand-receptor complex migrates to the nucleus and regulates gene expression.
  • Response is not observed until around 30 minutes or more and lasts hours to days.

Examples: steroids, vitamin D, and thyroid hormone.

Glucocorticoid model

In the lecture figure, hsp90 binds the receptor in the absence of hormone and prevents the active conformation. Steroid binding causes hsp90 dissociation, permits activation, and allows regulation of specific gene transcription.

Altering metabolic processes

Counterfeit incorporation or fraudulent production

A substance becomes a structural analog of a normal body constituent, enters the cellular system, and alters cell action. The lecture gives sulfonamide as an example.

An antimetabolite is a nonfunctional analog of a naturally occurring metabolite that interferes with normal metabolism; antineoplastic drugs are the stated example.

Do not confuse
Receptor-mediated action depends on ligand binding to a macromolecular target. Nonreceptor action may instead depend on bulk, osmosis, adsorption, neutralization, chelation, radioactivity, radiopacity, or metabolic mimicry.

Properties of Drugs

Affinity, efficacy, intrinsic activity, potency, and the key dose terms they connect to.

p. 4
Affinity

Ability to bind

Affinity is the drug’s ability or tenacity to bind to a receptor. Drugs differ in strength of affinity, and a drug with stronger affinity may displace another drug. Binding may be competitive or noncompetitive.

Efficacy

Ability to produce a maximal response

Efficacy describes the relation between receptor occupancy and the ability to initiate a response at molecular, cellular, tissue, and system levels. It is the maximal pharmacologic response, shown as the plateau or ceiling effect.

Intrinsic activity

Ability to activate after binding

Intrinsic activity (IA) is the capacity of a single drug-receptor complex to evoke an effect—the ability of the drug to activate the receptor once bound.

Potency

Amount needed for a specified response

Potency concerns the range of doses over which a drug produces increasing responses. It is commonly represented by EC50 or ED50, depending on the curve and context.

Essential terms
TermLecture definitionExam cue
EC50Drug concentration or dose required to produce 50% of the maximal response; also called median effective concentrationLower EC50 on a comparable curve means less amount is needed
LD50Concentration that induces death in 50% of subjectsThe lecture notes that in humans, toxicity rather than lethality is used
Therapeutic indexRatio of lethal or toxic median dose to median effective doseMeasures relative drug safety
Margin of safetyMargin between therapeutic and lethal dosesWider separation indicates greater safety
Exam trap
Affinity asks whether the drug can bind. Intrinsic activity asks whether the bound drug can activate. Efficacy asks how large the maximal response can be. Potency asks how much drug is needed for a specified response.

Graded Dose-Response Curve

Magnitude of response, efficacy, potency, slope, variability, agonism, and antagonism.

pp. 5–6
Dose-response framework

Pharmacodynamics quantifies drug action through the dose-response relationship: the effect depends on the administered dose and how many drug molecules reach receptors. The curve provides information about the type of drug, mode of action, potency, therapeutic dose, efficacy, and relative safety.

Potency

Position along the horizontal X-axis; related to the amount needed for a specified response.

Slope

Linear middle portion; a steeper slope means a small dose change produces a large response change.

Efficacy

The peak or plateau, representing the maximum response.

Definition of GDRC

Quantitatively gradable effect in an individual

  • Relates a dose to a quantitatively gradable effect in an individual or a given population.
  • Expresses an individual’s response to increasing doses.
  • The magnitude of response is proportional to the number of receptors with which the drug effectively interacts.
  • It is response-related and shows the maximal response.
Four parameters
  1. Efficacy
  2. Potency
  3. Slope
  4. Biologic variability
Efficacy
  • Ability to elicit a pharmacologic response
  • Maximum desirable ceiling effect
  • Reflected by the plateau
  • Maximum response / effectiveness of the drug
Potency
  • Relative amount required to produce a specified response such as 50%
  • Represented by ED50
  • Depends on receptor affinity (Kd) and efficiency of drug-receptor coupling
  • Expressed in dosage units

Absolute potency: the lowest dose that can produce an effect. Relative potency: ratio between the dose of a standard drug and a new drug when both produce the same effect.

Lecture sample case
Drug A 5 mg and Drug B 50 mg both reduce blood pressure. They have the same efficacy, but Drug A is more potent because less drug is required.
Slope

The slope is the change in response relative to the change in dose, measured at the middle portion of the S-shaped curve.

  • Steep slope: narrow therapeutic range; small dose change → large response change.
  • Slanted slope: safer range in the lecture’s wording.
Biologic variability

The normal range of doses needed to produce a desired response differs among individuals. The lecture lists sex, age, diet, and concurrent drugs among causes.

Drug typeAffinityIntrinsic activityResponse
Full agonist11Binds and activates; maximal activation

Must know
Agonists have positive efficacy. Inverse agonists have negative efficacy. Antagonists have affinity but zero intrinsic activity.
Competitive equilibrium antagonism
  • Also called surmountable antagonism.
  • Combines with the same receptor site as the agonist but does not activate it.
  • The lecture lists reversible binding, such as electrostatic binding, and irreversible binding, usually covalent.
  • Shifts the dose-response curve to the right and increases ED50, so a higher agonist dose is required.
  • Examples: acetylcholine versus atropine; morphine versus naloxone.
Noncompetitive antagonism
  • Binds at a site other than the agonist-binding site.
  • Described as irreversible and insurmountable.
  • Example: phenoxybenzamine, an irreversible α-adrenoceptor blocker.
  • The lecture also gives monoamine oxidases, which break down norepinephrine and serotonin.
Lecture-specific wording
The lecture places both reversible and irreversible binding under competitive antagonists, while separately describing noncompetitive antagonists as irreversible and insurmountable. Preserve the lecturer’s distinctions when answering from this trans.
Additional uses of GDRC
  1. Classify agonists, partial agonists, antagonists, and inverse agonists.
  2. Differentiate competitive and noncompetitive antagonists.
  3. Classify drugs according to mechanism of action.
  4. Determine the relative potency of drug analogs using ED50.

Quantal Dose-Response and Drug Safety

Population frequency, therapeutic index, certain safety factor, and standard safety margin.

pp. 6–7
Quantal dose-response curve

A quantal dose-response curve (QDRC) relates dose to the proportion of individuals in a population that manifest a stated response. It shows the percentage of the population responding to a specific effect versus dose or log dose.

  • The response may be an all-or-none phenomenon.
  • ED50 is the dose producing the specified response in half the population.
  • It expresses the frequency of occurrence of inhibition or stimulation.
  • The quantal endpoint may be chosen for clinical relevance or safety of experimental subjects.
Uses of QDRC
  1. Determine median therapeutic and toxic doses.
  2. Identify the margin of safety and therapeutic index.
  3. Determine drug selectivity between biologic effects.
  4. Show potential variability of responsiveness among people.
Graded versus quantal ED50
CurveWhat is measured?Meaning of ED50
GradedMagnitude or intensity of responseDose producing 50% of the maximal response
QuantalPercentage of a population showing a specified responseDose producing the response in 50% of the population
Therapeutic index

A relative measure of toxicity or safety that uses median values and does not show extreme-value overlap.

Humans: TI = TD50 / ED50
Animals: TI = LD50 / ED50

Higher TI = safer drug.

Certain safety factor

A more accurate estimate focused on overlap between an effective extreme and a lethal extreme.

CSF = LD1 / ED99LD1: lethal in 1%; ED99: effective in 99%

CSF > 1: safe. CSF < 1: not safe.

Standard safety margin

Percentage by which the dose can be increased before 1% die of overdose; used more in industries.

SSM = [(LD1 − ED99) / ED99] × 100
Therapeutic range or window

The serum concentration required to achieve therapeutic effects without toxicity. A wider therapeutic range is safer than a narrow range.

Exam trap
TI uses median values. CSF checks extreme overlap using LD1 and ED99. SSM converts that separation into a percentage.

Pharmacogenetics

Genetic influence on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic response.

pp. 7–9
Definition

Pharmacogenetics studies the influence of heredity on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic responses, including qualitative and quantitative variation in drug response due to genetics. It also studies genetic traits affecting metabolism of environmental and industrial chemicals.

The lecture’s principal introductory example is α1-antitrypsin deficiency due to mutation in SERPINA1, reducing protection from neutrophil elastase and potentially causing lung or liver disease.

Pharmacokinetic variation

Variation in absorption, distribution, metabolism, or excretion, most commonly described as polymorphisms in cytochrome P450 enzymes.

Pharmacodynamic variation

Variation in receptors, mode of action, drug interactions, or adverse effects. The lecture states that tacrine effectiveness may depend on genotype at the apoE locus.

Idiosyncratic reaction

A genetically altered qualitative response that is unexpected, different, or peculiar after drug exposure.

Genetic polymorphism examples
  • Acetyltransferase affects isoniazid and sulfonamides.
  • Slow acetylators: peripheral neuropathy.
  • Rapid acetylators: hepatitis.
  • Pseudocholinesterase deficiency: succinylcholine may cause apnea from respiratory muscle paralysis.
  • G6PD deficiency: hemolytic anemia after oxidizing agents such as primaquine, long-acting sulfonamides, or fava beans.
  • Malignant hyperthermia: inherited inability to chelate calcium by the sarcoplasmic reticulum, causing calcium release, muscle spasm, and temperature rise.
Consequences of variation
  1. Altered renal or metabolic clearance
  2. Functional overdose
  3. Failure to convert a prodrug to its active drug or metabolite
  4. Altered pharmacodynamics
  5. Idiosyncratic or unusual reactions
Must know
Idiosyncrasy is an abnormal, unpredictable response on a hereditary basis that may appear with the first dose. Genetic polymorphism is the existence of two or more phenotypes in a population with respect to a drug effect.

Route and time
  • Route: magnesium sulfate is an osmotic purgative orally, while a 20% IV solution is used to control convulsions in eclampsia.
  • Aminoglycoside example: streptomycin given IV may cause neuromuscular blockade, but not after IM injection.
  • Time: hypnotics act better when administered at night, with similar doses required.
Climate, altitude, and race
  • Metabolism is stated to be lower in hot and humid climates.
  • Purgatives act better in summer; diuretics act better in winter.
  • Drug oxidation is lower at higher altitudes.
  • Castor oil is stated to be ineffective in those of Chinese descent.
Preparation and age of drug
  • Solid forms disintegrate slowly; liquid forms have faster onset.
  • Prolonged storage or expiration may decrease potency.
  • Antibiotic suspensions should be consumed within two weeks after opening in the lecture’s example.
pH of medium

The lecture states: decreased acidity → decreased effectiveness of acidic drugs.

Pediatric variability
  • More water content
  • Underdeveloped liver metabolism, including absent glucuronosyltransferase in neonates with gray baby syndrome and inability to metabolize chloramphenicol
  • Immature renal tubular transport; kidney function develops after one year of age
  • Inefficient drug-metabolizing enzyme system; glucuronidation takes three months to develop
  • Not fully developed barriers, including blood-brain and blood-aqueous barriers
  • Malnutrition may impair kidney and liver function; the lecture states penicillins, aminoglycosides, and streptomycin are not prescribed to infants because of high toxicity
Geriatric variability
  • Decreased liver function
  • Declining renal function: starts to decline at age 20; down by 50% at age 75 in the lecture
  • Decreased cardiovascular reflexes and tendency toward hypothermia
  • Higher fat content favors lipid-soluble drugs
  • Less water content lowers the volume of distribution of water-soluble drugs
  • Decreased intestinal motility and mesenteric blood flow
  • Altered mental functions; the lecture states that the brain begins to shrink or mental function changes at age 25
Lecture-specific discrepancy
The age-variability section says geriatric patients have higher fat content, while the later posology list states decreased body weight and body fat. Keep both lecture statements in mind rather than silently reconciling them.

Drug Interactions

Where interactions occur, how combined effects are expressed, and types of antagonism.

pp. 8–10
Definition

A drug interaction is any reaction between an object drug (the drug affected) and a second substrate or interactant. It may occur in the body or outside it, and the effect may be enhanced or diminished, beneficial or adverse.

ClassificationWhat is altered?Lecture point
PharmaceuticalDrug before administrationCompounding, combination, or dilution may cause loss of activity of the object drug
PharmacokineticADMEInteractant changes absorption, distribution, or elimination of the object drug
PharmacodynamicEffect at the site of actionExample: additive action of two drugs with similar actions
Addition / summation
1 + 1 = 2

The combined effect equals the sum of individual effects and does not exceed them.

  • Paracetamol + ibuprofen for fever and muscle ache
  • Aspirin + ibuprofen, which may cause stomach ache
Synergism
1 + 1 = 3

The total effect is greater than the sum of individual effects. Both drugs should have the same type of effect in the lecture’s example.

Ampicillin + amikacin: both are antimicrobials.

Potentiation
1 + 0 = 3

One agent with little or no direct effect increases the effect of the active drug beyond simple addition.

  • Codeine + paracetamol
  • Piperacillin + tazobactam
  • Amoxicillin + clavulanic acid
  • Levodopa + carbidopa
Antagonism
1 + 1 = 0

No net effect is produced because one drug opposes or blocks the other.

Flumazenil + diazepam: flumazenil blocks benzodiazepine receptors and reverses sedation.

Lecture formula discrepancies
The trans notes that the synchronous lecture once described potentiation as 1 + 0 < 1, while the didactic guide gives 1 + 0 = 3. For antagonism, sources in the lecture materials vary between 1 + 1 = 0 and 1 + 0 = 0; the 2026 trans uses 1 + 1 = 0.

Posology and Variations in Responsiveness

Factors influencing dosage plus adaptive and atypical response patterns.

pp. 8–9
Definition

Posology is the study of drug dose and its effect.

Physiologic factors
  1. Age
    • Pediatric: underdeveloped liver metabolism, inefficient enzymes, immature barriers, immature renal tubular support
    • Geriatric: the lecture says half the adult dose is usually given because of adverse reactions; decreased body weight and body fat, intestinal motility, mesenteric flow, liver and kidney function, and altered mental function are listed

  3. Sex
  4. Pregnancy and lactation
  5. Nutrition, including plasma protein binding
  6. Emotional status

Pathologic: kidney and liver disease.

  1. Time of administration, including presence of food
  2. Place of administration, environment, and climate
  3. Route of administration
  4. Racial differences
  5. Drug preparation
  6. Drug age
  7. Medium
Genetic factors

The lecture places idiosyncrasy and genetic polymorphism among the factors influencing dosage and response. Examples include acetylator status, pseudocholinesterase deficiency, malignant hyperthermia, and G6PD deficiency.

  • Intrinsic activity: ability to activate once bound.
  • Efficacy: maximal response / plateau.
  • Potency: amount required for a specified response.
  • Posology: study of drug dose and its effect.
Receptor speed and mechanism
  • Ion channel: milliseconds; Na+ influx with nicotinic ACh, Cl− influx and hyperpolarization with GABA enhancement.
  • GPCR: seven transmembranes; Gq→PLC, Gi→↓adenylyl cyclase, Gs→↑adenylyl cyclase.
  • Enzyme-linked: tyrosine kinase / autophosphorylation; cytokines use JAK→STAT→nucleus.
  • Intracellular: lipid-soluble ligand; gene regulation; onset ≥30 min, duration hours–days.
Agonist values
  • Full agonist: affinity 1, IA 1
  • Partial agonist: affinity 1, IA <1
  • Antagonist: affinity 1, IA 0
  • Inverse agonist: affinity 1, IA −1
Curve cues
  • Left shift: greater potency in comparable curves
  • Higher plateau: greater efficacy
  • Steeper slope: narrower safety margin
  • Graded ED50: half-maximal effect
  • Quantal ED50: half the population
Spare receptors
  • Kd > EC50
  • Maximum response before full occupancy

If you see X, think Y
  • Only 1% occupancy but maximal response → spare receptors.
  • Sodium influx and skeletal muscle contraction → nicotinic ligand-gated channel.
  • Chloride influx and hyperpolarization → GABA receptor enhanced by benzodiazepines.

  • Long-term higher dose requirement → tolerance.

Interaction formulas
  • Addition: 1+1=2 — paracetamol + ibuprofen.
  • Synergism: 1+1=3 — ampicillin + amikacin.
  • Potentiation: 1+0=3 — piperacillin + tazobactam; amoxicillin + clavulanate; levodopa + carbidopa.
