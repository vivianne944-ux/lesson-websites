Whole-Lesson Overview

This lecture connects drug–receptor binding to cellular signaling, then quantifies effects through graded and quantal dose–response curves, safety indices, genetic variability, dosage factors, and drug interactions.

Pages 1–15

Core pharmacodynamic pathway

Drug→ Target / receptor→ Coupling / signal transduction→ Cellular response→ Clinical effect

Pharmacodynamics studies the biochemical, biologic, and physiologic effects of drugs. It includes the mechanism of action, clinical indications, adverse effects, and drug–drug interactions.

Intrinsic activity = ability to activate once bound.
Efficacy = maximal response.
Potency = dose needed for a specified response.

  • Enzyme-linked receptors
  • Ligand-gated ion channels
  • G protein-coupled receptors

Dose–response anchors

  • Quantal curve: proportion of a population.

Pharmacodynamics & General Mechanisms

The lesson proper begins with the definition, purpose, and the division between receptor-mediated and nonreceptor-mediated drug action.

Pages 1–4

What pharmacodynamics includes

  • Biochemical effects: the drug’s mechanism of action at the molecular and enzymatic level.
  • Physiologic effects: the observable changes produced in the body.
  • Clinical indications, adverse or side effects, and drug–drug interactions.

Pharmacodynamics supports the rational therapeutic use of drugs and the design of new agents.

Structure–activity relationship

Structure–Activity Relationship (SAR), also called biochemorphology in the lecture, relates a drug’s chemical structure to its biochemical and physiologic effects.

✅ WHY SAR MATTERS
It helps create more selective drugs with fewer toxic effects. The lecture cites norepinephrine and epinephrine.

Receptor-mediated action

This is the more common form of drug–cell interaction. A drug ligand binds to a specific cellular macromolecule and produces a biologic response.

  • Binding may be reversible or irreversible.
  • Binding forces include covalent, electrostatic, hydrogen bonding, and Van der Waals interactions.
  • Receptors determine dose–effect relationships, selectivity, and the actions of agonists and antagonists.

Nonreceptor-mediated action

The effect results from the drug’s physical property, chemical interaction, or ability to alter metabolic processes, rather than receptor activation.

⚠️ DO NOT CONFUSE
A drug can produce a clinically useful effect even without a classic receptor. Examples include activated charcoal adsorption, mannitol osmosis, antacid neutralization, and chelation.

Receptors, Coupling & Spare Receptors

Receptors are drug targets, but the final effect depends on the coupling process that converts binding into a response.

Pages 1–3

Main target classes

Regulatory proteins
Mediate endogenous signals such as neurotransmitters, autacoids, and hormones.
Enzymes
May be activated or inhibited by drugs. Lecture examples include dihydrofolate reductase, acetylcholinesterase, ACE, COX, carbonic anhydrase, DNA/RNA polymerases, monoamine oxidase, neuraminidase, Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase, and reverse transcriptase.
Transport proteins
Examples include Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase for digitalis and norepinephrine/serotonin transporters for antidepressants.
Structural proteins
The lecture example is tubulin as a target of colchicine.

Drug–receptor coupling

Coupling is the overall transduction process linking receptor occupancy by an agonist to a pharmacologic response.

Drug binding changes the shape of the receptor, producing signal transduction and a cellular response.

  1. Opening or closing an ion channel
  2. Activating second messengers, such as calcium
  3. Physically inhibiting a normal cellular function
  4. Turning on a cellular function
⭐ SPARE RECEPTORS
Some tissues contain more receptors than are needed for a maximal response. Spare receptors are present when Kd > EC50. The lecture notes that only about 1% receptor occupancy may produce maximal response in the insulin example.

Kd

Kd is the concentration at which 50% of receptors are occupied.

When maximal effect occurs before 50% occupancy, a receptor reserve exists.

Signal amplification

Receptors act as transducer proteins. Post-receptor signaling can amplify the signal, allowing a small amount of receptor occupancy to generate a large response.

Ligand-Gated Ion Channels & GPCRs

These membrane receptors act quickly and translate extracellular ligand binding into ion movement or second-messenger signaling.

Pages 2–5, 13, 15

Ligand-gated ion channels

  • Response occurs within milliseconds.
  • Drugs may mimic or antagonize endogenous ligands that regulate ion flow through excitable membranes.
  • Nicotinic ACh receptors: acetylcholine opens the channel, producing sodium influx, action potential formation, and skeletal muscle contraction.
  • GABA receptors: benzodiazepines enhance receptor stimulation, producing chloride influx and hyperpolarization.

G protein-coupled receptors

GPCRs are the largest receptor class. They are single peptides with seven membrane-spanning regions and are also called serpentine receptors.

They interact with cellular effectors that alter second-messenger concentrations. These second messengers support both signal conduction and signal amplification.

GqM1, M3, α1Activates phospholipase C

✅ GPCR STRUCTURE CUE
The lecture describes an extracellular N-terminus, an intracellular C-terminus, and seven transmembrane segments. G proteins interact especially with cytoplasmic receptor regions, including the third cytoplasmic loop.

Intracellular, Enzyme-Linked & Cytokine Receptors

These receptors produce slower but often prolonged responses through phosphorylation cascades or altered gene expression.

Pages 3–5, 14–15

Intracellular receptors

  • Ligand must diffuse into the cell.
  • Ligand must be very lipid-soluble.
  • Examples: steroid hormones, thyroid hormone, and vitamin D.
  • The activated ligand–receptor complex migrates to the nucleus and regulates gene expression.
  • Onset is delayed: response is not observed until about 30 minutes or more.
  • Duration lasts hours to days.
⭐ KEY DIFFERENTIATOR
Hydrophobic ligands cross the membrane and bind intracellular receptors. Hydrophilic ligands bind receptors located on the cell surface.

Enzyme-linked receptors

These are membrane receptors with intrinsic tyrosine kinase activity. The activated receptor undergoes autophosphorylation.

Best lecture examples: insulin and epidermal growth factor. The lecture also lists growth hormone and atrial natriuretic peptide.

Cytokine receptors: JAK–STAT sequence

Ligand binds→ Receptor dimerization→ JAK activation→ STAT phosphorylation→ STAT dimerization→ Nuclear transcription

Lecture examples include receptors for erythropoietin, somatotropin, and interferons.

Nonreceptor-Mediated Drug Action

These mechanisms depend on the drug’s physical properties, chemical reactions, or interference with metabolic processes.

Pages 3–4

Physical activity

Adsorption
Adhesion of gas or liquid molecules to a surface. Examples: activated charcoal in poisoning and simethicone as an antiflatulent.
Mass / bulk
Bulk laxatives such as psyllium, bran, and fibrosine stimulate defecation through their mass.
Osmotic property
Mannitol acts as an osmotic diuretic and is used in the lecture for cerebral edema.
Colligative effect
Action depends on the number of molecules and ions. Examples: magnesium sulfate, menthol, and tannic acid.

Chemical interaction

  • Neutralization: antacids buffer or neutralize hydrochloric acid.
  • Chelation: binding of divalent or heavy metal ions into excretable complexes.
  • Radioactivity: radioactive iodine is used for thyrotoxicosis.
  • Radio-opacity: barium sulfate and iodine-containing radiopaque agents aid diagnostic imaging.
🧠 CHELATION EXAMPLES
Penicillamine is listed for Wilson disease / copper poisoning. Dimercaprol (BAL) is listed for heavy-metal poisoning.

Altering metabolic processes

A drug may act as a structural analog of a normal body constituent and become incorporated into cellular processes, thereby changing cellular function.

The lecture compares this to counterfeit incorporation or fraudulent production. Examples include sulfonamides and antineoplastic drugs acting as nonfunctional analogs of normal metabolites.

⚠️ PITFALL
Do not label all pharmacologic actions as receptor-mediated. Adsorption, neutralization, chelation, osmotic effects, and antimetabolite effects are emphasized as distinct nonreceptor mechanisms.

Affinity, Efficacy, Intrinsic Activity & Potency

These terms describe different parts of the drug–receptor relationship and should not be used interchangeably.

Pages 4–6

Affinity

Affinity is the ability or tenacity of a drug to bind to a receptor.

Stronger affinity may allow one drug to displace another. Binding may be competitive or noncompetitive.

Intrinsic activity

Intrinsic activity is the ability of the bound drug–receptor complex to activate the receptor and evoke a response.

It describes agonist–receptor coupling, not simply binding.

Efficacy

Efficacy is the maximal response a drug can produce and is reflected by the plateau of the graded curve.

It is the drug’s maximum desirable ceiling effect.

Potency

Potency is the amount or dose required to produce a specified response, commonly represented by ED50 or EC50.

It is reflected by the curve’s position along the X-axis.

⭐ CLASSIC EXAM DISTINCTION
Two drugs may have the same efficacy but different potency. In the lecture example, Drug A at 5 mg and Drug B at 50 mg both lower blood pressure, so efficacy is the same, but Drug A is more potent.

Graded Dose–Response Curve

The graded curve relates dose to the magnitude of response in an individual and is used to analyze potency, efficacy, slope, and biologic variability.

Pages 5–6, 15

What the graded curve shows

  • A quantitatively gradable effect in an individual.
  • The magnitude of response as dose increases.
  • Efficacy, potency, slope, and biologic variability.
  • The response rises as more receptors effectively interact with the drug, until the system reaches its maximum capacity.

Efficacy

  • Maximum response
  • Ceiling effect
  • Reflected by the plateau
  • Response-related

Potency

  • Amount required to produce a specified response.
  • Best represented by ED50.
  • Depends on receptor affinity and coupling efficiency.
  • Absolute potency: lowest dose producing an effect.
  • Relative potency: ratio between standard and new drug doses producing the same effect.

Slope

The slope is the change in response relative to the change in dose and is measured in the curve’s linear midportion.

⚠️ SLOPE TRAP
Steeper slope → a small dose change causes a large response change → narrower margin of safety. A more slanted slope indicates a safer range.

Biologic variability

Biologic variability is the normal difference in dose needed among individuals to obtain the desired response.

Lecture-listed contributors include sex, age, diet, and concurrent drugs.

Competitive vs noncompetitive antagonism

Competitive / surmountable

  • Binds the same site as the agonist.
  • May be reversible or irreversible in the lecture discussion.
  • Shifts the dose–response curve to the right.
  • Increases ED50; more agonist is needed for the same effect.
  • Examples: acetylcholine vs atropine; morphine vs naloxone.

Noncompetitive / insurmountable

  • Binds at a site other than the agonist site.
  • Described as irreversible and often covalent.
  • Example: phenoxybenzamine.
  • The lecture also names monoamine oxidase in this section.

Quantal Dose–Response & Drug Safety

The quantal curve describes how many individuals in a population display a defined response at each dose.

Pages 6–7

Quantal dose–response curve

  • Relates dose to the frequency of a designated response in a population.
  • May be an all-or-none effect.
  • ED50 is the dose producing the specified response in 50% of the population.
  • Can assess median therapeutic dose, toxic dose, selectivity, population variability, and margin of safety.
⭐ ED50 DIFFERENCE
Graded ED50 = dose producing half-maximal response in an individual.
Quantal ED50 = dose producing the specified response in half the population.

Therapeutic Index

Humans: TI = TD50 / ED50

Animals: TI = LD50 / ED50

Higher TI indicates a safer drug, but TI provides only relative median values and does not describe extreme values.

Certain Safety Factor

CSF = LD1 / ED99

CSF > 1 = safe.
CSF < 1 = not safe.

It focuses on overlap between the dose effective in 99% and lethal in 1%.

Standard Safety Margin

SSM = (LD1 − ED99) / ED99 × 100

Shows the percentage by which dose can be increased before 1% will die of overdose. The lecture notes industrial use.

✅ THERAPEUTIC WINDOW
The therapeutic range or therapeutic window is the serum concentration needed for therapeutic effects without toxicity. A wider window indicates greater safety.

Pharmacogenetics & Heritable Drug Responses

This section explains how heredity alters both pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic drug responses.

Pages 7–9, 12

Definition

Pharmacogenetics studies the influence of heredity on pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic responses.

It includes both qualitative and quantitative variations in drug response caused by genetic differences.

The lecture highlights α1-antitrypsin deficiency due to mutations in SERPINA1, which may cause lung or liver disease.

Types of variation

  • Pharmacokinetic variation: differences in absorption, distribution, metabolism, or excretion; commonly CYP polymorphisms.
  • Pharmacodynamic variation: differences in receptors, mechanism of action, drug interactions, or adverse effects.
  • Idiosyncratic reactions: unexpected qualitative responses caused by genetic factors.

Important idiosyncratic examples

  • Acetylation differences
  • Oxidation differences
  • Succinylcholine apnea
  • G6PD deficiency
  • Hypersensitivity reactions
  • Malignant hyperthermia linked to an inherited ryanodine receptor abnormality

Consequences of variation

  1. Altered renal or metabolic clearance
  2. Functional overdose
  3. Failure to convert a prodrug to its active form
  4. Altered pharmacodynamics
  5. Idiosyncratic or unusual reactions

Drug Responsiveness, Age & Posology

Posology is the study of drug dose and its effects. Dose requirements are shaped by physiologic, pathologic, environmental, and genetic factors.

Pages 7–9

Pediatric variability

  • More water content
  • Underdeveloped hepatic metabolism
  • Immature renal tubular transport
  • Inefficient drug-metabolizing enzymes
  • Not fully developed barriers, including BBB and blood–aqueous barrier

The lecture notes that glucuronidation develops over about three months and kidney function develops after about one year.

Geriatric variability

  • Decreased liver function
  • Declining renal function
  • Decreased cardiovascular reflexes
  • Higher fat content favors lipid-soluble drug storage
  • Less water content reduces Vd of water-soluble drugs
  • Decreased intestinal motility and mesenteric blood flow
  • Altered mental function
Factor groupLecture-listed factors
PhysiologicAge, body weight/surface area, sex, pregnancy/lactation, nutrition, emotional status
PathologicPresence of disease, especially kidney and liver disease
EnvironmentalTime, place, route, racial differences, preparation, drug age, medium
GeneticIdiosyncrasy, polymorphism, enzyme deficiencies

Rapid vs gradual loss of response

Tachyphylaxis is a rapid decrease in response after repeated doses over a short period. Examples: ephedrine, salbutamol, and nasal decongestants.

Tolerance develops with long-term use and requires progressively higher doses. Example: opioids.

Receptor regulation

Downregulation is a long-term reduction in receptor number caused by continuous agonist exposure.

Upregulation occurs when a receptor is blocked for a prolonged period.

Other variations in responsiveness

  • Idiosyncratic response: uncommon and genetically based.
  • Hyporeactivity: decreased responsiveness.
  • Hyperreactivity: increased responsiveness.
  • Hypersensitivity / intolerance: undesirable reactions produced by the immune system, including allergy and autoimmunity.
✅ ROUTE, TIME, CLIMATE & PREPARATION
The lecture notes that route can change the drug response, hypnotics work better at night, metabolism is lower in hot/humid climates and at high altitude, solid dosage forms act more slowly than liquids, and expired drugs have reduced potency.

Drug Interactions & Antagonism

Interactions may alter drug preparation, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, or the combined clinical effect.

Pages 8–10

Classification by where the interaction occurs

Pharmaceutical
Occurs during compounding, combination, or dilution and may cause loss of activity.
Pharmacokinetic
Alters absorption, distribution, or elimination of the object drug.
Pharmacodynamic
Alters the drug effect at its site of action.

⚠️ LECTURE-SPECIFIC NOTE
The lecture records conflicting symbolic formulas across sources for potentiation and antagonism. It ultimately identifies the didactic study-guide formula for potentiation as 1 + 0 = 3, while the synchronous lecture gives antagonism as 1 + 1 = 0.

Other types of antagonism

Physical antagonism
Example: charcoal in poisoning.
Chemical antagonism
Formation of a complex or chemical reaction that reduces drug activity. Examples: deferoxamine for iron, chelating agents, protamine with heparin, and antacids.
Functional / physiologic antagonism
Two agonists acting at different receptors produce opposing actions. Example: epinephrine vs histamine in blood vessels and bronchi.
Pharmacologic antagonism
Includes competitive/reversible and noncompetitive antagonism.

Clinical significance

  • Corrects adverse drug effects
  • Treats drug poisoning
  • Prevents combinations that reduce drug efficacy
🧠 IF YOU SEE FLUMAZENIL + DIAZEPAM
Think antagonism: flumazenil blocks benzodiazepine receptor effects and reverses sedation.

Cram Sheet

Whole-lecture rapid review with sustained emphasis on the final sections.

Pages 1–15
⭐ MASTER FRAMEWORK
Drug binds target → coupling / transduction → cellular response → dose–response relationship. The magnitude and safety of that effect depend on affinity, intrinsic activity, efficacy, potency, receptor type, genetic factors, age, disease, and interactions.

Four receptor families

  • Ligand-gated ion channel: milliseconds; nicotinic ACh, GABA.
  • GPCR: seven transmembranes; second messengers; Gq/Gi/Gs.
  • Enzyme-linked: tyrosine kinase / autophosphorylation; insulin, EGF.
  • Intracellular: lipid-soluble ligand; gene transcription; steroid, thyroid, vitamin D.

Core definitions

  • Affinity: binding.
  • Intrinsic activity: activation once bound.
  • Efficacy: maximal response / plateau.
  • Potency: dose needed / X-axis / ED50.
  • Kd: 50% receptor occupancy.
  • Spare receptors: Kd > EC50.

G protein grouping

Gq M1, M3, α1 → phospholipase C

Gi M2, α2, D2 → inhibits adenylyl cyclase

Gs β1, β2, D1 → activates adenylyl cyclase

Agonist classification

  • Full agonist: affinity 1, IA 1.
  • Partial agonist: affinity 1, IA <1 but >0.
  • Antagonist: affinity 1, IA 0.
  • Inverse agonist: affinity 1, IA -1.

Dose–response essentials

  • Graded: intensity in one individual.
  • Quantal: percentage of population.
  • Steep slope: narrow safety margin.
  • Competitive antagonist: right shift, ↑ED50.
  • Noncompetitive antagonist: different site, insurmountable.

Safety formulas

TI = TD50 / ED50 (humans)

TI = LD50 / ED50 (animals)

CSF = LD1 / ED99

SSM = (LD1 − ED99) / ED99 × 100

Pharmacogenetic red flags

  • Rapid acetylator: hepatitis.
  • Slow acetylator: peripheral neuropathy.
  • Pseudocholinesterase deficiency: succinylcholine apnea.
  • G6PD deficiency: oxidant-triggered hemolysis.
  • RYR abnormality: malignant hyperthermia.

Responsiveness red flags

  • Tachyphylaxis: rapid decline over a short period.
  • Tolerance: gradual decline with long-term use.
  • Downregulation: fewer receptors after prolonged agonist exposure.
  • Upregulation: receptor increase after prolonged blockade.
  • Hypersensitivity: immune-mediated undesirable response.

⚠️ FINAL EXAM TRAPS
Affinity does not equal efficacy. Potency does not mean a higher maximum effect. Antagonists may bind strongly but have zero intrinsic activity. Graded and quantal ED50 are not the same concept. Rapid acetylation is linked to hepatotoxicity, while slow acetylation is linked to peripheral neuropathy.
