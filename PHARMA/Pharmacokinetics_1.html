Pharmacokinetics I

How the body handles a drug: transport, absorption, bioavailability, and distribution

Big picture

Overview

Start with the fate of a drug from administration to elimination, then connect each stage to the route, barriers, circulation, metabolism, and excretion.

Pages 1–2

Pharmacokinetics vs pharmacodynamics

Pharmacokinetics describes how the body handles the drug and therefore dictates its fate.

Pharmacodynamics focuses on how the drug acts on the body.

✅ Key point: Pharmacokinetics follows the drug through absorption, distribution, metabolism, and elimination.

Major organ anchors

  • Liver = major metabolic machine.
  • Kidney = major excretory organ.
  • Albumin = largest and most abundant plasma protein carrying drugs.
  • Route of administration = major factor influencing absorption.

Pharmacokinetic parameters in one pass Pages 1–2

Absorption

The drug passes from its administration site into the circulation. The route is the major influence.

Distribution

In plasma, the drug becomes free/unbound or protein-bound. Free drug distributes and produces effects; bound drug acts as a reservoir.

Metabolism

Also called biotransformation. The drug may become an active metabolite, an inactive metabolite, or a more polar form that is more readily excreted.

Elimination

Occurs through excretory organs, with the kidney identified as the major excretory organ.

Crossing barriers

Transport & Ionization

A drug must travel from its administration site to its site of action. The key ideas are solubility, concentration gradient, surface area, membrane thickness, carriers, and ionization.

Pages 2–3

Solubility

Lipid-soluble drugs cross lipid bilayers more easily. Water-soluble drugs pass through aqueous phases.

⭐ Must know: Higher lipid–water partition coefficient favors passage through lipid membranes.

Concentration gradient

Only the free, unionized form contributes to the concentration gradient that drives diffusion.

Surface area & vascularity

Larger area and greater vascularity improve absorption. The lecture uses the small intestine over the stomach as the key example.

Aqueous vs lipid diffusion

Aqueous diffusion occurs through interstitial space, cytosol, tight junctions, and endothelial aqueous pores.

Lipid diffusion moves drugs through lipid membranes separating body compartments and is the most important limiting factor for drug permeation.

✅ Key point: Both aqueous and lipid diffusion are governed by Fick’s Law.

Special carriers

Used for substances such as peptides, amino acids, glucose, ions, metabolites, neurotransmitters, and xenobiotics.

Carrier systems are selective, saturable, and inhibitable. They work through active transport or facilitated diffusion.

⚠️ Exam trap: Special carriers are not governed by Fick’s Law.
Endocytosis and exocytosis Pages 2–3

Endocytosis

The cell membrane engulfs material. Examples include small polar drugs complexed with special proteins and large drugs that bind receptors before vesicle breakdown.

Exocytosis

The reverse process, responsible for secretion of substances from the cell. The lecture lists iron bound to transferrin and vitamin B12 bound to intrinsic factor.

Fick’s Law of Diffusion

Flux = (C1 − C2) × (Area × Permeability Coefficient) / Thickness
  • Flux: molecules per unit time
  • C1: higher concentration
  • C2: lower concentration
  • Area: cross-sectional area of the path
  • Permeability coefficient: drug mobility in the medium
  • Thickness: length of diffusion path
Thin membrane → faster
Lung absorption > skin absorption.
Large area → faster
Intestinal absorption > stomach absorption.

  • pH > pKa: greater fraction is unprotonated. A weak base is more lipid-soluble and better absorbed in alkaline pH.

Lecture sample problem: weak acid, pKa 2.5, stomach pH 1.5 Page 3
1.5 = 2.5 + log([Unprotonated]/[Protonated])
−1 = log(ratio)
10−1 = ratio = 0.1

The lecture expresses this as 10% unprotonated and 90% protonated. Because Drug X is a weak acid, its lipid-soluble form is the protonated form. Therefore, the answer is 90%.

pH partition

Weak acids tend to accumulate in compartments of relatively high pH; weak bases show the opposite pattern.

  • Urinary acidification accelerates excretion of weak bases and retards weak acids.
  • Urinary alkalinization has the opposite effect.
  • The lecture example is aspirin poisoning: aspirin is a weak acid, so alkalinization is used to draw it from the CNS into plasma.
Entry into circulation

Drug Absorption

Absorption is the passage of a drug from its administration site into the circulation. Route is the major determinant, but membrane, perfusion, pH, time, dose, motility, food, and patient factors all matter.

Physiologic factors affecting absorption and bioavailability Pages 4–5

Gastric acidity

An acidic environment favors absorption of weak acids. The lecture lists a small molecule, weakly acidic character, and high concentration as features favoring stomach absorption.

Gastric emptying

Most drugs are absorbed in the small intestine. The lecture states that if the stomach does not empty, absorption does not proceed normally. It lists normal gastric emptying at around 4 hours.

Small bowel motility

Faster motility gives less time for absorption, reducing bioavailability. Diarrhea is the lecture example.

Blood perfusion

More GIT vascularity and higher perfusion mean better absorption and higher bioavailability.

🔬 Gastric-emptying factors: food—especially fat, drug size, blood supply, movement/mobility, and abdominal compartment pressure.

First-pass effect

Also called presystemic metabolism. An orally administered drug enters portal blood and reaches the liver first; extensive biotransformation reduces the unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation.

⚠️ Exam trap: First-pass effect lowers bioavailability; it does not mean the drug acts more quickly because it reaches the liver first.

Interactions in the GIT

  • Food
  • Other drugs
  • Bacterial growth

The lecture example: calcium- and magnesium-containing antacids can chelate tetracycline, rendering it ineffective.

Patient status

Age, gender, nutrition, and related patient factors can alter bioavailability.

✅ If you see: small-bowel resection + failed oral therapy → think reduced surface area and decreased absorption.
Systemic exposure

Bioavailability

Bioavailability is the fraction of unchanged drug that reaches systemic circulation. The AUC summarizes total exposure and allows comparison of oral and injected formulations.

Pages 4–5

Definition and major causes of reduction

Bioavailability is the fraction of unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation after administration by any route.

First-pass effect
Hepatic metabolism reduces unchanged drug.
Presystemic redistribution
Drug is reduced before reaching systemic blood.
Incomplete absorption
Not all administered drug crosses into circulation.

Absolute bioavailability

F = (AUCPO / dosePO) ÷ (AUCIV / doseIV)

Used to compare a new drug formulation given orally against IV administration. When doses are equivalent, the lecture simplifies the comparison to AUCPO / AUCIV.

Three considerations

  1. Extent of absorption: lipophilicity, hydrophilicity, P-glycoproteins.
  2. First-pass elimination: expressed by extraction ratio.
  3. Rate of absorption: determined by administration site and formulation.
ER = CLliver / Q
↑ ER → more hepatic metabolism → lower bioavailability
Factors affecting bioavailability Pages 4–5

Biopharmaceutical factors

Dosage form, excipients, and physicochemical properties influence disintegration and dissolution.

⭐ Dissolution order: Solutions > Suspensions > Capsules > Compressed tablets > Timed-release tablets.

Physiologic factors

Gastric acidity, gastric emptying, small-bowel motility, gut-wall absorption, first-pass effect, and GIT perfusion all influence exposure.

Parameters used to determine bioavailability

  • Plasma data: time of peak concentration, peak concentration, AUC.
  • Urine data: total amount excreted.
  • Acute pharmacologic effect
  • Clinical observation

Volume of distribution (Vd)

The apparent volume of biologic fluid into which a drug appears to distribute homogeneously at the concentration measured in blood, plasma, or plasma water.

Vd = Total amount of drug in the body / Plasma concentration
or
Vd = Dose (Q) / Cp
  • Most drugs: 0.1–0.6 L/kg in the lecture.
  • More binding to tissues lowers plasma concentration and makes apparent Vd larger.
  • More binding to plasma proteins raises plasma concentration and makes apparent Vd smaller.
⚠️ Source arithmetic note: Page 6 prints “500 mg ÷ 500 mg/L = 100 L.” That displayed arithmetic is internally inconsistent. Keep the Vd = dose / plasma concentration formula as the dependable lecture rule.

Low Vd

  • Drug remains largely in the vascular compartment.
  • Associated with high plasma protein binding, low lipid–water partition coefficient, or poor membrane penetration.
  • Acidic drugs tend to bind plasma proteins and have smaller Vd.

High Vd

  • Indicates tissue binding and widespread distribution.
  • Extravascular tissue concentration exceeds vascular concentration.
  • Basic drugs bind more extensively to extravascular sites and tend to have larger Vd.
Drug forms in blood and plasma protein binding Page 6

Forms in blood

  • Free form = active form.
  • Protein-bound form = inactive form / reservoir.
  • Metabolites = degraded forms, usually.

Binding rules

  • Acidic drugs → albumin.
  • Basic drugs → α1-acid glycoprotein.
  • Hypoalbuminemia reduces binding and raises free drug level.

Central, peripheral, and redistribution Page 6

Central organs

Highly perfused: liver, kidney, heart, spleen. Drug equilibrates rapidly with these organs and plasma.

Peripheral organs

Poorly perfused: viscera, muscle, fat, bone. They pick up drug slowly and equilibrate more slowly.

🔬 Redistribution cue: Thiopental rapidly reaches brain and induces anesthesia, but its short action reflects redistribution into adipose tissue rather than rapid metabolism.
Models of distribution Page 7

Single-compartment model

Drug appears to distribute instantaneously and remains in the central compartment without distribution to peripheral compartments.

Multiple-compartment model

Drug moves from central to peripheral compartments before elimination. The lecture identifies a distribution phase, steady-state transition, and terminal elimination phase.

Storage sites Page 7

Adipose tissue

Stores lipid-soluble drugs such as thiopental and may prolong duration because fat is poorly perfused and metabolically slow.

Bone

Stores heavy metals and tetracycline.

Muscle

Drugs may reversibly bind intracellular structures; quinacrine is the lecture example.

Organs

Gentamicin may be stored in renal proximal tubular cells; aluminum may be stored in kidneys, liver, and neurons.

CNS and placental permeation

Placenta

Most small-molecular-weight drugs can cross. Fetal blood levels are usually lower than maternal levels, and efflux transporters move drug from fetus to mother.

Blood–brain barrier

Penetration depends on ionization, lipid–water partition coefficient, and protein binding. The BBB is permeable mainly to lipid-soluble drugs or drugs of very low molecular weight.

✅ If you see: high lipid–water partition coefficient → think better BBB penetration.
🔬 Inflammation cue: The lecture notes that inflammation may permit some drugs to penetrate the BBB, with penicillin in inflamed meninges as the example.

Last-minute review

Cram Sheet

Ultra-condensed rules, formulas, sequences, and “if you see X → think Y” cues.

Pages 1–9

Core definitions

  • Pharmacokinetics: how the body handles the drug.
  • Absorption: administration site → circulation.
  • Bioavailability: unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation.
  • Distribution: movement from plasma to organs/tissues.
  • Metabolism: biotransformation, mainly liver.
  • Elimination: excretion, mainly kidney.

Must-know formulas

Flux = (C1 − C2) × Area × Permeability / Thickness
pH = pKa + log(Unprotonated / Protonated)
F = (AUCPO/dosePO) ÷ (AUCIV/doseIV)
Vd = Dose / Plasma concentration

Absorption cues

  • More lipid soluble → faster absorption.
  • More blood flow → better absorption.
  • Larger surface area → faster absorption.
  • Longer contact time → better absorption.
  • Small intestine = major absorption site.
  • Fast intestinal motility / diarrhea → less absorption.

Ionization cues

  • Weak acid protonated = lipid-soluble.
  • Weak base unprotonated = lipid-soluble.
  • LUNA = absorbed.
  • WHIPE = excreted.
  • Acidify urine → excrete weak bases.
  • Alkalinize urine → excrete weak acids.

Bioavailability cues

  • IV = 100% by definition.
  • First-pass effect lowers oral bioavailability.
  • AUC = overall body exposure to drug.
  • ↑ extraction ratio → more liver metabolism → lower bioavailability.
  • Dissolution: solution > suspension > capsule > compressed tablet > timed-release.

Distribution cues

  • Free drug = active and diffusible.
  • Bound drug = reservoir.
  • Acidic drugs → albumin.
  • Basic drugs → α1-acid glycoprotein.
  • High plasma binding → small Vd.
  • High tissue binding → large Vd.

Common traps

  • Do not confuse absorption with bioavailability.
  • Do not call protein-bound drug the active form; the lecture identifies free drug as active.
  • Do not apply Fick’s Law to special carriers.
  • Do not assume an ionized drug crosses lipid membranes easily.
  • Do not interpret high Vd as high plasma binding; the lecture links high plasma binding to small Vd.
  • Do not forget that BBB penetration favors lipid solubility, low molecular weight, and a high lipid–water partition coefficient.
