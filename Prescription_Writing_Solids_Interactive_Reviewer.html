⭐ Must know summary

  • A prescription is an order for medicinal agents plus directions for preparation and patient use.
  • The session objective is to write prescriptions of solids with correctness of form and precision in dosage.
  • For most ready-made tablets/capsules, use the available preparation that satisfies the dose requirement.
  • For powder/capsule compounding, the physician should perform the computation, not push the burden to the pharmacist.

Core formula

For ready-made tablets/capsules, the lecture repeatedly uses:

Total dose = Dose × Frequency/day × Duration
Number to dispense = Total dose ÷ Size of preparation

If you see dose + frequency + duration + available size → compute total dose, then divide by preparation size.

Powder/paper formula

For powders and compounded capsules, the lecture emphasizes dose count first:

Total no. of doses = Frequency/day × Duration
Total dose = Usual dose × Total no. of doses

If you see “divide into equal parts” → think multiple-dose method.

Big-picture decision tree

When ready-made tablets/capsules fit

Use the single dose method and write the dosage form in the inscription. Ready-made preparations are preferred when they satisfy the patient’s dose requirement.

✅ Key point: The lecture recommends this for single drugs in tablet or capsule form whenever ready-made preparations are available.

When powders or compounding appear

For powder or capsules that include one or more ingredients, the multiple dose method is preferred because the physician performs the computation.

⚠️ Pitfall: The single dose method is acceptable for powders, but the lecture says it is not the optimal approach because it leaves more calculation to the pharmacist.

Correctness of form

Prescription anatomy: know which part talks to the pharmacist and which part talks to the patient.

Most exam traps in the review questions come from mixing up superscription, inscription, subscription, and signatura.

Pages 1, 9

Parts of a prescription

  • Identity of prescriber: full name, license classification/professional degree, address, office telephone number.
  • Date.
  • Name and address of patient, plus age. The lecture flags age as important especially for a child or senior citizen.
  • Superscription: R/ or Rx, from Latin recipe, meaning “take thou of.”
  • Inscription: body of the order: generic name, dosage form, and amount per dose and/or strength.
  • Subscription: directions to the pharmacist.
  • Signatura/Sig.: directions intended for the patient, usually preceded by Label.
  • Prescriber signature followed by M.D., PRC license number, PTR number, and S2/opium license number when required.

Inscription order for multiple ingredients

When several ingredients are present, the lecture lists them in this order:

  1. Basis - principal drug giving the chief action.
  2. Adjuvant - aids or increases the action of the basis.
  3. Corrective - modifies or corrects undesirable effects of basis/adjuvant.
  4. Vehicle - solvent; increases volume or dilutes the mixture.
⚠️ Exam trap: The review question identifies the agent that adds flavor/improves palatability as a corrective, not adjuvant, vehicle, or basis.
Correct form checklist

Names and spacing

Use official and/or generic names of drugs from the listed lecture references, and keep correct/proper spacing between prescription parts.

Grammar and punctuation

Correct form includes grammar, capitalization of certain words, spelling, and proper punctuation, not only the subscription and label.

Ready-made solid preparations

Tablets and capsules: use the available strength, compute the dispense number, and write the form clearly.

The lecture repeatedly models single-ingredient tablet/capsule prescriptions using the single-dose method when ready-made products are available.

Pages 2-5

Solid, single-ingredient tablet

Always state the form of drug in the inscription. For ready-made tablets, the dose is often the same as the available preparation size.

Total dose = Dose × Frequency/day × Duration
No. tablets = Total dose ÷ tablet strength
🔬 Lab cue: If the case says available in 325 mg and 500 mg tablets and dose is 500 mg, choose the 500 mg tablet to match the dose.

Solid, single-ingredient capsule

Ready-made capsule preparations are preferred when they satisfy the dose requirement. Powder-made capsule prescriptions exist but are rarely utilized when ready-made preparations are available.

✅ Key point: Whenever the size of a ready-made preparation is inadequate for the patient’s needs, the powder form should be used and the pharmacist instructed to make capsules containing the required dose.

Powders and papers

For powders, think in total number of doses and decide whether to use capsules or papers.

This is where prescription writing becomes calculation-heavy. The lecture favors the multiple-dose method for powder/capsule prescriptions with one or more ingredients.

Pages 5-6

Multiple-dose method

This is preferred every time a prescription is written for powder or capsules that include one or more ingredients.

Total no. of doses = Frequency/day × Duration
Total dose = Usual dose × Total no. of doses

Prescription wording commonly becomes: “Divide into [number] equal parts and dispense as papers/capsules.”

✅ Key point: The physician should do the computation.

Single-dose method

Acceptable for powders, but not considered the optimal approach in general because it places more calculation burden on the pharmacist.

⭐ Must know: The lecture says the single-dose method is preferred if the prescription contains a very potent drug.
Capsules vs papers rule

Use capsules

Weight < 600 mg → use capsules.

Use papers

Weight ≥ 600 mg → use papers.

⚠️ Trap: This rule is qualified by whether the patient can swallow the solid dosage form. The lecture notes small children usually have difficulty swallowing solid dosage forms, and pediatricians sometimes prescribe medications in papers.

Special solid forms

Suppositories and aerosols are prescription-form traps: the route/device instruction must be clear.

The lecture highlights label wording for rectal suppositories and device-specific instructions for inhaled preparations.

Pages 6, 8

Solid, single-ingredient suppository

Instructions on where and how to insert the suppository must be included.

🔬 Label cue: The lecture allows: “Insert suppository per rectum” or “Insert suppository into the anus.”
  • Paracetamol rectal suppository sample: 10 mg/kg/dose every 4 hours PRN x 4 days, 25 kg child, available 125 mg and 250 mg.
  • Glycerin example: adult suppository 2.5 g, single dose, insert per rectum at bedtime.

Solid, single-ingredient aerosol

The active ingredient is in micropulverized form and placed in a special container such as a metered dose inhaler.

  • MDI sample contains 400 doses, each puff delivering 250 mcg.
  • The drug is delivered to the lower respiratory tract via inspired air where it exerts a local effect.
  • Instructions should specify frequency of use and device-specific instructions.
⚠️ Trap: Do not only write the drug name. For aerosol devices, the prescription should identify the device/form, dose per actuation or puff, and frequency.

Multiple ingredients

Modern combination prescriptions usually use only the parts needed for simplicity and rationality.

The lecture explicitly says modern prescriptions rarely include all classic inscription parts; more often, two or more drugs corresponding to basis and adjuvant are combined.

Multiple ingredients, capsule

Examples include cough/allergic-rhinitis/bacterial-pneumonia prescriptions where powder forms are mixed and dispensed as capsules.

Recognition cues

TabletLook for a ready-made tablet strength; inscription names the tablet and strength; label uses “take 1 tablet…”
CapsuleReady-made capsule if available; compounded capsule if powder is used and pharmacist is asked to make capsules.
PaperPowder divided into equal parts and dispensed as papers; often when dose weight is ≥ 600 mg or patient factors justify powder.

Short-answer templates

Ready-made tablet/capsule:

[Drug] [dosage form] — [strength]
Dispense No. [computed quantity]
Label: Take [dose/count] [route] [frequency/timing] for [duration].

Powder/capsule multiple-dose:

[Drug 1] powder — [total amount]
[Drug 2] powder — [total amount]
Mix and divide into [N] equal parts and dispense as [papers/capsules].

Single-dose powder/capsule:

[Drug] powder — [single dose]
For one [paper/capsule]. Make no. [N].

Lab cues by pattern
If you see “BID” or “2x/day” → use 2 doses/day as shown in lecture computations.
If you see “TID” → use 3 doses/day as shown in the carbocisteine and sinusitis examples.
If you see “round the clock” → follow the stated interval/frequency in the case.
If you see “good for 3 months” → the lecture examples compute with 90 days.
If you see “regulated drug” → add S2 and No refills.
If you see “very potent” → prefer single-dose method and never round off.

Last-minute cram sheet

Ultra-condensed Prescription Writing (Solids)

No quiz. Just definitions, formulas, calculations, and traps you can scan before lab.

Pages 1-9

Core definitions

  • Prescription: order for medicinal agents + pharmacist preparation directions + patient use directions.
  • Rx/R/: superscription, Latin recipe, “take thou of.”
  • Sig./Signatura: patient instructions; review answer for “take one tablet TID.”
  • Subscription: pharmacist instructions.
  • Inscription: generic name, dosage form, amount per dose/strength.

Non-negotiable prescription details

  • Prescriber identity: name, license classification/degree, address, telephone number.
  • Patient name/address/age; age is important especially child/senior.
  • Signature + M.D., PRC, PTR, and S2/opium license if required.
  • Other possible parts: refill info, childproof-container waiver, additional labels.

Formulas

Ready-made tablet/capsule:
Total dose = Dose × Frequency/day × Duration
Dispense no. = Total dose ÷ Prep size
Powder/multiple dose:
Total no. doses = Frequency/day × Duration
Total dose = Usual dose × Total no. doses

Metric traps

  • ≥ 1 g → write grams.
  • < 1 g → write mg, not decimal grams.
  • < 1 mg → write mcg, not decimal mg.
  • Use leading zero: 0.5 ml, not .5 ml.
  • Use ml, not cc.
