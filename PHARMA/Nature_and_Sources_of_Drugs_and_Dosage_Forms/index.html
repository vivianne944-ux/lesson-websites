The whole lecture in one framework

A drug changes biologic function through chemical action. A medicine is the therapeutic preparation given to a patient, usually combining an active ingredient with functional but pharmacologically inactive excipients. The lecture then follows the drug from its nature and source to its dosage form and route of administration.

High-yield summary

  • Active principle = biologically active chemical constituent in a crude animal or plant preparation.
  • Active ingredient = declared pharmacologically active substance in the finished product.
  • Excipient = no direct physiologic or pharmacologic effect, but supports formulation, release, identification, preservation, or manufacturing.
  • IV is the comparison standard because it has 100% bioavailability by definition.

Lecture-level exam emphasis

⚠️ PITFALL
The lecturer emphasizes knowing the characteristics of each route and the meaning of bioavailability, rather than memorizing every numerical bioavailability value.
⭐ MUST KNOW

Foundations: what exactly is in a medicine?

Separate the chemical agent, the biologically active component, the finished therapeutic preparation, and the inactive formulation aids.

Core distinction

Drug describes the biologically active substance.

Medicine describes the preparation intended to produce a therapeutic effect.

Drug

A substance that changes biologic function through its chemical actions. It may be used to modify or explore physiological systems or pathological states for the recipient’s benefit.

PreventionDiagnosisCure

Medicine

A chemical preparation administered with the intention of producing a therapeutic effect. It usually, but not necessarily, contains one or more drugs.

Common contents: active drug, excipients, stabilizers, and solvents.

Medicine vs drug nuance

Endogenous insulin or thyroxine becomes a drug when it is intentionally administered from outside the body. Some drugs, such as methicillin in the lecture, may no longer be used clinically but remain useful research or diagnostic tools.

Active principle: from crude preparation to usable drug

The active principle is the specific chemical constituent in a crude animal or vegetable preparation that is responsible for biological activity. Lecture examples include alkaloids, glycosides, saponins, oils, waxes, gums, resins, balsams, and tannins.

IdentifyFind the active constituent or constituents.
AnalyzeStudy pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics of each constituent.
StandardizeEnsure precise and constant therapeutic dosing of pure constituents.
DevelopEnable synthesis and structure–activity analysis.
✅ KEY POINT
Chemical synthesis can reduce dependence on limited natural supplies and allows derivatives to be made in an attempt to improve pharmacologic properties and therapeutic usefulness.
Active ingredient: the declared therapeutic component

The active ingredient is the substance or combination of substances in the finished pharmaceutical product that directly furnishes pharmacologic activity or affects diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, prevention, or physiological function.

⭐ MUST KNOW
The active ingredient is required to be declared and is given in the generic name. In the lecture example, AmoClav is the brand, while co-amoxiclav is the active ingredient/generic name.
Excipients: inactive, but functionally important

Excipients have no direct physiologic or pharmacologic effect on the patient. They are inactive substances formulated alongside the active ingredient to bulk up potent formulations, support manufacturing, improve quality control and uniformity, or modify properties such as absorption and sustained release.

Structure & manufacture

  • Antiadherents: magnesium stearate
  • Binders: saccharides, sorbitol, gelatin, PEG
  • Lubricants: talc, silica, fats, stearic acid
  • Sorbents

Release & protection

  • Coatings: protect against GI acid or enzymes and prevent premature disintegration
  • Disintegrants: CMC, sodium starch glycolate; facilitate dissolution
  • Preservatives: extend shelf life

Patient convenience

  • Fillers: lactose, sucrose, glucose, mannitol, sorbitol, calcium carbonate, magnesium stearate
  • Coloring agents: aid identification
  • Flavors/sweeteners: improve oral use, especially in children
⚠️ PITFALL
An excipient may have more than one role. The lecture uses magnesium stearate as an example that may serve more than one function.

Nature of drugs: composition, size, and shape

A drug must be chemically suited to move through body compartments and sufficiently unique in shape, charge, and related properties to bind selectively to its receptor.

Two broad chemical groups

Organic: carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and smaller molecules.

Inorganic: lithium, iron, and heavy metals.

100–1000

Typical molecular weight

Most drugs fall between 100 and 1000 molecular-weight units.

7

Very small example

Lithium ion is listed at a molecular weight of 7.

50,050

Very large example

Alteplase (t-PA) is listed at a molecular weight of 50,050.

Drug size and movement between compartments
  • Selective binding requires the drug to be sufficiently unique in shape, charge, and other properties.
  • In most cases, drugs should be at least 100 MW units in size.
  • Drugs much larger than MW 1000 do not readily diffuse between body compartments and may need carriers or transporters.
  • Very large drugs, usually proteins, may be given directly into the compartment where they act. The lecture example is alteplase delivered into the vascular compartment by IV or intra-arterial infusion.
✅ KEY POINT
Size affects access. The larger the molecule, the less readily it diffuses between compartments, so route and delivery strategy become more important.
Drug shape, chirality, and receptor fit

Drug shape should be complementary to the receptor site. More than half of useful drugs are described in the lecture as chiral molecules, meaning they can exist as mirror-image enantiomeric pairs.

  • A drug with two asymmetric centers can have four diastereomers; ephedrine is the lecture example.
  • One enantiomer is often more potent because it fits the receptor better.
  • The lecture states that dextromethamphetamine is 10 times more potent as a centrally acting indirect sympathomimetic.
  • Dextro means right; levo means left.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP
Being an organic compound is a way to classify a drug’s nature, but it does not by itself guarantee selective receptor binding. The lecture’s binding determinants include shape, charge, chirality, and molecular size.
IF YOU SEE
A very large protein drug that does not diffuse readily → think direct delivery to its target compartment.
IF YOU SEE
Two mirror-image forms with different potency → think chirality and receptor fit.

Sources of drugs

Natural products can serve directly as drugs or as patterns/templates for synthetic and semi-synthetic development. The lecture groups natural sources into plants, animals, microorganisms, and minerals.

Source map

Natural → plant, animal, microorganism, mineral

Synthetic → starting materials not found in nature

Semi-synthetic → natural starting material altered chemically

Plants: the most prevalent natural source

Historically, medicines were often dried or fresh plants and plant parts. The lecture notes that plant products may contain therapeutic constituents or toxic constituents, such as poison ivy triggering contact dermatitis.

Scale mentioned in the lecture

  • 500,000 natural products
  • 160,000 products used worldwide
  • Half of 100,000 new metabolites under study are from plants

Plant-derived examples

  • Alkaloids: atropine, vinblastine, vincristine, quinine, colchicine, morphine
  • Glycosides: digoxin, ouabain
  • Oils: castor, olive, peppermint, spearmint
  • Gums and resin: latex

⭐ MUST KNOW
The lecture calls coffee the most widely used OTC alkaloid.

Animal sources

  • Lanolin: sebaceous glands of wool-bearing animals; used in lotions
  • Insulin: pancreatic β-cells, historically obtained from animal pancreas
  • Oxytocin and vasopressin: supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus
  • Heparin: liver extract
  • Cyanocobalamin: animal liver

Microorganisms & biotechnology

  • Penicillin: Penicillium chrysogenum
  • Streptomycin: Streptomyces gryceus
  • Chloramphenicol: Streptomyces venezuelae
  • Erythromycin: lecture notes the original name Ilotycin after discovery in Iloilo
  • Genetically modified E. coli can be used to produce insulin

Mineral sources

  • Ferrous sulfate: iron deficiency anemia
  • Calcium: treatment and prevention of osteoporosis
  • Magnesium sulfate: laxative
  • Magnesium trisilicate: antacid
  • Zinc sulfate

Synthetic sources

Produced from starting materials not found in nature; described as less expensive and suitable for mass production.

  • Aspirin: synthetic derivative of salicylic acid
  • Sulfonamide: chemotherapeutic antibiotic
  • Pethidine: opioid analgesic
  • Procaine: local anesthetic
  • Methotrexate: anticancer
Semi-synthetic drugs and radiopharmaceuticals

Semi-synthetic

Made by converting a natural starting material into the final product through chemical reactions. Ampicillin is presented as a modified penicillin with broader coverage or spectrum.

Radiopharmaceuticals

  • Radioactive iodine: diagnosis and destruction or shrinkage of diseased thyroid tissue or nodules
  • Technetium Tc99: diagnosis of bone, heart, or other organ problems
  • Iridium-192: placed in or near a tumor to kill cancer cells while sparing nearby cells
CategoryAdvantagesDisadvantages
Natural drugsGreater structural diversity and novelty; complex molecules that can interact with proteins and other biological molecules; some structures are difficult or impossible to synthesize.Time-consuming, may depend on seasonal growth, costly over time, may be less sustainable, and isolated compounds may behave differently than expected.
Synthetic drugsLess time-consuming and less costly; can be mass-produced; may be more sustainable.Tend to have fewer therapeutic effects and may cause unacceptable adverse effects.
⚠️ EXAM TRAP
Compared with natural drugs, the lecture identifies fewer therapeutic effects as a characteristic disadvantage of synthetic drugs.

Dosage forms: turning a drug into a usable product

A dosage form is the marketed pharmaceutical product: a mixture of active drug components, nondrug components, and sometimes other non-reusable material such as a capsule shell.

Selection factors

Pharmacokinetics · Pharmacodynamics · Age · Sex & gender · Disease process

✅ KEY POINT
The dosage form must fit the intended mode of use and support stability, precision of dosing, ease of handling, and patient-specific needs such as age and allergies to excipients.
Solid dosage forms

Tablets: basic structure

  • Popular, disk-like form made by mechanical compression
  • Fillers make the size convenient to handle and swallow
  • Binders hold the tablet together and prevent premature disintegration
  • Disintegrators encourage dissolution
  • Lubricants improve manufacturing efficiency

Coated tablets

A drug-containing core covered by wax, sugar, or another shell.

  • Protects perishable drugs from decomposition
  • Masks unpleasant taste or odor
  • Facilitates swallowing
  • Permits color coding and easier compliance

Buccal

Placed between the cheek and the inside of the mouth until dissolved; supports faster absorption and bioavailability.

Sublingual

Placed under the tongue until dissolved. Lecture example: clonidine.

Scored

Has grooves or partitions so the tablet can be cut when only part of the dose is required.

Multilayered

Colored layers or stripes indicate multiple ingredients; some analgesic and cough/cold combinations are presented this way.

Lozenges / pastilles

Taken like candy and allowed to remain in the mouth rather than being swallowed immediately.

Effervescent

Placed in water to dissolve and produce fizzing.

Capsules & spansules

Usually an oblong gelatin casing containing powdered or liquid medicine. Spansules use variably coated particles for gradual, extended, or sustained release in the GI tract.

Suppositories

A firm-base mixture inserted into rectal, vaginal, or urethral cavities. The lecture gives metronidazole for certain vaginal infections as an example.

Semi-solid dosage forms

Lotion

Oil mixed with water; usually less alcohol and thicker than solutions.

Cream

Oil-and-water emulsion in approximately equal proportions; thicker than lotion and usually oil in water.

Ointment

Homogeneous, viscous, greasy preparation, often petrolatum based (lecture: 80% oil, 20% water). Occlusive, moisturizing, and useful for prolonged contact.

Gel

Semisolid emulsion in an alcohol base. More viscous gels may melt at body temperature and tend to be drying.

✅ KEY POINT
Topicals are applied to skin for protection, care, and treatment of conditions ranging from allergies and infections to cuts and acne.
Liquid dosage forms

Solution

The drug is dissolved in a solvent. Forms listed include parenteral solutions, syrup, alcoholic solutions, aromatic water, gargles, nasal solutions, decoctions, and infusions.

Suspension

Small water-insoluble solid drug particles dispersed in water. Oral suspensions may require reconstitution and shaking before use.

Emulsion

Minute droplets of a liquid agent dispersed in another fluid, such as oil in water; it must be shaken to distribute the material evenly.

Decoction

The plant is boiled in the solution to extract the active constituent.

Infusion

Hot or cold water is poured over the plant to extract active constituents. The lecture uses traditional tea as the model.

⚠️ EXAM TRAP
Boil the plant → decoction. Pour hot or cold water over the plant → infusion.
Parenteral preparations
  • Special liquid preparations injected by IV, IM, or SC routes.
  • Sterile solutions may be clear fluids, but some begin as powder and require a diluent such as NSS before use because they are unstable after reconstitution.
  • Sterile suspensions contain crystalline particles and may be given IM, SC, or intra-articularly, but not IV.
  • Fat-soluble sustained-release formulations may be administered IM as a depot injection. The lecture gives depot medroxyprogesterone acetate as an example.
⭐ MUST KNOW
A product may be sold as a powder because it becomes unstable after reconstitution. Reconstitute only when it is time for administration.

Routes of administration

Route selection changes bioavailability, onset, first-pass exposure, convenience, sterility requirements, and the practicality of treatment.

Route classes

Enteral: oral, feeding tubes, rectal

Parenteral: IV, IO, IM, intracerebral, intrathecal, SC, intradermal, intra-articular, intravitreal, intraperitoneal

Topical/transmucosal: dermal, sublingual, buccal, nasal, eye, ear, inhalational

⭐ MUST KNOW
IV is the gold standard for comparing bioavailability because it is 100% by definition. The lecture also notes common use of IV for antibiotics and IM for antibiotics and vaccines.
Oral route
Advantages
Safest, most convenient, most economical, and cheaper than parenteral preparations.
Limitations
Gastric irritation; destruction by acid or enzymes; insolubility in GI fluids; complexes with food; variable absorption; slow for emergencies; unpleasant taste; limited to conscious patients because of choking or aspiration risk.
⚠️ PITFALL
Some drugs must be taken on an empty stomach because food can change pharmacokinetics. Oral absorption varies with gastric emptying, GI motility, and mixing.
Sublingual and buccal routes

Sublingual drugs are placed under the tongue. Buccal drugs are placed between the cheek and gingiva.

Advantages
Delayed first-pass metabolism because venous drainage from the mouth goes to the superior vena cava; easy self-administration; bypasses the stomach, so acid lability and gut permeability are less relevant.
Disadvantages
Unpleasant taste and local irritation.
Rectal route

Typical dosage form: suppository, which melts at body temperature.

Advantages
Can be given to unconscious, vomiting, uncooperative, or non-command-following patients. The lecture states that 50–60% of the dose may enter general circulation directly, bypassing the liver.
Disadvantages
Rectal irritation, inconvenient administration, and slow or erratic absorption.
✅ KEY POINT
Rectal administration has less first-pass effect than oral, not necessarily complete avoidance for the entire dose.
Parenteral routes
Advantages
Highest bioavailability, avoids first pass, rapid onset for emergencies, and best control over the delivered dose.
Disadvantages
Overdose or adverse reactions are difficult to reverse; requires sterile dosage forms and aseptic technique; infection risk; painful; expensive; generally not self-administered, with exceptions such as an EpiPen.
⭐ MUST KNOW
Fast and reliable comes with the trade-off of invasiveness and irreversibility.
Pulmonary and topical routes

Pulmonary

Advantages: preferred for gaseous and volatile drugs such as anesthetics; rapid access to systemic circulation because of large surface area; no first-pass hepatic loss; anti-asthma drugs can work directly on target tissue.

Disadvantages: difficult dose regulation, cumbersome administration, need for constant monitoring in surgery, and pulmonary epithelial irritation.

Topical

Advantages: direct local action; mucosal absorption occurs readily.

Disadvantages: absorption varies with applied surface area and lipid solubility; may cause irritation or stinging.

IF YOU SEE
Emergency + rapid onset → think parenteral, especially IV.
IF YOU SEE
Unconscious or vomiting patient → think rectal or parenteral, not oral.
IF YOU SEE
Direct lung target + rapid local action → think pulmonary route.
IF YOU SEE
Prolonged action without first pass → transdermal is described as slow and prolonged.

Ultra-condensed · Pages 1–7

Cram sheet

Read this immediately before an exam: definitions, differentiators, route cues, and common traps.

Rapid rule

Source → active component → formulation → dosage form → route → bioavailability/onset.

Definitions

  • Drug: chemical substance that changes biologic function.
  • Medicine: therapeutic preparation, usually containing drug(s).
  • Active principle: active constituent in a crude natural preparation.
  • Active ingredient: declared active substance in the finished product/generic name.
  • Excipient: inactive formulation aid with no direct pharmacologic effect.

Nature

  • Organic compounds vs inorganic elements.
  • Most drugs: MW 100–1000.
  • Very large drugs may need carriers or direct-compartment delivery.
  • Selective binding depends on shape, charge, chirality, and size.
  • One enantiomer may be more potent because it fits the receptor better.

Sources

  • Natural: plants, animals, microorganisms, minerals.
  • Synthetic: starting materials not found in nature.
  • Semi-synthetic: natural starting material chemically modified.
  • Radiopharmaceuticals: radioactive iodine, Tc99, Ir-192.
  • Synthetic advantage: mass production; disadvantage: fewer therapeutic effects.

Dosage forms

  • Tablet: fillers + binders + disintegrators + lubricants.
  • Spansule: gradual/extended release.
  • Suspension: solid in water; shake/reconstitute.
  • Emulsion: liquid droplets in another fluid; shake.
  • Decoction = boil plant. Infusion = pour water over plant.
  • Sterile suspension: IM/SC/intra-articular, not IV.

Routes

  • IV: 100% bioavailability, rapid, gold standard.
  • Oral: safest and cheapest but first pass, variable, slow, conscious patients only.
  • Sublingual/Buccal: delayed first pass; bypasses stomach.
  • Rectal: useful in vomiting/unconscious; less first pass; erratic.
  • Parenteral: rapid and controlled but invasive, sterile, hard to reverse.
  • Pulmonary: rapid and no first-pass hepatic loss; difficult dose regulation.

Exam traps

  • Active principle, not excipient, causes activity in a crude preparation.
  • Infusion, not decoction, uses poured hot/cold water.
  • Organic classification does not guarantee selective binding.
  • PO is the route where first pass is most important.
  • Rectal suppositories can reduce liver first pass.
  • Disease severity, duration, response, and comorbidities influence dosage-form choice.
