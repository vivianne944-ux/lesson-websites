Big picture: identify the coccus, then connect it to disease.

This lecture organizes aerobic cocci into Gram-positive cocci and Gram-negative cocci. The Gram-positive side is mainly Staphylococcus and Streptococcus. The Gram-negative side is Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Neisseria meningitidis.

⭐ Must know
Start every case with the lab identity: Gram stain, arrangement, catalase/coagulase or hemolysis pattern, then the species-specific test. The disease associations become much easier after that.

Identity

  • Coccal, round, Gram-positive; grows in clusters.
  • Golden-yellow colonies on blood agar, hence aureus.
  • Facultative anaerobe; non-motile; no spores.
  • Catalase-positive and coagulase-positive.
If you seegolden-yellow colonies + coagulase-positive clusters → S. aureus.
TSST-1PVLHemolysinExfoliatinEnterotoxin

Toxins and syndromes

  • TSST-1: cytokine storm → toxic shock syndrome with fever, sunburn-like rash, low blood pressure, poor end-organ perfusion.
  • Panton-Valentine leukocidin: perforates leukocytes → necrosis and inflammation; can lead to organ failure such as necrotizing pneumonia.
  • Hemolysin: destroys erythrocytes and releases hemoglobin; S. aureus uses the iron.
  • Exfoliatin: staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome/Ritter disease.
  • Enterotoxin: food poisoning; toxin may remain active after bacteria are killed by cooking.

Diseases and spread

  • Common colonizer of nostrils, armpits, and groin.
  • Low amount + intact skin → colonization; high amount + breaks in skin → infection.
  • Localized: pimples, boils, carbuncles.
  • Diffuse/deeper: impetigo, cellulitis, subcutaneous abscess, pyomyositis, osteomyelitis, septic arthritis.
  • Bloodstream spread → bacteremia, sepsis, CNS infection, pneumonia, infective endocarditis.
  • Biofilms on catheters, prosthetic valves, and artificial joints.
If you seemedical implant + biofilm + Staph features → think difficult-to-eradicate device infection.

Local epidemiology and MRSA

  • Lecture notes high MRSA prevalence in the Philippines: >50% since 2010.
  • MRSA is a major cause of healthcare-associated infections: surgical site infection, bacteremia, pneumonia in hospitalized patients.
  • Community-acquired MRSA is increasing, including pediatric infections without typical healthcare risk factors.
  • Nosocomial-acquired MRSA mortality rates cited were very high in PGH reports.

Resistance and treatment

  • Penicillins disrupt DD-transpeptidase/PBP, blocking peptidoglycan crosslinking.
  • Many S. aureus strains make beta-lactamase, which breaks the beta-lactam ring.
  • Beta-lactamase inhibitors like clavulanic acid disable beta-lactamases.
  • Methicillin-resistant strains express mecA, encoding special PBPs unaffected by beta-lactams.
  • MRSA treatment commonly uses clindamycin or vancomycin; alternatives listed include tetracyclines, TMP-SMX, linezolid, tigecycline, daptomycin, and quinupristin-dalfopristin.
⚠️ Pitfall / exam trap
MRSA is not just “penicillin-resistant.” The lecture specifically links resistance to mecA and altered PBPs, making beta-lactams ineffective.
Coagulase -Novobiocin sensitivepp. 4-5

Identity

  • Normal skin flora and dominant human skin bacterium, especially scalp, face, nasal cavities, axillae.
  • Gram-positive, clustered, facultative anaerobic, non-motile, non-spore forming.
  • Catalase-positive, urease-positive, coagulase-negative.
  • Novobiocin-sensitive: colonies die off around novobiocin disk.
  • Common contaminant of blood cultures.

Clinical pattern

  • Pathogenic when the skin barrier is breached.
  • Forms adherent biofilms on indwelling medical devices.
  • Leading cause of infections related to catheters, prosthetic valves, and joint prostheses.
  • Catheter infection may show local pain, swelling, warmth, erythema, purulent discharge, plus systemic fever, fatigue, chills, rigors.
  • Prosthetic valve endocarditis: fever, valve dysfunction; confirmed by positive blood cultures and transesophageal echocardiography.
  • Prosthetic joint infection: fever, pain, swelling, erythema, warmth; sinus tract communicating with prosthesis is a strong indicator.
If you seeprosthetic device/catheter + coagulase-negative Staph → think S. epidermidis.
✅ Key point
Treatment is typically vancomycin. If methicillin-sensitive, oxacillin or nafcillin can be used. Infected medical devices should be removed.
Coagulase -Novobiocin resistantUrease +pp. 5-6

Identity

  • Gram-positive, clustered, facultative anaerobic, non-motile, non-spore forming.
  • Catalase-positive and urease-positive.
  • Coagulase-negative like S. epidermidis, but novobiocin-resistant.
  • Commonly found on contaminated meat products and can become part of perineal flora.

UTI mechanism

  • Common cause of uncomplicated UTIs, especially in young, sexually active females.
  • Second most common uropathogen after E. coli in UTI cases per lecture.
  • Urease hydrolyzes urea into carbon dioxide and ammonia.
  • Ammonia increases urinary pH, favoring growth.
  • Ammonia can form complexes that precipitate into magnesium ammonium sulfate → struvite urinary stones → possible urine outflow obstruction.
If you seeUTI in young sexually active female + novobiocin-resistant Staph → S. saprophyticus.
✅ Key point
Treatment listed: nitrofurantoin or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole. If biofilm on an indwelling device is suspected, remove and replace the device.

Beta-hemolyticBacitracin sensitivePYR +pp. 6-8

Identity and tests

  • “Strepto” = chain, “coccus” = round, “pyo” = pus, “genes” = forming.
  • Gram-positive round bacteria that grow in chains.
  • Group A Strep in Lancefield classification.
  • Non-motile, non-spore forming, facultative anaerobe.
  • Catalase-negative.
  • Beta-hemolytic on blood agar due to streptolysins.
  • Bacitracin-sensitive: colonies die off.
⭐ Must know
Mnemonic from lecture: TRACE THE GENES OF PYOGENES - trace for bacitracin because S. pyogenes is bacitracin-sensitive.

Virulence factors

  • Capsule/adherence proteins: lipoteichoic acid, Sfbl, and M protein.
  • M protein: helps attach to host cells in skin or pharyngeal mucosa.
  • Hyaluronidase: destroys hyaluronic acid, enabling local inflammation and spread to bloodstream.
  • Streptolysin O and S: cause hemolysis.
  • SpeA and SpeC: superantigens that stimulate a large T-cell population and cytokine storm.
If you seesandpaper-like rash after sore throat → think SpeA/SpeC and scarlet fever.

Diseases

  • Strep throat/pharyngitis: fever, painful swallowing, red swollen tonsils, pus.
  • Scarlet fever: strep pharyngitis with bright-red rash and sometimes sandpaper feel.
  • Impetigo: itchy honey-colored scabs; can become fluid-filled blisters.
  • Erysipelas: upper dermis and superficial lymphatics; warm painful lesions with raised edges.
  • Cellulitis: lower dermis and subcutaneous tissues.
  • Necrotizing fasciitis: deeper spread to muscle fascia with necrosis, fever, painful purple skin.
  • Post-strep sequelae: ARF/RHD and PSGN.

Post-infectious sequelae

  • Acute Rheumatic Fever: occurs 2-4 weeks after infection; M protein mimics host tissue structures and triggers type II sensitivity reaction.
  • ARF manifestations mnemonic: J♡NES - joints inflammation, heart damage/murmurs, nodules, erythema marginatum, Sydenham chorea.
  • PSGN: typically 2-4 weeks after initial infection, commonly after impetigo; type III sensitivity reaction with antigen-antibody complexes in glomerular basement membrane.
  • PSGN symptoms: facial edema and dark red urine.
⚠️ Pitfall / exam trap
M protein is not the main hemolysin. Hemolytic activity is from streptolysin O and S. M protein is tied to attachment and post-infectious sequelae.
✅ Diagnosis / treatment
Rapid Strep Test looks for bacterial antigens in throat swab but can give false positives because colonization may occur. Culture is definitive. Treatment: Penicillin G; cephalosporins such as ceftriaxone and macrolides such as azithromycin for people allergic to penicillin; tissue debridement for necrotizing fasciitis.
Alpha-hemolyticOptochin sensitiveBile solublepp. 8-9

Identity and tests

  • Round bacteria that grow in chains, usually lancet-looking pairs called diplococci.
  • Gram-positive, non-motile, non-spore forming, facultative anaerobe.
  • Catalase-negative.
  • Alpha-hemolytic/green hemolysis.
  • Optochin-sensitive: growth inhibited around optochin disk.
  • Bile-soluble: bile salts promote bacterial lysis.

Virulence factors

  • Capsule with pili/fimbriae: attachment and biofilm formation.
  • PspA: inhibits complement activation and helps avoid opsonization.
  • IgA protease: cleaves mucosal IgA leading to mucosal infections.
  • Autolysin: breaks down bacteria and releases internal components, increasing inflammation and host tissue destruction.
  • Pneumolysin: binds cholesterol in membranes and forms pores causing cell lysis.
If you seealpha-hemolytic + optochin-sensitive + bile-soluble → S. pneumoniae.

Disease pattern

S. pneumoniae can colonize nasal cavities and sinuses. Disease is more likely in weaker immune systems: infants, elderly, HIV, diabetes, malignancy, alcohol abuse, spleen problems, splenectomy, and sickle cell disease/functional asplenia.

If you seecommunity-acquired pneumonia → think S. pneumoniae.
If you seeotitis media → mastoiditis → meningitis pathway → think S. pneumoniae.
If you seeasplenia/sickle cell + sepsis/meningitis → think encapsulated S. pneumoniae.
  • Diseases listed: rhinosinusitis, otitis media, mastoiditis, pneumonia, sepsis, meningitis, pneumococcal endocarditis, pericarditis, septic arthritis, intracranial/spinal epidural abscess, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis.
  • Diagnosis: culture from pus/sputum/CSF/peritoneal fluid; PCR can detect S. pneumoniae.
  • Vaccines discussed: PPV23 mainly recommended in adults; PCV13 recommended in young children.
  • Treatment: beta-lactams such as Penicillin G/V or cephalosporins; resistant strains may use fluoroquinolones, third-generation cephalosporins, vancomycin, or linezolid.

Pregnancy/newborn pattern

  • Screened in pregnant women because it is known for neonatal infections.
  • Colonizes gastrointestinal tract or vagina in about 30% of healthy adults per lecture.
  • Pregnant women: chorioamnionitis, miscarriage/premature delivery, cystitis, risk of transmission during childbirth.
  • Newborns: pneumonia, bacteremia, neonatal sepsis, neonatal meningitis, possible septic arthritis.
  • Adults with underlying disease or asplenia/sickle cell disease are more susceptible.
If you seepregnant mother/neonate + sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis → think S. agalactiae.
✅ Treatment / prevention
Penicillin G or ampicillin. Cefazolin or vancomycin for resistant strains or penicillin allergy. No currently available vaccine in the lecture; intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis is used for colonized mothers.
Alpha-hemolyticOptochin resistantBile insolublepp. 11-12

Identity

  • “Viridans streptococci” is more correct than a single species name because it refers to multiple groups.
  • Includes anginosus, mitis, sanguinis, salivarius, and mutans groups.
  • Common inhabitants of oral cavity and upper respiratory tract.
  • Gram-positive, catalase-negative, usually alpha-hemolytic; some S. anginosus may be beta or non-hemolytic.
  • Lack the polysaccharide-based capsule typical of S. pneumoniae.

Diseases and locations

  • Dental plaques, dental caries, and gum disease, especially with S. mutans and S. mitis.
  • S. sanguinis promotes dental health by inhibiting S. mutans growth.
  • Dental work/tooth extraction can lead to bacteremia.
  • Subacute bacterial endocarditis, especially with S. sanguinis, in adults and children.
  • Septic emboli can detach from vegetations.
  • Viridans group streptococcal shock syndrome in at-risk children with central venous catheters or immunodeficiency states.
  • S. intermedius can form brain and liver abscesses.
If you seedental work + subacute endocarditis + alpha-hemolytic optochin-resistant Strep → think viridans streptococci.
✅ Diagnosis / treatment
Diagnosis may use cultures from abscesses and blood, rapid antigen tests or antibody titers, echocardiography for vegetations, and CT for brain/liver abscesses. Treatment listed: penicillin G, ampicillin, cephalosporins, vancomycin, and linezolid.

Gram -Maltose -Thayer-Martinpp. 13-15

Identity

  • Gram-negative oval bacterium; typically diplococci.
  • Non-motile, non-spore forming, unencapsulated obligate aerobe.
  • Catalase-positive and oxidase-positive.
  • Grows on Thayer-Martin agar with sheep’s blood and antimicrobials such as vancomycin and nystatin to inhibit undesired organisms.
  • Does not ferment maltose; lecture note says it ferments only glucose.

Virulence factors

  • Pili: attach to mucosal surfaces, connect bacteria, allow conjugation, and vary by phase variation.
  • Phase variation: pili antigens change each infection, preventing quick specific immune response; lecture links this to lack of an effective vaccine.
  • IgA protease: destroys mucosal IgA and neutralizes first-line mucosal defense.
  • Catalase: breaks down hydrogen peroxide inside neutrophils.
  • LOS: triggers widespread immune reaction and sepsis.
  • Sialylation: wraps LOS with sialic acid to hide it from host defenses.
If you seechanging pili + no effective vaccine → think N. gonorrhoeae phase variation.

Clinical disease

  • Sexually transmitted infection.
  • Males: urethritis, prostatitis, epididymitis.
  • Females: urethritis, vaginitis, cervicitis, PID.
  • PID can lead to Fitz-Hugh-Curtis syndrome, with inflammation around the liver capsule and “violin string” adhesions.
  • Pregnancy/newborn: early neonatal conjunctivitis 2-5 days after birth.
  • Gonococcemia can cause gonococcal arthritis and endocarditis.

Diagnosis, treatment, prevention

  • Diagnosis usually through vaginal or urethral swab, Gram stain/biochemical tests.
  • Results may show pink, coffee-bean-shaped bacteria within neutrophils.
  • Thayer-Martin agar confirms growth; NAAT can identify bacterial genetic material.
  • First-line antibiotic: ceftriaxone, a third-generation cephalosporin.
  • Because gonococcal infection is frequently associated with Chlamydia trachomatis, azithromycin or doxycycline are added with ceftriaxone.
  • Prevention mentioned: condom use and broader safer sex/STI screening measures in the appendix.
⚠️ Pitfall / exam trap
Maltose test: N. gonorrhoeae stays red. N. meningitidis turns yellow.
Gram -Maltose +Capsulepp. 15-16, 21

Identity

  • Also called meningococcus.
  • Gram-negative round diplococcus, coffee-bean shaped.
  • Non-motile, non-spore forming, obligate aerobe.
  • Catalase-positive and oxidase-positive.
  • Culture on Thayer-Martin agar.
  • Ferments maltose, turning the solution yellow.

Virulence factors

  • Capsule: polysaccharide layer.
  • Pili: help attach to host cells.
  • Opa/Opc: opacity proteins that help attach to host cells.
  • IgA protease: neutralizes mucosal IgA defense.
  • Catalase: neutralizes H2O2 in phagolysosome complex.
  • Factor H binding protein: disables factor H and allows spread and toxin production in bloodstream.
  • LOS: triggers widespread immune reaction and sepsis.

Meningitis, meningococcemia, DIC

  • Can colonize the nasopharynx harmlessly in healthy individuals.
  • Disease occurs when bacteria invade bloodstream/CSF, especially in infants, elderly, immunocompromised, asplenia, or sickle cell disease.
  • Only bacterium in the lecture noted to cause meningitis epidemically.
  • Outbreaks can occur in close-living communities such as dorms and military camps.
  • Meningitis symptoms: headache, fever, neck stiffness.
  • Meningococcemia: petechial red/purple rash on trunk and lower extremities.
  • Sepsis: hypotension and tachycardia.
  • DIC: endothelial injury releases procoagulant-like tissue factor; severe bleeding; adrenal pooling can lead to Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome.
If you seemeningitis + petechial rash + DIC/Waterhouse-Friderichsen → think N. meningitidis.

Epidemiology, diagnosis, treatment, vaccine

  • Lecture notes low endemicity but likely underestimated burden due to surveillance limitations and rapid disease progression.
  • Highest incidence in young children in the cited data; NCR and CALABARZON frequently report the highest number of cases.
  • Mortality rates cited are high, including 41.5% in 2023.
  • Diagnosis: blood culture, lumbar puncture with CSF analysis and culture.
  • Treatment: IV ceftriaxone; may switch to penicillin G depending on antibiogram.
  • Prophylaxis for close contacts: ceftriaxone, rifampin, or ciprofloxacin.
  • Vaccines: meningococcal conjugate vaccine for children/teens; meningococcal recombinant vaccine for adults with spleen issues and frequent travelers to endemic areas.
⚠️ Pitfall / exam trap
The recap section contains lines about neonatal infections and intrapartum prophylaxis that match S. agalactiae content. For N. meningitidis, anchor on meningitis, meningococcemia, DIC, Waterhouse-Friderichsen, ceftriaxone, and close-contact prophylaxis.

Cram Sheet: Aerobic Cocci

Use this as the “night before” version: identity → test → disease → trap.

Golden identification rules

Clusters + catalase-positive = Staphylococcus.
Coagulase-positive = S. aureus.
Coagulase-negative + novobiocin-sensitive = S. epidermidis.
Coagulase-negative + novobiocin-resistant = S. saprophyticus.
Chains/pairs + catalase-negative = Streptococcus.
Beta hemolysis + bacitracin-sensitive = S. pyogenes.
Beta hemolysis + CAMP-positive + hippurate-positive = S. agalactiae.
Alpha hemolysis + optochin-sensitive + bile-soluble = S. pneumoniae.
Alpha hemolysis + optochin-resistant + bile-insoluble = viridans streptococci.
Gram-negative coffee-bean diplococci + Thayer-Martin = Neisseria.

If you see X → think Y

  • Golden-yellow colonies + coagulase + → S. aureus.
  • MRSA + beta-lactam failure → mecA altered PBP.
  • Catheter/prosthetic device + biofilm → S. epidermidis.
  • UTI in young sexually active female + novobiocin-resistant → S. saprophyticus.
  • Sore throat + sandpaper rash → S. pyogenes SpeA/SpeC.
  • ARF 2-4 weeks after infection → S. pyogenes M protein/type II reaction.
  • Dark red urine + facial edema after impetigo → PSGN.
  • Community-acquired pneumonia/otitis/sinusitis/meningitis → S. pneumoniae.
  • Pregnant mother/neonate + sepsis/pneumonia/meningitis → S. agalactiae.
  • Dental work + subacute endocarditis → Viridans streptococci.
  • Urethral/cervical discharge + coffee-bean diplococci → N. gonorrhoeae.
  • Meningitis + petechiae + DIC → N. meningitidis.

Common traps

  • M protein trap: M protein is not primarily responsible for hemolysis; streptolysin O/S are.
  • Alpha hemolysis trap: S. pneumoniae and viridans both alpha-hemolytic; use optochin and bile.
  • Coagulase-negative trap: both S. epidermidis and S. saprophyticus are coagulase-negative; use novobiocin.
  • Maltose trap: N. gonorrhoeae does not ferment maltose; N. meningitidis does.
  • RST trap: Rapid strep test can have false positives because S. pyogenes may colonize the pharynx.
  • N. meningitidis recap trap: focus on meningitis/meningococcemia/DIC rather than GBS neonatal lines repeated in the recap.

Mechanism mini-summaries

  • Biofilm: extracellular matrix/exopolysaccharides protects bacteria, limits antibiotic/neutrophil access, and can require device removal.
  • Urease: urea → CO₂ + ammonia → alkaline urine → growth + struvite stones.
  • Superantigen: MHC II/T-cell stimulation → cytokine storm → toxic shock physiology.
  • DIC in meningococcemia: endothelial injury → tissue factor-like procoagulant release → severe bleeding; adrenal pooling/necrosis → Waterhouse-Friderichsen.
  • Sialylation: wraps LOS with host-like sialic acid to help hide LOS antigen.

Prevention cues

Staphylococci

Infection control, aseptic technique, PPE, environmental cleaning, prompt device removal, surveillance, antibiotic stewardship, patient education.

Streptococci

Hand hygiene, respiratory etiquette, environmental cleaning, ventilation, avoid close contact and sharing personal items, improve living conditions. Species-specific: prompt S. pyogenes treatment, maternal GBS screening, pneumococcal vaccines.

Neisseria

Gonorrhea: safer sex practices, STI testing, prompt treatment, resistance awareness. Meningitidis: vaccination, early recognition, prompt treatment, close-contact chemoprophylaxis within 24 hours, hygiene/environmental measures.
