Prescribing as an art

Skills required

  • Diagnostic skills
  • Knowledge of medicines
  • Understanding the principles of clinical pharmacology
  • Communication skills
  • Appreciation of the risks and benefits of a drug for a particular patient
Why it matters

Medication-use problems

  • More than 50% of medicines are prescribed, dispensed, or sold inappropriately.
  • 50% of patients fail to take medicines correctly.
  • About one-third of the world’s population lacks access to essential medicines.

Appropriate drug use is an essential element of good-quality health and medical care.

Definitions used in the lecture

WHO · 1952
Rational use requires medications appropriate to the patient’s clinical needs, doses meeting individual requirements, an adequate period of treatment, and the lowest cost to the patient and community.
WHO · 1985 revision
Prescribe the right drug, in an adequate dose, for a sufficient duration, appropriate to the patient’s clinical needs, at the lowest cost.
Exam Trap
Rational therapy is not simply choosing an effective drug. The lecture definition also requires the correct dose, correct duration, fit with the patient’s clinical needs, and lowest cost.

Clinical sequence

Considerations before and after prescribing

  1. Evaluate and define the patient’s problem to establish a diagnosis.
  2. Determine the therapeutic objectives.
  3. Select an appropriate medication, including cost.
  4. Give information, warnings, and instructions.
  5. Monitor the patient regularly.
Prescription Order Standard
All orders should be legible, unambiguous, dated, timed for chart orders, and signed clearly to support communication among the prescriber, pharmacist, and nurse.

Prescribing errors needing special attention

Omission of information

“Continue present IVF” fails to identify the exact intravenous fluid. “Resume preoperative meds” assumes a complete and accurate prior medication record is available.

Poor prescription writing

Illegible handwriting or an ambiguous decimal point can change the dose: 0.1 may be read as 1, and 1.0 may be read as 10.

Inappropriate drug choice

May result from missed contraindications, incomplete information on other drugs, or failure to recognize physicochemical incompatibilities.

Lecture Examples
Certain insulin preparations should not be mixed. The lecture also states that simultaneous administration of antacids or products high in “methyl content” may compromise intestinal absorption of many drugs, with tetracycline given as the example.

Special attention

Legal & Socioeconomic Factors

Drug classes, regulatory oversight, labeled and off-label use, generic prescribing, and treatment cost.

Pages 1–2
Drug classes

OTC versus prescription drugs

Over-the-counter drugs can be safely administered by the layperson for self-limiting conditions. Most other drugs require a prescription by a licensed physician.

Controlled substances listed in the lecture include opioids, hallucinogens, stimulants, depressants, and anabolic steroids. Physicians must obtain an S-2 license before prescribing regulated or controlled drugs.

Drug safety surveillance

Government and professional control

  • Government controls drug labeling and distribution.
  • Licensing boards control who may prescribe.
  • Drugs in the Philippine National Formulary may be purchased by government hospitals and reimbursed by PhilHealth.
  • The FDA monitors drug safety through mandatory company reporting and voluntary reports from healthcare practitioners.

Labeled and unlabeled uses

Labeled indications

The approved label may not include every condition in which a drug might be useful. The lecture notes this issue even when a patient suffers an injury from the drug.

Off-label use

Off-label use means using a drug for a disease or medical condition it is not approved to treat, or using a different dosage form or dose from the approved use.

The trans also includes a lecture-specific statement involving statin use and package-insert labeling; the reviewer preserves the lecture’s framing without expanding it.

Generic prescribing and cost

  • Physicians must prescribe the generic name.
  • This allows the pharmacist to provide a generically equivalent product and the cheapest available option.
  • The prescriber may indicate a particular brand.
  • The lecture states that every generic product is as satisfactory as the trade-name product, while listing rare generic-product problems: a high ratio of inert ingredient to active drug, poor solubility, and a low therapeutic index.
  • Bioequivalence information and prices should be available from the FDA.
  • Other cost factors include the prescription fee, refill frequency, and the quantity dispensed.
Do Not Confuse · RA 6675 Box
The trans states that doctors are not allowed to instruct a pharmacist not to substitute the brand or to write “Dispense only as Prescribed.” It cites DOH AO 169, s. 2004 for generic-only terminology in DOH facilities and DOH AO 62, s. 1989 for prescriptions using “No Substitution” language as violative. The trans explicitly notes that this legal box was not discussed during the lecture and was corrected/supplemented by the team.

Clinical Cue
Not all health problems need drugs, and not every personal treatment consists of medication. Treatment selection may be advice, information, non-drug treatment, a P-drug, or referral.

Outline of P-drug selection

  1. Diagnosis
  2. Therapeutic objectives
  3. Non-drug measures
  4. Inventory of effective drug groups, then specific drugs
  5. Write the prescription for the patient
  6. Counsel the patient on appropriate drug use
Diagnosis

Primary + relevant secondary diagnoses

Prescribing decisions should be based on the primary diagnosis and relevant secondary diagnoses.

Example: A diabetic patient with fever, cough, and shortness of breath has pneumonia as the primary diagnosis and type 2 diabetes mellitus as the secondary diagnosis.

Treatment goals

Cure, relief, or prevention

For pneumonia, goals listed in the lecture are to cure the infection, ease coughing, and prevent complications or recurrence.

Treatment selection

Range of strategies

  • Advice and information
  • Non-drug treatment
  • Drug treatment with a P-drug
  • Referral

P-drug selection

ESSC Criteria

Each drug is assessed for Efficacy, Safety, Suitability, and Cost.

Page 2
E · Efficacy

Can the drug achieve the objective?

Efficacy concerns effectiveness in relieving symptoms through the drug’s mechanism of action.

Pharmacokinetic properties

  • Onset and duration may influence adherence.
  • Consistent bioavailability makes response more predictable.
  • Tissue distribution affects adverse effects outside the target site.
  • Metabolism and excretion help anticipate variable responses.

Pharmacodynamic properties

  • Target specificity and selectivity
  • Dose-response characteristics and ease of titration
  • Therapeutic index and ease of dose selection
  • Spectrum of activity for antibiotics
S · Safety

What harms can occur?

  • Adverse effects
  • Frequency of adverse effects
  • Seriousness of adverse effects
  • Predictability of adverse effects
Table Guideline
List adverse effects from the most common to the rarest and state whether there is a chance of toxicity.
S · Suitability

Does this drug fit this patient?

  • History of previous adverse drug reactions
  • Vulnerability: very young, older, pregnant, or with comorbidities
  • Current drug therapy and possible interactions
  • Likely adherence; once-daily dosing may improve compliance
  • Taste and dosage form: patch, oral, IV, intramuscular, syrup, suspension
  • Appropriate dose and form for pediatric or geriatric patients
  • Availability and practicality in the locality or Philippines
C · Cost

What is the cost of the whole treatment?

  • Compare the total treatment cost; the lowest price receives the higher rating.
  • Start with the retail price of one tablet or vial, then calculate the amount required for the entire duration.
  • Consider alternatives with similar efficacy.
Exam Trap
Do not compare only the unit price. The lecture requires the cost for the full treatment duration.
From comparison to selection

Rating the ESSC Criteria

How to populate the table, assign plus scores, and keep each criterion conceptually clean.

Pages 2–3

Table content by criterion

  • Efficacy: MOA, PK, PD, antimicrobial spectrum, and supporting journal reports if any.
  • Safety: adverse effects from common to rare and toxicity.
  • Suitability: contraindications, comorbidities, other drugs, convenience, dosing frequency, dose, dosage form, and local availability.
  • Cost: least cost for the whole treatment duration.

Scoring method

  • List drug groups or drugs.
  • Score each category from + to ++++.
  • Compare drugs vertically.
  • For SGDs, present data column-by-column, comparing all drugs under one criterion at a time.
  • After a group wins, list its specific drugs and score again.
Critical Scoring Rule
Scores must reflect only their designated column. Do not lower Efficacy because of adverse effects; those belong under Safety. Do not lower Safety merely because a drug is contraindicated in pregnancy; population-specific restrictions belong under Suitability.

Appendix mini case

Mini Case: “Thirdy So”

A full rational-therapy exercise for urticaria in a 10-year-old child.

Pages 6–8

Thirdy is a 10-year-old boy who develops itchy maculopapular rashes on the chest, back, and extremities after playing outside during a provincial visit. He has similar episodes with climate changes and travel; family history is positive for atopic dermatitis. Vital signs are stable; weight is 30 kg; the rest of the examination is unremarkable. The physician’s impression is urticaria.

1 · Define the diagnosis
  • Urticaria or hives is characterized by edematous, erythematous, itchy plaques involving skin and mucous membranes.
  • A specific antigen binds antibodies and triggers cutaneous mast-cell degranulation.
  • Released mediators such as histamine bind receptors on post-capillary venules or nerve endings, producing itch signaling.
  • Erythema and vasodilation increase vascular permeability and permit inflammatory cells such as neutrophils and basophils, leading to edema and pale swelling with a surrounding red flare or wheal.
  • The lecture classifies urticaria as acute spontaneous, chronic spontaneous, chronic inducible, and episodic chronic urticaria. Acute lasts less than 6 weeks; chronic classifications last more than 6 weeks.
  • Triggers include respiratory allergens, food, medications, infections, and physical factors. Because Thirdy has recurrent episodes with climate changes, the appendix identifies the pattern as physical urticaria / chronic inducible urticaria.
2 · Therapeutic objectives
  • Relieve itchiness
  • Clear skin rash and wheal
  • Maintain skin integrity and prevent infection
  • Identify triggers to avoid future episodes
  • Educate the patient and family
3 · Non-drug measures
  • Wear sunscreen every day
  • Avoid tight clothing
  • Refrain from scratching
  • Keep fingernails short and clean
  • Minimize high temperature and excessive sweating

Cram Sheet

Rapid review of the entire lecture—definitions, sequences, ESSC rules, case winners, warnings, and counseling.

Pages 1–8
Definition

Rational use

Right drug + adequate dose + sufficient duration + appropriate clinical need + lowest cost.

Good prescribing matters because more than 50% of medicines are used inappropriately, 50% of patients do not take them correctly, and about ⅓ of the world lacks access to essential medicines.

Ethical aims

Four obligations

Maximize effectiveness · Minimize harm · Avoid waste · Respect patient choice.

Orders must be legible, unambiguous, dated, timed, and signed.

Main sequence

Rational prescribing pathway

Diagnosis → therapeutic objectives → non-drug measures → inventory of groups/drugs → ESSC → prescription → counseling → monitoring.

ESSC

What each letter means

Efficacy: MOA, PK, PD, spectrum.
Safety: adverse effects, frequency, seriousness, predictability.
Suitability: patient factors, interactions, adherence, form, availability.
Cost: total treatment cost.

Common Exam Traps
  • Do not penalize Efficacy for side effects; use Safety.
  • Do not penalize Safety solely for pregnancy/age restrictions; use Suitability.
  • Cost means the whole treatment duration, not one tablet or vial.
  • A lower ESSC score does not mean the drug can never be used; it may remain an alternative.
