Big-picture framework

Solid prescription writing combines correct form with precision in dosage. The lecture moves from the anatomy of a prescription to tablets, capsules, powders, suppositories, combinations, and inhaled solid preparations.

Pages 1–9
Core definition

What is a prescription?

A physician’s order for one or more medicinal agents, with instructions to the pharmacist for preparation and directions to the patient for use. The lecture also describes it as a written, verbal, or electronic order from a licensed healthcare provider for a specific patient.

Page 1
Correctness of form

Write it clearly

Use the official and/or generic name, proper spacing, correct grammar, capitalization, spelling, and punctuation. The prescription must clearly separate the drug order, pharmacist instructions, and patient directions.

Pages 1–2
Precision in dosage

Compute before prescribing

Know the recommended dose, available preparation, frequency per day, and duration. Then compute the total quantity the pharmacist must dispense.

Total dose = dose × frequency/day × duration

Pages 2–6

Metric-writing rules

  • ≥1 gram: write in grams.
  • <1 gram: write in milligrams, e.g. 300 mg, not 0.3 g.
  • <1 mg: write in micrograms, e.g. 100 mcg, not 0.1 mg.
  • Use a leading zero: 0.5 ml, not .5 ml.
  • Write ml, not cc.
Page 2

Rapid recognition cues

  • If you see a regulated drug → include the S2 number and No refills.
  • If the ready-made strength matches the dose → use the ready-made tablet or capsule.
  • If powder weight is <600 mg → generally use capsules.
  • If powder weight is ≥600 mg → use papers.
  • If the patient cannot swallow the usual form → the lecture allows the dosage form to be adapted.
Pages 3–8
Prescription foundations

Anatomy of a prescription

Know who each part is written for. Most exam errors happen when inscription, subscription, and signatura are mixed up.

Pages 1–2

Parts in order

  1. Identity of the prescriber: full name, license classification, address, office telephone number.
  2. Date.
  3. Patient name and address: include age, especially for children and senior citizens.
  4. Superscription: R/ or Rx, from recipe, “take thou of.”
  5. Inscription: body of the order—generic drug name, dosage form, amount per dose and/or strength.
  6. Subscription: directions to the pharmacist.
  7. Signatura: directions intended for the patient, usually preceded by “Label.”
  8. Prescriber’s signature: followed by M.D., PRC, PTR, and S2 when required.

Subscription = pharmacist. Signatura = patient. The instruction “Take one tablet three times a day” belongs to the Signatura.

Other items that may appear

The lecture lists refill information, waiver of childproof-container requirements, and added labels such as “May cause drowsiness” or “Do not drink alcohol.”

  • Generic names are preferred; brand names are not always required.
  • The PRC number must be written.
  • Rx means recipe or “take,” not “drug.”
Page 9
Ready-made solids

Tablets & capsules

For ready-made single-ingredient preparations, the lecture favors the single dose method: write the available form and strength, then calculate the number to dispense.

Pages 2–5

Tablet/capsule calculation pattern

Total dose = dose × frequency/day × durationNumber to dispense = total dose ÷ strength of each tablet/capsule
✅ Key point
Always state the dosage form in the inscription. When a ready-made preparation satisfies the required dose, it is preferred for ease and simplicity.
Pages 2–4

Worked example: paracetamol tablet

500 mg every 4 hours as needed for 3 days. The lecture computes 4 tablets/day based on 16 waking hours.

500 mg × 4/day × 3 days = 6000 mg
6000 mg ÷ 500 mg/tablet = 12 tablets
Page 2

Worked example: regulated tablet

Diazepam 5 mg once daily after dinner for 10 days.

5 mg × 1/day × 10 days = 50 mg
50 mg ÷ 5 mg/tablet = 10 tablets
⚠️ Pitfall
Because diazepam is regulated, include the S2 number and No refills.
Pages 2–3

Metformin + atorvastatin

For 90 days: metformin 500 mg BID gives 180 tablets; atorvastatin 40 mg daily gives 90 tablets.

Page 3

Clarithromycin in a child

15 mg/kg/day × 33 kg = 495 mg/day; divided BID = 247.5 mg/dose; rounded to the available 250 mg tablet. For 7 days: 14 tablets.

Page 3

Single-dose mebendazole

One 500 mg chewable tablet as a single dose → Dispense No. 1.

Page 2

Capsules: ready-made vs compounded

Ready-made capsules are preferred when the available size meets the patient’s dose. If the ready-made size is inadequate, the lecture permits use of the powder form and instructs the pharmacist to make capsules containing the required dose.

Tacrolimus

1 mg every 12 hours for 60 days: 1 × 2 × 60 = 120 capsules.

Page 4

Carbocisteine

500 mg TID for 7 days: 500 × 3 × 7 = 10,500 mg; divide by 500 mg = 21 capsules.

Page 4

Vitamin D softgel

800 IU daily for 90 days: 72,000 IU total; divide by 800 IU = 90 capsules.

Page 5
Compounded solids

Powders & papers

The lecture emphasizes choosing the correct method and packaging by dose size, potency, and the patient’s ability to swallow.

Pages 5–8

Multiple dose method

Preferred for powders or capsules with one or more ingredients because the physician performs the full calculation.

Total doses = frequency/day × duration
Total drug = dose per intake × total doses
✅ Key point
The lecture considers this the optimal approach because it does not shift the calculation burden to the pharmacist.
Page 5

Single dose method

Each single dose is written, and the pharmacist is instructed to make the required number of units. The lecture says it is preferred for a very potent drug.

⚠️ Never
Never round off the dose of a very potent drug. The lecture flags drugs given in microgram doses as very potent.
Pages 5 and 7

Capsule or paper?

If the weight is <600 mg →
Use capsules.
If the weight is ≥600 mg →
Use papers.

This general rule applies to a single drug or a combination. Patient factors still matter: the lecture notes that small children may have difficulty swallowing solid dosage forms, while compounded papers may be used when a suitable liquid preparation is unavailable.

Page 5

Magnesium trisilicate

1 g TID for 5 days → 3 × 5 = 15 doses → 15 g total, divided into 15 papers.

Page 5

May Nana

15 kg × 10 mg/kg/dose × 28 doses = 4200 mg or 4.2 g, divided into 28 papers.

Page 5

Malou Ga

10 kg × 90 mg/kg/day = 900 mg/day; divided into 3 doses = 300 mg per paper; make 30 papers for 10 days.

Page 6
Route + multiple ingredients

Suppositories & combinations

These prescriptions test whether you can make the route unmistakable and correctly total every component of a compounded mixture.

Pages 6–8

Suppository essentials

The label must say where and how to insert the suppository. Accepted lecture wording includes:

  • “Insert suppository per rectum.”
  • “Insert suppository into the anus.”
🔬 Recognition cue
If you see suppository → the route belongs in the patient label, not merely in the dosage-form name.
Page 6

Worked suppository examples

  • 25 kg × 10 mg/kg/dose = 250 mg, matching the available 250 mg paracetamol suppository.
  • Glycerin adult suppository: 2.5 g, one suppository at bedtime as a single dose.
Page 6

Multiple-ingredient capsules

Use the powder form of each ingredient and compute the total amount of every drug. In the MEC example, 10 capsules are needed:

Chlorphenamine maleate: 2 mg × 10 = 20 mg
Phenylephrine HCl: 10 mg × 10 = 100 mg

If the combined dose is too small—12 mg per capsule in this example—an excipient such as lactose may be added.

Multiple-ingredient powders

The antacid example combines dried aluminum hydroxide gel 600 mg, magnesium trisilicate 1 g, and aromatic powder. For 15 doses:

600 mg × 15 = 9 g
1 g × 15 = 15 g
  • The combined single dose exceeds 600 mg, so papers are used.
  • Aromatic powder adds flavor and has no therapeutic action in the lecture example.
  • The lecture stresses simplicity and rationality when combining drugs.
⚠️ Rounding trap
The lecture allows rounding of selected non-potent doses for pharmacist convenience, but explicitly warns: never round off a very potent drug.

Patient factor can override the usual package

In the Mat Tiu example, ibuprofen 200 mg + paracetamol 325 mg gives a 525 mg dose, which would normally fit the capsule rule. Because the patient has difficulty swallowing large tablets, the lecture still uses powder papers.

Page 8
Inhaled solid preparations

Solid aerosols

The active ingredient is in micropulverized form and delivered by a specific inhaler device. The prescription must pair the drug with clear device instructions.

Page 8

Metered-dose inhaler

The lecture’s terbutaline inhaler contains 400 doses, each puff delivering 250 mcg. The label instructs 1–2 puffs every 6 hours round the clock for 3 days.

✅ Key point
Specify the frequency and give instructions appropriate to the device.
Page 8

Dry inhalation powder

Umeclidinium is written as 62.5 mcg/actuation using the Ellipta device, one puff once daily for one month.

🔬 Recognition cue
If the device is named → carry the device into the inscription and write the actuation-based label.
Page 8

Devices named in the lecture

Powder inhaler forms include rotacap, rotahaler, turbohaler, and dischaler. Aerosol inhalers include handihaler and the metered-dose inhaler.

Lab practical guide

How to build the prescription

Use this as a writing checklist when the case gives you a patient, dose, frequency, duration, and available preparation.

Pages 1–8

Seven-step build

1. Copy patient identifiersName, address, age; include sex where the template shows it.
2. Mark RxStart the medication order under the superscription.
3. Write the inscriptionGeneric drug name + dosage form + strength or total quantity.
4. Compute the quantityUse dose × frequency × duration, then divide by available strength when needed.
5. Write subscription“Dispense No. …,” “Divide into … equal parts,” or “Make No. …” as appropriate.
6. Write the patient labelState amount, route, frequency, timing, duration, and relevant indication.
7. Complete prescriber detailsSignature, M.D., PRC, PTR, and S2 for regulated drugs.
Pages 1–8

Template: ready-made tablet/capsule

Rx [Generic drug] [tablet/capsule] [strength] Dispense No. [number] Label: Take [number] [tablet/capsule] [route] [frequency/timing] for [duration].
Pages 2–5

Template: multiple-dose powder

Rx [Drug 1 powder] [total amount] [Drug 2 powder] [total amount] Mix and divide into [N] equal parts and dispense as [papers/capsules]. Label: [How to take one unit] [frequency] for [duration].
Pages 5–8

Template: single-dose powder

Rx [Drug powder] [dose per unit] For one [paper/capsule]. Make No. [N]. Label: [How to take one unit] [frequency] for [duration].
Pages 4–7

Route-specific finish

  • Suppository: state insertion route explicitly.
  • Aerosol: state puff/actuation, inhaler device, and frequency.
  • Regulated drug: add S2 and “No refills.”
Pages 2, 6, 8

Common omissions to catch before submitting

  • Missing dosage form in the inscription.
  • Correct dose but wrong number to dispense.
  • Label missing route, frequency, timing, or duration.
  • Confusing subscription with signatura.
  • Writing 0.3 g instead of 300 mg, .5 ml instead of 0.5 ml, or cc instead of ml.
  • Forgetting S2/No refills for a regulated drug.
  • Using capsules despite a dose ≥600 mg, unless the patient-specific example directs otherwise.

Last-minute review

Cram sheet

Ultra-condensed rules, formulas, and “if you see X → think Y” cues from the lecture.

Pages 1–9

Parts

  • Rx = superscription.
  • Inscription = drug/form/strength.
  • Subscription = pharmacist.
  • Signatura = patient label.
  • Finish with signature, PRC, PTR, and S2 when required.

Formulas

Total doses = frequency/day × days
Total drug = dose/intake × total doses
No. units = total drug ÷ unit strength

Metric safety

  • ≥1 g → grams
  • <1 g → mg
  • <1 mg → mcg
  • Leading zero: 0.5
  • Use ml, not cc

If you see X → think Y

  • Regulated drug → S2 + No refills.
  • Ready-made strength matches → use it.
  • <600 mg powder → capsule.
  • ≥600 mg powder → paper.
  • Very potent drug → single-dose method; never round.
  • Suppository → write insertion route.
  • Inhaler/device → write puff/actuation + device instructions.

Common traps

  • Do not confuse subscription and signatura.
  • Do not omit the dosage form.
  • Do not write 0.3 g when 300 mg is clearer.
  • Do not use .5 without a leading zero.
  • Do not round a very potent drug.
  • Do not forget total quantity after computing the dose.

⭐ Final must-know
A correct prescription is not just the right drug. It must also show the right form, strength, total quantity, pharmacist instruction, patient label, route, frequency, duration, and prescriber details.
