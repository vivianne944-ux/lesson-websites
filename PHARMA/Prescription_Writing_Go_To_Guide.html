Master workflow

One prescription, five moves.

Every case becomes easier when you follow the same sequence: extract the order, classify the preparation, calculate, write, then check.

1. ExtractPatient, drug, dose, route, frequency, duration, available preparation.
2. ClassifyTablet? Compounded powder? Oral suspension? Injection? Drops?
3. CalculateUnits, total mass, total volume, or number of containers.
4. WriteInscription → subscription → signatura.
5. CheckGeneric name, dosage form, strength, quantity, directions, credentials.

Solids pp.1–2

Patient details

Name, age, sex, address, date, and weight when the dose is weight-based. Age is especially important in children and older adults.

Medication identity

Generic name, dosage form, route, prescribed dose, and the strength or concentration that is actually available.

Administration plan

Frequency, timing, whether it is round the clock or as needed, and the total duration in days, weeks, or months.

Prescription Writing: Solids

Work through the exact solid categories used in the trans: single-ingredient tablet, capsule, powder, suppository, multiple-ingredient capsule, multiple-ingredient powder, and aerosol.

A. Solid, Single Ingredient, Tablet

Ready-made tablet strength satisfies the required dose.

+
Ready-made unitSingle-dose methodDispense number of tablets
Total dose = dose per intake × doses/day × number of days Number of tablets = total dose ÷ strength per tablet

Writing pattern

Inscription: Generic name + tablet + strength.

Subscription: Dispense No. ___ tablets.

Label: Take ___ tablet(s) orally every ___ hours / ___ times daily for ___ days.

High-yield checks

Always state the dosage form. Use a tablet strength that matches the required dose whenever possible.

Regulated drug: Include the appropriate S2 number and indicate No refills.
Worked example — Paracetamol 500 mg

500 mg × 4 doses/day × 3 days = 6,000 mg. Then 6,000 mg ÷ 500 mg/tablet = 12 tablets.

Weight-based tablet example — Clarithromycin

15 mg/kg/day × 33 kg = 495 mg/day. Divide into 2 doses = 247.5 mg/dose. The trans rounds to the available 250 mg tablet: 1 tablet twice daily × 7 days = 14 tablets.

Solids pp.2–3

B. Solid, Single Ingredient, Capsule

Use a ready-made capsule when it satisfies the required dose; compound only when needed.

+
Number of capsules = dose per intake × doses/day × days ÷ strength per capsule

Ready-made capsule

Write the generic name, capsule, strength, total number to dispense, and complete patient directions.

When a custom capsule is needed

If a ready-made preparation is inadequate for the patient’s required dose, use the powder form and instruct the pharmacist to make capsules.

Worked example — Tacrolimus

1 mg × 2/day × 60 days = 120 mg. At 1 mg/capsule, dispense 120 capsules.

Worked example — Carbocisteine

500 mg × 3/day × 7 days = 10,500 mg. At 500 mg/capsule, dispense 21 capsules.

Solids pp.3–5

C. Solid, Single Ingredient, Powder

Usually written by the multiple-dose method and divided into papers or capsules.

+
Total number of doses = doses/day × days Total amount = dose per intake × total number of doses

Multiple-dose method

Write the total amount, then instruct: “Divide into ___ equal parts and dispense as papers/capsules.” The physician performs the computation.

Single-dose method

Writes the amount in each individual unit. The trans states this may be preferred for a very potent drug.

General packaging rule from the trans: If the weight of one dose is < 600 mg, use capsules. If it is ≥ 600 mg, use papers—unless the patient’s needs require otherwise.
Worked example — Magnesium trisilicate

1 g three times daily for 5 days: 3 × 5 = 15 doses; 1 g × 15 = 15 g total. Divide into 15 equal parts and dispense as papers.

Weight-based powder example — Phenoxymethylpenicillin

15 kg × 10 mg/kg/dose = 150 mg/dose. Four doses/day × 7 days = 28 doses. 150 mg × 28 = 4,200 mg or 4.2 g.

Solids pp.5–6

D. Solid, Single Ingredient, Suppository

Directions must state exactly where and how the suppository is inserted.

+

Required wording

Use Insert, not “take.” The trans accepts:

• Insert suppository per rectum
• Insert suppository into the anus

Quantity

Calculate the number of suppositories from frequency × days, unless it is explicitly a single dose.

Worked example — Glycerin suppository

Single adult dose, 2.5 g at bedtime: write Dispense No. 1 suppository; label: “Insert 1 suppository into the rectum at bedtime as a single dose.”

Solids p.6

E. Solid, Multiple Ingredients, Capsule

Compute the total amount of every ingredient, then mix and divide into equal capsules.

+
Total amount of each ingredient = dose per capsule × total number of capsules

Use the powder form of each drug. If the total amount per capsule is too small, an excipient such as lactose may be added.

Worked example — Chlorpheniramine + phenylephrine

Twice daily for 5 days = 10 capsules. Chlorpheniramine 2 mg × 10 = 20 mg. Phenylephrine 10 mg × 10 = 100 mg. Mix and divide into 10 equal parts and dispense as capsules.

Single-dose-method example — Trimethoprim + sulfamethoxazole

Write the amount intended for one capsule, then instruct “For one capsule. Make No. 14.”

Solids pp.6–7

F. Solid, Multiple Ingredients, Powder

Compute the total quantity of every component and divide into papers.

+
Total amount of each ingredient = dose per intake × total number of doses

The inscription may include a basis, adjuvant, and an optional flavoring powder. When the combined single dose exceeds 600 mg, the trans uses papers.

Never round off the dose of a very potent drug. The trans specifically warns against rounding drugs given in microgram doses.
Worked example — Ibuprofen + paracetamol powder

Every 6 hours for 5 days = 20 doses. Ibuprofen 200 mg × 20 = 4,000 mg. Paracetamol 325 mg × 20 = 6,500 mg. Mix and divide into 20 papers.

Solids pp.7–8

G. Solid, Single Ingredient, Aerosol

Specify the device, strength per actuation/puff, frequency, and number of devices.

+

The trans includes metered-dose inhalers and dry-powder inhalers. The inscription should identify the drug, device/form, and strength per puff or actuation.

Label essentials

State the number of puffs, route by inhalation, frequency, duration, and indication when needed.

Device examples in the trans

Metered-dose inhaler, Ellipta device, rotacap, rotahaler, turbohaler, dischaler, handihaler.

Worked example — Umeclidinium

62.5 mcg/actuation through an Ellipta device, one puff once daily for one month; dispense one device.

Prescription Writing: Liquids & Parenterals

Use the exact categories from the liquid trans: single-ingredient oral, multiple-ingredient oral, solution for parenteral use, dry solids for reconstitution, liquid aerosol, and ophthalmic/otic preparations.

A. Liquid, Single Ingredient, Oral

Includes teaspoonful dosing, drops, stock solutions, syrups, suspensions, and oral drops.

+

1) Teaspoonful

Total amount = volume per intake × total number of doses
Wording: Write teaspoonful, not “teaspoon.” One teaspoonful = 5 mL.

2) Drops

The amount may be prescribed using a stock solution, but the trans stresses the use of an Official Medicine Dropper when accuracy depends on drops.

Official dropper: 3 mm external diameter at delivery; held vertically, it delivers 20 drops of water per mL. Other liquids may have different drops/mL.

3) Ready-made stock solution / suspension

Total daily dose = weight × prescribed mg/kg/day Dose per administration = total daily dose ÷ doses/day Volume per dose = ordered dose ÷ available dose × available volume Total volume = mL per dose × doses/day × days Number of bottles = total volume ÷ bottle volume → round up
Worked example — Amoxicillin oral drops

7.5 kg × 40 mg/kg/day = 300 mg/day. Divide by 3 = 100 mg/dose. At 100 mg/mL, give 1 mL/dose. For 10 days: 1 × 3 × 10 = 30 mL. With 10-mL bottles, dispense 3 bottles.

Worked example — Clarithromycin suspension

20 kg × 15 mg/kg/day = 300 mg/day. Divide by 2 = 150 mg/dose. At 250 mg/5 mL, 150 mg = 3 mL. Total: 3 mL × 2 × 7 = 42 mL. A 60-mL bottle is sufficient: dispense 1 bottle.

Liquids p.2

B. Liquid, Multiple Ingredient, Oral

Compute the total amount of each ingredient and the final bulk.

+
Total number of doses = doses/day × days Total amount of each drug = dose per intake × total number of doses Total bulk = volume per intake × total number of doses

Write the active ingredients first, then the vehicle. Follow the final ingredient with “to make” and the desired final volume.

Extemporaneous / magistral order

The preparation is compounded according to the physician’s formula.

Official prescription order

Calls for a preparation with a definite formula in the pharmacopeia or National Formulary.

Worked example — Dextromethorphan + ammonium chloride

20 doses total. Dextromethorphan: 10 mg × 20 = 200 mg. Ammonium chloride: 300 mg × 20 = 6 g. One teaspoonful × 20 = 100 mL total bulk. Add vehicle “to make 100 mL.”

Flavoring as a percentage of total bulk

For a 150-mL final volume with flavoring syrup at 20%: 150 mL × 0.20 = 30 mL syrup.

Vehicle types listed in the trans: aqueous, hydroalcoholic, and alcoholic vehicles.
Liquids pp.3–5

C. Liquid, Solution, Parenteral

Ready-made injectable solution in an ampule, vial, or prefilled syringe.

+
Volume to administer = ordered dose ÷ dose on hand × volume on hand

State the official drug name, concentration, container, number to dispense, route, dose volume, frequency, and timing.

Worked example — Midazolam

Ordered 1.5 mg; available 5 mg/mL. (1.5 ÷ 5) × 1 mL = 0.3 mL. Label: inject 0.3 mL (1.5 mg) by slow IV push at the specified time.

Ready-made single-dose examples

Diphenhydramine 50 mg/mL: dispense 1 ampule and inject 1 mL IM as a single dose. Filgrastim 300 mcg/0.5 mL: dispense 1 prefilled syringe and inject the full 0.5 mL subcutaneously as a single dose.

Liquids pp.5–6

D. Liquid, Solution/Suspension from Dry Solids, Parenteral

Dry sterile powder that must be reconstituted before injection.

+

Reconstitution means adding a diluent to a dry solid to produce a liquid. When the final product is a solution, begin the instruction with “Dissolve.”

Dry sterile drug
Add specified diluent
Dissolve / swirl
Withdraw required volume
Inject by stated route
Skin-test instruction in the trans: “Inject only after a negative skin test” is generally included for parenteral drugs likely to cause hypersensitivity, especially antimicrobials, except in life-threatening or emergency situations.
Worked example — Ceftazidime

1 g every 8 hours for 3 days = 9 doses. Dispense 9 vials. Dissolve each vial in 3 mL sterile water, further dilute as specified, then infuse for 30 minutes.

Worked example — Ceftriaxone

20 kg × 50 mg/kg/day = 1,000 mg/day. Divide into 2 doses = 500 mg/dose. For 24 hours only: dispense 2 vials of 500 mg.

Liquids pp.7–8

E. Liquid, Aerosol

Nebules or respules administered using a nebulizer.

+
Number of nebules/respules = administrations/day × number of days

The label should specify the nebulizer, frequency, duration, and indication when needed.

Worked example — Budesonide respules

Every 12 hours for 2 days = 2 doses/day × 2 days = 4 respules.

Liquids p.8

F. Liquid, Ophthalmic/Otic Preparations

Specify the exact site: right, left, or both eyes/ears.

+
Drops/day = drops per site × number of sites × administrations/day Total volume = total drops × (1/20 mL per drop, when using the trans equivalent) Number of bottles = total volume ÷ bottle volume → round up

Use Instill. For ophthalmic preparations, the trans specifies instillation into the conjunctival sac.

Two eye medications: The trans states that a 5-minute interval should be specified to avoid washing out the previously instilled drug.
Worked example — Ofloxacin otic drops

3 drops × 4/day = 12 drops/day. At 20 drops/mL, that is 0.6 mL/day. For 7 days = 4.2 mL; a 5-mL bottle is enough, so dispense 1 bottle.

Worked example — Prednisolone eye drops, both eyes

1 drop × 2 eyes × 3/day = 6 drops/day = 0.3 mL/day. For 5 days = 1.5 mL total.
