Whole-Lesson Overview

This lecture follows a drug from administration to membrane passage, absorption, systemic availability, and distribution into body compartments.

Pages 1–9

Core framework

Administration→ Absorption→ Plasma→ Distribution→ Site / storage / metabolism / excretion

Pharmacokinetics is how the body handles the drug and therefore dictates the drug’s fate. This differs from pharmacodynamics, which focuses on how the drug acts on the body.

⭐ MUST KNOW
The lecture repeatedly anchors the lesson on four relationships: unionized + lipid-soluble favors membrane passage; route of administration strongly influences absorption; AUC reflects systemic drug exposure; and protein/tissue binding changes apparent volume of distribution.

Permeation anchors

  • Aqueous diffusion and lipid diffusion follow Fick’s law.
  • Special carriers are selective, saturable, and inhibitable, and do not follow Fick’s law.
  • Endocytosis/exocytosis move especially impermeant or complexed substances.

Absorption anchors

  • Major determinant: route of administration.
  • Most drugs are absorbed in the small intestine.
  • Absorption improves with lipid solubility, suitable pH, vascularity, contact time, concentration, and surface area.

Distribution anchors

  • Free drug is active and distributable.
  • Bound drug acts as a reservoir.
  • Initial distribution follows blood flow; final distribution follows tissue affinity.

Pharmacokinetic Journey & Formulation

The lesson proper begins with how route changes the sequence a drug follows, then connects formulation choices to the barriers and clinical setting.

Pages 1–2

What happens after administration?

  • Oral: gut → liver → heart → lungs → aorta → arterial circulation. The lecture identifies oral administration as the safest route.
  • Parenteral / IV: vein → right side of heart → lungs → aorta → arterial circulation.
  • Inhaled: lungs → left side of heart → aorta → arterial circulation.
✅ LECTURE RELATIONSHIP
Duration by route: oral > parenteral > inhalation.

Four pharmacokinetic events

Absorption
Passage of drug from its administration site into circulation. The route of administration is the major influencing factor.
Distribution
After absorption, drug enters plasma as free/unbound or protein-bound. Free drug can reach the site of action, enter a storage depot, or undergo metabolism.
Metabolism
Also called biotransformation. The liver is the major metabolic machine; metabolism can create active or inactive metabolites and may make a drug more polar for excretion.
Elimination
Occurs through excretory organs. The kidney is the major excretory organ.

Drug formulation

Drug formulation combines chemical substances, including the active drug, into the final medicinal product and includes the dosage form.

  • Barriers: the drug must be capable of penetrating the needed membrane.
  • Setting: outpatient use may favor oral treatment, while a hospital setting may provide IV access.
  • Urgency: the clinical situation influences the needed route and formulation.
  • Stability: the product must retain appropriate characteristics.
  • First-pass effect: hepatic metabolism can reduce active-drug concentration.

High-yield recap

Route determines the early drug pathway. Once in plasma, the free–bound balance influences what is immediately available for distribution and effect.

Drug Permeation & Fick’s Law

Permeation is movement of drug molecules into and within the biologic environment.

Pages 2–3

What controls permeation?

  • Solubility: lipid-soluble drugs cross lipid bilayers; water-soluble drugs move through aqueous phases.
  • Concentration gradient: only free, unionized forms contribute to the gradient.
  • Surface area and vascularity: a larger surface area and greater vascularity support better absorption.
⭐ MUST KNOW
For passage through a lipid bilayer, the drug should be lipid-soluble and have a high lipid–water partition coefficient.

Passive mechanisms

Aqueous diffusion

Occurs through aqueous pores in interstitial space, cytosol, epithelial tight junctions, and vascular endothelium. It is driven by the concentration gradient.

Lipid diffusion

Moves drugs through lipid membranes separating body compartments. The lecture calls it the most important limiting factor for drug permeation. Absorption rises as lipid solubility rises.

Carrier and vesicular mechanisms

Special carriers are used by peptides, amino acids, glucose, ions, metabolites, neurotransmitters, and xenobiotics. They may use active transport or facilitated diffusion.

Endocytosis is membrane engulfment; exocytosis is the reverse process and supports secretion.

⚠️ DO NOT CONFUSE
Special carriers are not governed by Fick’s law. The lecture specifically links Fick’s law to aqueous and lipid diffusion.

Fick’s Law of Diffusion

Flux = (C1 − C2) × (Area × Permeability Coefficient) / Thickness
Flux

C1 − C2

🧠 APPLY THE RELATIONSHIP
Thinner membrane → faster absorption, illustrated by lungs > skin. Larger surface area → faster absorption, illustrated by intestines > stomach.

Weak Acids, Weak Bases & pH Partition

Ionization determines which fraction of a drug is lipid-soluble and able to diffuse across lipid membranes.

Page 3
⭐ MEMBRANE-CROSSING FORMS
Weak acid: lipid-soluble when protonated.
Weak base: lipid-soluble when unprotonated.
✅ UNIVERSAL ANCHOR
Unionized = lipid-soluble and diffusible.
Ionized = relatively water-soluble and poorly diffusible.

Henderson–Hasselbalch equation

pH = pKa + log [Unprotonated] / [Protonated]

For a weak acid, the ratio may be written as [A−]/[HA]. For a weak base, it may be written as [B]/[BH+].

  • pH < pKa: greater protonated fraction. A weak acid is more likely to be in its lipid-soluble form.
  • pH > pKa: greater unprotonated fraction. A weak base is more likely to be in its lipid-soluble form.

Mnemonics

LUNA Lipophilic • Unionized • Nonpolar • Absorbed

WHIPE Water-soluble/hydrophilic • Ionized • Polar • Excreted

⚠️ KEY DIFFERENTIATOR
“Protonated” and “unionized” are not interchangeable across acids and bases. A protonated weak acid is unionized, while a protonated weak base is ionized.

Lecture sample problem

Drug X is a weak acid with pKa 2.5 in stomach pH 1.5. The lecture calculates:

1.5 = 2.5 + log(U/P) → −1 = log(U/P) → 10⁻¹ = U/P = 0.1

Unprotonated fraction = 10%; protonated fraction = 90%. Because the drug is a weak acid, the protonated form is lipid-soluble. Therefore, the answer is 90%.

pH partition and ion trapping

Weak acids tend to accumulate in relatively higher-pH compartments, while weak bases show the reverse tendency.

  • Urinary acidification: accelerates excretion of weak bases and retards excretion of weak acids.
  • Urinary alkalinization: increases excretion of weak acids.
  • Aspirin poisoning: aspirin is a weak acid; increasing plasma/urine pH helps move it from CNS toward plasma and supports excretion.

Drug Absorption & Routes

Absorption is movement from the administration site into circulation, and most drugs are absorbed in the small intestine.

Pages 3–5

Routes of administration

Enteral: sublingual, oral, rectal Parenteral: IV, IM, SubQ, intrathecal Transdermal Inhalation Topical

Route affects both the rate of absorption and bioavailability.

Factors increasing absorption

  • Greater lipid solubility
  • Appropriate pH at the absorption site
  • Greater vascularity
  • Longer contact time
  • Higher drug concentration
  • Larger membrane surface area

Site-specific cues

  • Acidic site → favors absorption of a weak acid.
  • Basic site → favors absorption of a weak base.
  • The small intestine has larger surface area and greater vascularity than the stomach.
  • Small bowel resection reduces surface area and may reduce absorption.

Gastric emptying and intestinal motility

Most drugs are absorbed in the small intestine. The lecture states that if the stomach does not empty, absorption will be impaired; normal gastric emptying is described as around 4 hours.

Factors affecting gastric emptying include food—especially fat, drug size, blood supply, movement/mobility, and abdominal compartment pressure.

🧠 CLINICAL REASONING CUE
Faster small-bowel motility → less contact time → less absorption and bioavailability. The lecture uses diarrhea as an example.

For better stomach absorption

  • Small molecule
  • Weakly acidic
  • pKa greater than stomach pH, which is listed as 1.5–3
  • High concentration / large dose

Interactions within the GIT

  • Food may alter gastric emptying and drug contact.
  • Other drugs: calcium- and magnesium-containing antacids can chelate tetracycline and render it ineffective.
  • Bacterial growth may affect drug handling.
  • Patient status: age, gender, and nutrition can alter bioavailability.

Bioavailability & AUC

Bioavailability is the fraction of unchanged drug reaching systemic circulation after administration by any route.

Pages 4–5

Area under the curve

AUC means the area under the blood concentration–time curve and represents the body’s exposure to the drug.

For a drug eliminated by first-order kinetics, the lecture states that AUC is proportional to the dose and extent of bioavailability.

⭐ BIOAVAILABILITY IS REDUCED BY
First-pass effect, presystemic redistribution, and incomplete absorption.

Absolute bioavailability

F = (AUC PO / dose PO) ÷ (AUC IV / dose IV)

The simplified lecture form, when doses are treated as comparable, is AUC oral / AUC IV. The IV formulation is the reference.

Three considerations

  1. Extent of absorption: lipophilicity, hydrophilicity, and P-glycoproteins.
  2. First-pass elimination: described by extraction ratio.
  3. Rate of absorption: determined by administration site and formulation.
ER = CL liver / Q

Higher ER → more hepatic metabolism → lower bioavailability. The lecture lists hepatic blood flow near 90 L/h for a 70-kg person.

Determining bioavailability

  • Plasma data: time of peak, peak concentration, AUC.
  • Urine data: total drug excreted.
  • Acute pharmacologic effect.
  • Clinical observation.
Bioavailability = AUC oral / AUC injected × 100

Factors affecting bioavailability

Biopharmaceutical
Dosage form, excipients, physicochemical properties, disintegration, dissolution.
Dissolution order
Solutions > suspensions > capsules > compressed tablets > timed-release tablets.
Physiologic
Gastric acidity, gastric emptying, bowel motility, gut-wall absorption, first-pass effect, GIT perfusion.

Drug Distribution & Volume of Distribution

Distribution begins once the drug is in blood and is generally more rapid than elimination.

Pages 5–6

Body compartments listed in the lecture

  • Plasma: 5% of body weight
  • Interstitial fluid: 16%
  • Intracellular fluid: 35%
  • Transcellular fluid: 2%
  • Fat: 20%

Distribution and elimination occur simultaneously after drugs enter blood, but distribution is described as much more rapid and strongly influenced by regional blood flow and cardiac output.

Factors affecting distribution

  • Higher lipid solubility → better distribution
  • Smaller molecular size → more extensive distribution
  • Ionized drug → trapped in a compartment
  • Greater vascularity → better distribution
  • BBB and placental barriers
  • Plasma binding proteins

Volume of distribution (Vd)

Vd is the apparent volume of biologic fluid into which a drug appears to distribute homogeneously at the concentration measured in blood, plasma, or plasma water.

Vd = Total amount of drug in the body / Plasma concentration
  • Most drugs are listed as having Vd of 0.1–0.6 L/kg.
  • Tissue binding lowers plasma concentration and makes apparent Vd larger.
  • Plasma protein binding raises plasma concentration and makes apparent Vd smaller.
✅ FAST RELATIONSHIPS
↑ plasma protein binding → small Vd.
↑ extravascular/tissue binding → large Vd.
Acidic drugs tend to bind plasma proteins and have smaller Vd; basic drugs bind more extensively at extravascular sites and have larger Vd.

Low Vd

Drug remains largely within the vascular compartment. Lecture-listed contributors include:

  • High plasma protein binding
  • Very low lipid–water partition coefficient
  • Low membrane penetrability
  • Storage in adipose tissue

High Vd

Suggests extensive tissue binding and widespread distribution, with much higher concentrations outside the vascular space.

  • A higher dose may be required to reach a target plasma concentration.
  • Displacement from tissue sites can alter free drug; the lecture cites verapamil and quinidine displacing digoxin.
⚠️ LECTURE DISPLAY NOTE
The lecture’s numerical example displays 500 mg ÷ 500 mg/L = 100 L. This reviewer preserves the lecture’s displayed value as written.

Protein Binding, Compartments & Redistribution

The free–bound balance determines immediate activity, reservoir behavior, distribution, metabolism, and interaction risk.

Pages 6–7
⭐ DRUG FORMS IN BLOOD
Free form = active.
Protein-bound form = inactive reservoir.
Metabolites are usually the degraded form.
✅ BINDING PARTNERS
Acidic drugs → albumin.
Basic drugs → α1-acid glycoprotein.

Clinical significance of plasma protein binding

  • Delays onset of action
  • Prolongs duration
  • May require a higher dose
  • Serves as a reservoir
  • Can cause drug–drug interactions through displacement
  • Hypoalbuminemia reduces binding and increases free drug
  • Free drug is available for metabolism
🧠 IF YOU SEE HYPOALBUMINEMIA
Think less protein binding → more free drug, with increased potential effect or toxicity. Chronic renal failure is the lecture example.

Displacement example

Simultaneous drugs may compete for binding. The lecture highlights sulfonamide displacing bilirubin in infants, which may contribute to kernicterus.

Lecture-listed manifestations include jaundice, elevated bilirubin, cerebral palsy, hearing loss, and floppy baby syndrome.

Central compartment

Highly perfused organs—liver, kidney, heart, spleen—where drug equilibrates rapidly with plasma.

Peripheral compartment

Poorly perfused tissues—viscera, muscle, fat, bone—that pick up drug slowly and equilibrate more slowly.

Redistribution

Redistribution is movement from more highly perfused to less perfused tissues. The lecture example is thiopental: rapid equilibration with brain produces anesthesia within seconds, but the short anesthetic duration reflects redistribution into adipose tissue rather than rapid metabolism.

⚠️ DO NOT CONFUSE
A short clinical effect does not always mean rapid elimination. In the thiopental example, redistribution is the key explanation.

Distribution models

Single-compartment model: drug distributes “instantaneously,” remains central, and does not distribute to peripheral compartments.

Multiple-compartment model: drug moves from central to peripheral compartments before elimination.

  1. Distribution phase: plasma concentration falls while total body drug remains the same.
  2. Steady state: transition between distribution and elimination.
  3. Terminal elimination phase: drug is removed through liver and kidney; both total amount and plasma concentration change.

Storage Sites, CNS & Placental Permeation

The later lecture sections apply distribution principles to tissue depots and protected compartments.

Page 7

Storage sites

Adipose tissue
Stores lipid-soluble drugs such as thiopental. Poor perfusion and low metabolic rate can prolong drug duration.
Bone
Stores heavy metals such as arsenic, mercury, and lead, as well as tetracycline.
Muscle
Drugs may bind reversibly to nucleoproteins and phospholipids; quinacrine is the lecture example.
Organs
Aminoglycosides may enter by passive or active processes. Gentamicin is listed as stored in renal proximal tubular cells; aluminum is listed as stored in kidney, liver, and neurons.

Factors controlling retention

  • Blood flow to tissues
  • Cardiac output
  • Plasma protein binding
  • Tissue binding and competition
  • Lipid content of tissue
  • Lipid–water partition coefficient
  • Ionization and environmental pH
  • Molecular size

Warfarin and phenytoin are highlighted as highly protein-bound drugs with narrow therapeutic ranges and interaction risk.

Placental barrier

  • Most smaller-molecular-weight drugs can cross.
  • Fetal blood levels are usually lower than maternal levels.
  • Efflux transporters can move drug from fetus back to mother.
  • The lecture contrasts propylthiouracil and methimazole as an example.

Blood–brain and blood–CSF barriers

Penetration depends on ionization, lipid–water partition coefficient, molecular weight, and plasma protein binding.

  • Diffusion and specialized receptor/carrier transport may operate.
  • BBB is permeable mainly to lipid-soluble or very-low-molecular-weight drugs.
  • Higher lipid–water partition coefficient → better penetration.
🧠 INFLAMMATION CHANGES THE BARRIER
The lecture notes that some drugs penetrate the BBB more readily with meningeal inflammation. It cites penicillin and states that first- and second-generation cephalosporins generally do not cross, except cefuroxime, which is listed for meningitis.

Special compartments

CSF/CNS, pericardial fluid, bronchial secretions, and middle-ear fluid are identified as difficult-to-treat compartments because access can be limited.

✅ KEY DIFFERENTIATOR
Initial organ distribution depends mainly on local blood flow; final distribution depends more on tissue affinity.

⭐ THE BIG PICTURE
Route shapes absorption and bioavailability. Unionized, lipid-soluble drug crosses membranes. Once in blood, free drug distributes and acts, while bound drug serves as a reservoir. Distribution depends on blood flow, tissue affinity, barriers, and binding.

⚠️ FINAL EXAM TRAPS
Special carriers—not lipid or aqueous diffusion—are the mechanism explicitly excluded from Fick’s law. Do not call protein-bound drug active. Do not interpret high Vd as high plasma binding. Do not treat “protonated” as automatically unionized without first identifying whether the drug is a weak acid or weak base.
