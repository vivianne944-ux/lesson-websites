Prescription Writing: Liquids

This reviewer condenses the lecture into a practical writing-and-computation guide for liquid oral preparations, liquid mixtures, parenteral solutions, dry-solid reconstitutions, aerosols, and ophthalmic/otic preparations.

p. 1p. 2p. 3p. 4p. 5p. 6p. 7p. 8p. 9p. 10

What the lecture wants you to master

The stated goals are correctness of form and precision in dosage. For liquids, that means the prescription must clearly show the preparation/concentration, the amount or number of containers to dispense, and the patient-facing label with dose, frequency, route/device, and duration.

✅ Key pointComputation is not optional: liquid prescriptions repeatedly ask you to compute total doses, total volume/bulk, drops, bottles, ampules, vials, or respules.

Abbreviation quick scan

IU = International UnitsNF = National FormularyUSP = US PharmacopeiaNPO = Nothing by mouth

Page 10 also gives exam abbreviations: h.s. means at bedtime, and q.i.d. means four times a day.

Liquid, Single Ingredient, Oral

Drops: official medicine dropper and drops/mL vary by solution⌄

The lecture’s drop example uses Belladonna Tincture. The dose per take is computed as 0.6 mL × 45 drops/mL = 27 drops.

⭐ Must knowThe official medicine dropper is 3 mm in external diameter at its delivery and, when held vertically, delivers 20 drops of water.

Drug in stock solutions and ready-made bottles⌄

Stock solution logic

In the teaspoonful and drops samples, there is no well-defined subscription. The instruction to the pharmacist is incorporated in the inscription as the quantity in mL to be dispensed.

This applies when the drug is prescribed from stock solutions, because only the specified amount is dispensed.

Ready-made bottle logic

For ready-made liquid preparations, the physician must know the available preparation and calculate the appropriate number of bottles.

Total volume requiredvolume/dose × doses/day × days
Bottle counttotal required volume ÷ bottle volume → round up
If you see “available in 60 mL bottle” → compute total volume and divide by 60 mL. If total required is less than the only available bottle size → still dispense the available bottle.

Core topic B

Liquid, Multiple Ingredient, Oral

These prescriptions combine ingredients and use a vehicle to reach a final volume. The lecture emphasizes basis-adjuvant-vehicle order, total bulk, and “to make”.

p. 3p. 4p. 5

Order of inscription

The lecture’s cough mixture lists items as basis-adjuvant-vehicle. The drug ingredients are in powder form except the vehicle.

✅ Key pointThe final ingredient is generally the excipient/vehicle and is followed by “to make”, meaning add enough vehicle to reach the final bulk or volume.

Core computations

Total # dosesfrequency/day × duration
Total bulk/volumesingle-dose volume × total # doses
Total dose of each drugdose per intake × total # doses

Example from page 3: 1 teaspoonful = 5 mL; 20 doses gives 100 mL total bulk.

Vehicles and their roles⌄
Aqueous vehicle

Used for water-soluble salts. Examples listed: aromatic water, syrup, and mucilage.

Hydroalcoholic vehicle

Used for substances relatively soluble in both water and alcohol. Example: elixir.

Alcoholic vehicle

Used for substances soluble in strong alcohol or as flavor added to syrups/elixirs. Examples: tincture, spirit.

⚠️ PitfallA vehicle usually adds bulk, acts as solvent, flavoring, or coloring; in some instances, it may exert corrective or therapeutic action, as the lecture notes for Terpin Hydrate.
Extemporaneous/magistral vs official prescription order⌄

Extemporaneous or magistral

The prescription is compounded according to the physician’s formula.

Official

The prescription calls for preparations with definite formulae for compounds in the pharmacopeia or National Formulary.

⭐ Must knowThe lecture warns that official or ready-made preparations can sacrifice adjustment of the individual drugs in the formula for convenience.
Worked examples from the lecture⌄

Tibby Khaleesis

For 1 month: 30 doses, 150 mL bulk, isoniazid 6 g, pyridoxine HCl 0.3 g, and strawberry syrup 30 mL.

Altha Presyon

Valsartan 2.4 g, amlodipine 0.15 g, orange syrup 30 mL. The lecture labels valsartan as basis, amlodipine as adjuvant, orange syrup as corrective.

Sara Turete

Every 8 hours for 10 days gives 30 doses, 150 mL total bulk, cefuroxime 7.5 g, bromhexine 120 mg, grape syrup 60 mL.

Core topic C

Liquid, Solution, Parenteral

This section is about injectable liquids in ampules or pre-filled syringes. The prescription must identify the official preparation name, concentration/amount per unit, and number dispensed.

p. 5p. 6

What the lecture says to note

  • Use the official name of the preparation.
  • After the official name, state the concentration and/or amount contained in one ampule.
  • Follow this with the number to be dispensed.
  • The dose per injection is determined by the physician, using the usual dose in the U.S.P. as the starting point.

Dose-volume formula

For the midazolam practice problem, the lecture uses:

Desired dose(ordered dose amount ÷ amount on hand) × quantity

Example: (1.5 mg / 5 mg) × 1 mL = 0.3 mL.

⚠️ PitfallIf the scenario says write in triplicate, copy that requirement into the prescription task context.
If you see “ampule” → think concentration per mL/ampule + number of ampules. If you see “pre-filled syringe” → the label should direct how and where to inject it.
Practice-pattern cues⌄

Diphenhydramine

Single STAT dose of 50 mg solution for injection intramuscularly; available as 50 mg/mL ampule.

Filgrastim

300 mcg in 0.5 mL solution in a pre-filled syringe; given subcutaneously at arm or abdomen.

Midazolam

5 mg/mL ampule; order 1.5 mg slow IV push; computed volume 0.3 mL.

Dexamethasone

6 mg every 12 hours for 4 doses; lecture computes 1.2 ampule ≈ 2 ampules per dose for Dexamet 5 mg/mL.

Core topic D

Liquid Solutions/Suspensions from Dry Solids, Parenteral

These drugs start as dry sterile solids and become liquids after adding a diluent. The most exam-visible word here is “dissolve.”

p. 7p. 8

Reconstitution rules from the lecture

  • Reconstitution means adding a diluent to a dry solid ingredient to make it a liquid.
  • For the penicillin sample, the amount of diluent is ideally 1.5 mL, but may range between 1-2 mL.
  • When the solid produces a solution once reconstituted, the label should start with “dissolve.”
⭐ Must knowExam cue: the word used to emphasize that a solution is produced after reconstitution is “Dissolve.”

Skin-test instruction

The lecture states that “Inject only after a negative skin test” should generally be placed in all parenteral drugs, especially drugs likely to produce hypersensitivity reactions such as antimicrobials.

⚠️ ExceptionThe lecture names an exception: when prescribing a parenteral drug for life-threatening or emergency situations.
Reconstitution and infusion patterns⌄
RSV vaccine

Withdraw the adjuvant suspension, transfer it to the lyophilized antigen powder, gently swirl until dissolved, then withdraw 0.5 mL and administer IM.

Ceftazidime

1 g every 8 hours for 3 days gives 9 doses and 9 g. Reconstitute with 3 mL sterile water, dilute with 50 mL D5W or NSS, infuse for 30 minutes.

Ceftriaxone

20 kg × 50 mg/kg/day = 1000 mg/day; in 2 divided doses = 500 mg/dose; prescription dispenses 2 vials.

If you see dry sterile powder → think diluent + dissolve/reconstitute label. If you see antimicrobial parenteral → think negative skin test instruction, unless the lecture’s emergency exception applies.

Core topics E-F

Liquid Aerosol and Ophthalmic/Otic Preparations

This tab covers liquids delivered through nebulizers, plus eye and ear drops where site-specific instructions matter.

p. 8p. 9

Liquid aerosol

The lecture sample uses Salbutamol Solution in nebules, administered with a nebulizer. A tube or mask is placed at the nostrils and mouth, and the patient breathes the mist formed.

⭐ Must knowInstructions should specify frequency, duration, and if needed, the indication for use such as PRN attack, maintenance once daily, or round the clock.
Respules/nebules to dispensefrequency of administration × duration

Ophthalmic and otic

The drug is in a solution placed in plastic containers. Ophthalmic drugs are instilled in the conjunctival sac; some are gel ointment forms applied to the conjunctival sac.

✅ Key pointIf another eye drug is prescribed, specify a 5-minute interval to avoid wash out of the previously instilled drug.
Drops computation patterns⌄

Otic example: Dennis

Ofloxacin left ear, 2-3 drops every 6 hours for 1 week. The lecture computes with 3 drops × 4 times/day = 12 drops/day; using 1 drop = 1/20 mL, total is 4.2 mL, so 1 bottle of 5 mL.

Ophthalmic example: Brylle Yant

Prednisolone Na phosphate, 1 drop to each eye TID for 5 days. Drops/day = 1 × 2 eyes × 3 = 6 drops/day; total = 1.5 mL for 5 days.

If you see right eye/left ear/both eyes → write the exact site in the label. If you see another ophthalmic drug → include the 5-minute interval cue.

Lab practical guide

Recognition Cues and Short-Answer Templates

Use this as a practical “what am I looking at?” guide when a prescription plate, problem stem, or computation appears.

p. 1p. 2p. 3p. 4p. 5p. 6p. 7p. 8p. 9

How to identify the prescription type fast

  • If you see “teaspoonful” → oral liquid household measure; 1 teaspoonful = 5 mL.
  • If you see “drops” → use official medicine dropper and solution-specific drops/mL.
  • If you see concentration like 100 mg/mL in 10 mL bottles → compute volume per dose then bottle count.
  • If you see multiple ingredients and syrup/elixir → compute total bulk and each ingredient’s total dose.
  • If you see ampule/pre-filled syringe → write for injection, route, and exact volume/dose.
  • If you see dry powder/vial → write dissolve and include diluent/reconstitution directions.
  • If you see nebule/respule → include nebulizer directions.
  • If you see ophthalmic/otic → specify eye/ear and compute drops.

Short-answer computation template

  1. Write the daily dose or dose per intake from the stem.
  2. Compute dose per administration if the dose is divided.
  3. Convert to volume per dose using the available concentration.
  4. Compute total volume or total number of dose units.
  5. Divide by bottle/ampule/vial size and round up when containers are counted.
  6. Write the label with route/site/device, frequency, and duration.

Last-minute review

Cram Sheet

Ultra-condensed formulas, must-know definitions, and exam cues from the lecture. No quiz, just what to recall fast.

p. 1p. 2p. 3p. 4p. 5p. 6p. 7p. 8p. 9p. 10

Household and drops

  • 1 drop = 1/20 mL
  • 1 teaspoonful = 5 mL
  • 1 tablespoon = 15 mL
  • 1 glassful = 250 mL
  • Official dropper: 3 mm, delivers 20 drops of water vertically.

Core formulas

  • Total # doses = frequency/day × days
  • Total amount = dose per intake × total # doses
  • Total volume = volume/dose × doses/day × days
  • Bottles = total volume ÷ bottle volume → round up
  • Drops = mL × drops/mL

Multiple oral

  • Inscription order: basis-adjuvant-vehicle.
  • Vehicle final line uses “to make” final volume.
  • Total bulk = single-dose volume × total # doses.
  • Extemporaneous = physician formula.
  • Official = pharmacopeia/NF formula.

Parenteral

  • Use official name.
  • State concentration/amount per ampule.
  • Then state number dispensed.
  • Desired dose formula: (ordered dose/amount on hand) × quantity.

Dry solid parenteral

  • Reconstitution = adding diluent to dry solid to make liquid.
  • When solution forms, label starts with “Dissolve.”
  • Penicillin sample: diluent ideally 1.5 mL, range 1-2 mL.
  • Generally add Inject only after a negative skin test, especially antimicrobial parenterals.

Aerosol + eye/ear

  • Nebules/respules use nebulizer instructions.
  • Respules to dispense = frequency × duration.
  • Ophthalmic: instill in conjunctival sac.
  • If another eye drug: specify 5-minute interval to avoid wash out.
  • Always specify right/left/both eye or ear.
