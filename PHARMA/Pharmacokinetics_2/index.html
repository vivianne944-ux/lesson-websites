Renal logic

Free, small molecules are filtered; carrier systems secrete drugs; uncharged lipid-soluble drugs may be reabsorbed.

Metabolic logic

Metabolism may produce inactive, active, or toxic metabolites and may activate a prodrug.

Monitoring logic

Peak, trough, steady state, therapeutic range, and the therapeutic index help detect inefficacy or toxicity.

Renal route and determinants

Drug Excretion

Excretion is the mode of elimination for drugs that are not metabolized and may remove the unchanged drug or its metabolites.

p. 1

Forms and routes

  • Forms: unchanged drug or drug metabolites.
  • Major route: kidneys.
  • Other routes: GIT through biliary excretion and feces, pulmonary route, saliva, sweat, milk, and tears.

Elimination versus excretion

The lecture notes that elimination of a chemical molecule may not terminate action when the drug has active metabolites. Excretion specifically removes drug material from the body.

Glomerulus
Passive filtration of small, free molecules.
→
Proximal tubule
Active secretion through carrier systems.
→
Distal tubule
Predominant passive reabsorption of uncharged drugs.
→
Urine
Polar, ionized, water-soluble drug remains for excretion.

Filtration

  • Influenced by molecular size: larger molecules are harder to filter.
  • Influenced by plasma protein binding: more binding means less filtration.
  • The glomerulus acts as a dialyzing membrane.
  • Approximately 20% of unbound drug is filtered.
  • Molecules with MW <20,000 may diffuse through the filtrate; albumin MW is about 68,000.

Secretion

  • Active process, mostly in the PCT.
  • Uses transporters; therefore it is saturable and subject to competition.
  • Accounts for excretion of about 80% of ionized drug forms.
  • Weak-base carrier system: natural substrate histamine.
  • Weak-acid carrier system: natural substrate uric acid.

Reabsorption

  • Passive simple diffusion driven by a concentration gradient.
  • Ionized drugs do not diffuse passively.
  • Uncharged drugs may diffuse out of the tubular fluid and escape elimination.
  • Polar, water-soluble, more ionized drugs are readily excreted.
Lecture-Specific Note
Inulin clearance is used to estimate GFR because inulin is neither reabsorbed nor secreted.

Removal from the body

Drug Elimination and Clearance

Drug elimination is an irreversible shift of drug from one part of the body to another and has two major components.

pp. 1–2

Two components

  • Excretion: removal of the intact drug.
  • Biotransformation: conversion of drug into metabolites, described here as inactive in the elimination framework.

Factors affecting elimination

  1. Organ perfusion
  2. Protein binding
  3. Metabolism
  4. Polarity and size
  5. Clearance
  6. Disease or pathological state
  7. Genetic variations

Drug clearance

Clearance is the volume of blood cleared of drug per unit time. It relates the rate of elimination to the plasma concentration and defines the administration rate needed to maintain a plateau concentration.

Clearance = Rate of elimination ÷ Plasma concentration

At steady state, clearance remains constant because drug input equals drug output. The lecture identifies clearance as the most important parameter for obtaining the dosage rate; disturbance may lead to toxicity.

Clinical Cue
When elimination slows, expect drug accumulation, a prolonged half-life, and a greater need for dose adjustment or monitoring.
Biotransformation map

Drug Metabolism: Foundations

Biotransformation is the chemical conversion of a drug, usually into a more polar or water-soluble form.

p. 2

Active metabolite

  • Capable of pharmacologic activity.
  • May be reabsorbed.
  • Described as less polar, less ionized, and lipid-soluble.

Inactive metabolite

  • No pharmacologic activity.
  • Prepared for excretion.
  • More polar, more water-soluble, and more ionized.

Sites of drug-metabolizing enzymes

Main site: liver. Other tissue sites include the lungs, intestinal mucosa, skin, placenta, and kidney. Subcellular sites listed in the lecture include the endoplasmic reticulum, mitochondria, cytosol, lysosomes, nuclear envelope, and plasma membrane.

Phase I

Converts the parent drug to a more polar, water-soluble, more reactive product by unmasking or inserting a polar functional group.

Phase II

Conjugates subgroups to hydroxyl, amino, and sulfhydryl functions using endogenous compounds.

Key Differentiator
A drug may undergo Phase I then Phase II, or the sequence may be reversed. The lecture gives isoniazid as an example of Phase II preceding Phase I.
Nonsynthetic reactions

Phase I Metabolism

Phase I reactions directly modify the drug molecule through oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis, or deamination.

p. 2

Oxidation

  • Predominant Phase I reaction.
  • May be CYP450-dependent or independent.
  • CYP450 is concentrated in the smooth ER of liver cells and is not highly substrate-selective.
  • CYP3A4 is responsible for about 50% of drug metabolism in the lecture.
  • Examples: paracetamol N-oxidation and ibuprofen hydroxylation.

Reduction

  • Substrate gains electrons.
  • More likely when oxygen content is low.
  • May activate xenobiotics rather than detoxify them.
  • Uses cytoplasmic enzymes including hepatic nitroreductase.
  • Example: dantrolene nitroreduction in malignant hyperthermia.

Hydrolysis

  • Breaks a compound into parts.
  • May occur intracellularly in ER/cytoplasm or extracellularly in plasma.
  • Cholinesterases hydrolyze ester and amide drugs.
  • Procaine (ester) → pseudocholinesterase.
  • Lidocaine (amide) → liver microsomal CYP450.

Other Phase I content

  • Deamination is listed as a Phase I reaction.
  • Oxidation reactions include hydroxylation and dealkylation.
  • Cytochrome P450 isozymes are the major microsomal enzymes.

Synthetic/conjugation reactions

Phase II Metabolism

Phase II reactions conjugate a drug or its Phase I metabolite with an endogenous compound through transferases.

p. 2

Core conjugates and result

Drugs are conjugated with acetate, glucuronate, sulfate, or glycine. The parent drug or metabolite is coupled to endogenous substances in the ER or cytoplasm. Products typically become more polar, less lipid-soluble, water-soluble, inactive, and readily excreted.

Glucuronidation

  • Most common conjugation reaction.
  • Inducible.
  • May undergo enterohepatic cycling.
  • Examples: morphine and chloramphenicol.
  • Has low activity in neonates.

Acetylation

  • Shows fast and slow metabolizer genotypes.
  • The lecture links drug-induced SLE to acetylator status with hydralazine, clonazepam, and isoniazid.
  • Fast isoniazid acetylator: hepatotoxicity risk.
  • Slow metabolizer: peripheral neuropathy risk.

Glutathione conjugation

  • Protects hepatocytes and other cells.
  • GSH depletion is associated with acetaminophen hepatotoxicity.
  • Excess unconjugated acetaminophen forms NAPQI.
  • N-acetylcysteine is the antidote.

What metabolism changes

Effects of Drug Metabolism

Metabolism may increase renal elimination, alter pharmacologic activity, activate prodrugs, or generate toxic metabolites.

p. 3

Increase polarity

Highly polar drugs are poorly absorbed and transported across membranes but are readily excreted. Higher lipid solubility and lower polarity are associated with a longer half-life.

Increase water solubility

Addition of N, S, O, or halogen can render a drug more polar and rapidly excreted. Metabolism does not always increase water solubility.

Increase ionization

Many metabolites are more acidic and more ionized at physiologic pH. More ionization means more polarity and water solubility, promoting rapid excretion.

Reactive intermediates

  • Epoxides: highly reactive, form covalent bonds with macromolecules and may cause carcinogenesis, teratogenicity, or tissue damage.
  • Hydroxylamines: reducing agents that may act as antioxidants for fatty acids.

  • Activated oxygen/superoxides: contain superoxide ion; the lecture gives KO₂ as an example.
  • Free radicals: highly reactive, short-lived, uncharged molecules with unpaired valence electrons.
  • Antioxidants neutralize free radicals.
Important Warning
For excess paracetamol, the lecture gives N-acetylcysteine within 8–16 hours. For isoniazid toxicity, it lists pyridoxine.
Intrinsic modifiers

Factors Affecting Biotransformation

Age, physiology, nutrition, disease, temperature, and inherited enzyme differences alter the rate of metabolism.

p. 3

Age

  • Both extremes of age show slow metabolism.
  • Very young: metabolic sites are not fully developed; enzyme development occurs over about 1–2 months.
  • Chloramphenicol in babies: low glucuronyl transferase may lead to Gray Baby Syndrome with cyanosis, diarrhea, circulatory collapse, and possible death.
  • Very old: reduced tissue and enzyme activity, organ shrinkage, lower liver function, and reduced hepatic blood flow.
  • The lecture advises decreasing elderly doses, describing about half the standard adult dose.

Gender and hormones

  • Overall difference is described as limited, but certain drugs may be affected by hormones.
  • Estradiol generally inhibits enzyme degradation of certain drugs; estrogen induces enzymatic activity.
  • Testosterone promotes enzymatic degradation.
  • More fat tissue is linked to decreased metabolism; more lean tissue to faster oxidation.
  • The lecture states CYP3A4 activity is higher in women, while some other systems may be higher in men.
  • Men have higher BMR in the lecture; pregnancy is associated with an increased metabolic rate.

Nutrition

  • Malnutrition may cause amino acid depletion.
  • Reduced protein leads to fewer microsomal enzymes, slower biotransformation, longer half-life, higher drug levels, and possible toxicity.
  • Dose adjustment may be needed.

Disease and temperature

  • Liver disease: may cause drug toxicity because the liver is the main metabolic site.
  • Comorbid illness usually decreases metabolism.
  • Cardiac disease: slow distribution due to slow blood flow.
  • Hypothyroidism: slow metabolism; hyperthyroidism: fast metabolism.
  • Fever: may enhance metabolism; hypothermia: may decrease it.

External modifiers

Environment, Diet, and Drug–Drug Interactions

Environmental exposures, food, alcohol pattern, and enzyme induction or inhibition can raise or lower drug concentrations.

p. 4

Environmental factors

  • Cigarette/tobacco smoke: benzopyrene and other hydrocarbons induce CYP450 1A1.
  • Acute alcohol: enzyme inhibitor → decreased metabolism → prolonged and enhanced bioavailability.
  • Chronic alcohol: enzyme inducer → increased metabolism → reduced effectiveness.
  • Pesticides/DDT/PCBs: increased metabolism.
  • Chronic lead exposure: decreased metabolism.
  • High altitude: reduced oxygen, reduced oxidation, slower metabolism.

Diet

  • Charcoal-broiled food: increased metabolism; contains benzopyrene.
  • Cabbage, sprouts, Brussels sprouts: increased metabolism.
  • Chocolate, coffee, tea, cola: decreased metabolism.
  • Caffeine and theobromine are listed as methylxanthine derivatives.

Enzyme induction

  • Increases metabolism through increased CYP450 synthesis.
  • Decreases levels and effect of the non-inducer drug.
  • Several days are usually required for maximum induction.
  • Examples in text: phenobarbital and testosterone.

Enzyme inhibition

  • Decreases metabolism.
  • Raises free drug level and increases effect or toxicity risk.
  • Examples: cimetidine, ketoconazole, chloramphenicol, estradiol.
Key Differentiator
Inducer = less drug and less effect. Inhibitor = more free drug and greater toxicity potential.

Clinical pharmacokinetics

First-Order and Zero-Order Kinetics

The lecture’s central hypothesis is that pharmacologic or toxic response is related to drug concentration in plasma or serum.

pp. 4–5

First-order kinetics

  • Most common type.
  • Linear and non-saturable.
  • A constant fraction or percentage is handled per unit time.
  • Rate is directly proportional to the concentration remaining.
  • Double dose → double concentration.
  • Concentration falls by 50% per half-life.
  • Elimination rate varies; half-life stays constant.

Zero-order kinetics

  • Also called Michaelis–Menten kinetics.
  • Non-linear and saturable.
  • Rate of elimination is constant regardless of concentration once mechanisms are saturated.
  • A constant amount is handled per unit time.
  • Elimination rate stays constant; half-life varies.
  • Margin of safety is narrow.
  • A small dose increase may produce toxic levels.
Monitoring Warning
Only a few drugs follow zero-order kinetics; the lecture emphasizes monitoring because small dose increases may cause toxicity.

Core dosing parameters

Half-Life and Clearance

Half-life links the drug’s apparent distribution space with the body’s ability to clear it.

p. 5

Half-life (t½)

  • Time for plasma concentration to fall by 50%.
  • Applies to first-order kinetics.
  • Function of volume of distribution and clearance.
  • Used to estimate time to steady state.
  • Steady state takes about 4–5 half-lives.
t½ = 0.693 × (Vd ÷ Cl)

Vd and half-life

  • Only drug in the central compartment can be eliminated, mainly by the liver and kidneys.
  • At constant clearance, a drug with a higher Vd has a longer elimination half-life.
  • A lower Vd corresponds to a shorter half-life when clearance is unchanged.

Clearance formulas

Cl = Rate of elimination ÷ Plasma concentration
Total systemic Cl = Clrenal + Clhepatic + Clothers

Clearance is the body’s ability to eliminate drug from blood and is the most important concept for designing a rational administration regimen.

Bioavailability

Fraction of drug reaching the circulation unchanged.

Volume of distribution

Apparent space in the body to which drug is uniformly distributed at a concentration equal to plasma.

Clearance

Measure of the body’s ability to eliminate the drug.

Concentration over dosing intervals

Peak, Trough, and Steady State

Repeated dosing creates alternating concentration peaks and valleys until drug input and output balance.

pp. 5–6

Peak level

Highest plasma concentration reached after each dose.

  • IV: within a few minutes.
  • Oral: about 1–2 hours.

Trough / valley level

Lowest plasma concentration between dosing intervals. The lecture identifies this as the best time to obtain blood samples.

Steady-state level

Equilibrium between the dose entering and amount eliminated. Achieved after about 4 half-lives in this section; wait four half-lives before drawing blood.

Effect of repeated doses

  • For first-order drugs, concentration increases exponentially with repeated doses until a plateau.
  • The plateau is the steady-state plasma concentration.
  • At steady state, there are no further upward or downward shifts in the peak and trough pattern when doses are given regularly and none are added or missed.
Exam Trap
Peak is the highest level after a dose; trough is the lowest level before the next dose. The lecture links trough sampling with the best blood-sampling time.
Rapid target and sustained target

Loading and Maintenance Doses

A loading dose rapidly reaches the target concentration; a maintenance dose keeps the concentration within the desired range.

pp. 5–6

→

→
Maintenance dose
Replace drug eliminated over each interval.
→
Steady state
Maintain the optimal therapeutic range.

Loading dose

  • Single dose or series of doses to rapidly achieve target concentration.
  • Used when waiting four half-lives is undesirable.
  • For most drugs, may be given as a single dose by the selected route.
  • Depends on elimination half-life, dosing interval, and target concentration.
  • Followed by a maintenance dose.
LD = Vd × Target concentration

Maintenance dose

  • Designed to achieve and sustain steady state in the optimal therapeutic range.
  • Computed from clearance.
  • May be repetitive doses or continuous infusion.
  • Usually follows the loading dose or one half-life of elimination.
  • The lecture notes the amount is approximately half the loading dose.
Maintenance dose = Dosing rate × Dosing interval
Dosing rate = Clearance × Target concentration
Factors Affecting Dosage Rate
Volume of distribution, bioavailability, and plasma availability are listed as factors affecting dosage rate.
Do Not Confuse
Loading dose is Vd-driven in the lecture formula. Maintenance dose is clearance-driven.
Safety and monitoring

Therapeutic Range, Therapeutic Index, and TDM

Therapeutic drug monitoring helps detect toxicity, guide acute or maintenance therapy, and adjust doses when pharmacokinetics are uncertain.

p. 6

Therapeutic range / window

  • Range between the minimum effective concentration or dose and the minimum toxic concentration or dose.
  • Above the toxic threshold → toxicity.
  • Below the therapeutic threshold → no clinical effect.
  • Wider range = safer drug.
  • Lecture examples of wider windows: ibuprofen and paracetamol.

Therapeutic index

A measure of the drug’s margin of safety, determined from the quantal dose–response curve.

TI = TD50 ÷ ED50   or   LD50 ÷ ED50

Why monitor drug levels?

  • Determine drug toxicity.
  • Guide acute therapy.
  • Guide maintenance therapy.

Indications for monitoring

  • Narrow therapeutic index.
  • Dose-related toxicity: inadvertent overdose, surreptitious or intentional drug taking, misdispensing.
  • Therapeutic inefficacy: non-compliance or poor absorption.
  • Unexpectedly rapid elimination.
  • Selection of a prophylactic dose.
  • Dose adjustment using pharmacokinetic principles.
  • Possible drug–drug interactions.
  • A loading-dose regimen is being used.

Cram Sheet

Condensed high-yield map of the complete lecture.

pp. 1–6

Renal excretion

  • Filtration: passive, glomerulus, small/free drug; ~20% of unbound drug filtered.
  • Secretion: active, PCT, carrier-mediated, saturable; competition matters.
  • Reabsorption: passive; unionized/lipid-soluble drugs diffuse back.
  • Alkaline urine increases weak-acid excretion; acidic urine increases weak-base excretion.
  • Renal impairment: ↓ clearance, ↑ half-life, ↑ accumulation/toxicity.

Metabolism

  • Main site: liver.
  • Phase I: oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis; CYP450, especially CYP3A4.
  • Phase II: conjugation with acetate, glucuronate, sulfate, glycine; usually more polar and excretable.
  • Glucuronidation: most common; low in neonates.
  • GSH depletion + NAPQI: acetaminophen hepatotoxicity; antidote N-acetylcysteine.

Activity changes

  • Active → inactive: erythromycin.
  • Active → active: codeine → morphine; caffeine → theophylline.
  • Prodrug → active: prednisone → prednisolone.
  • Active → toxic: paracetamol → NAPQI; isoniazid → acetyl hydrazine.

Intrinsic factors

  • Extremes of age → slow metabolism.
  • Chloramphenicol + neonate → Gray Baby Syndrome.
  • Fast INH acetylator → hepatotoxicity; slow → peripheral neuropathy.
  • Succinylcholine + pseudocholinesterase variant → prolonged apnea.
  • Hypothyroidism slows; hyperthyroidism speeds metabolism.

External factors

  • Acute alcohol: inhibitor; chronic alcohol: inducer.
  • Smoking and charcoal-broiled food: enzyme induction.
  • High altitude: slower oxidation and metabolism.
  • Inducer: less drug/less effect.
  • Inhibitor: more drug/more toxicity.

Kinetics

  • First-order: constant percentage; half-life constant; elimination rate variable.
  • Zero-order: constant amount; half-life variable; elimination rate constant; saturable and narrow safety margin.
  • Zero-order mnemonic: Zero WHATT PowEr.

Critical Warnings
Small dose increases in zero-order kinetics may cause toxicity. Renal impairment prolongs half-life. Enzyme inhibition can raise drug levels. Drugs with a narrow therapeutic index should be monitored.
