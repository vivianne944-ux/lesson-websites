Whole-lesson orientation

Overview

A map of how drugs leave the body, how metabolism changes them, and how elimination behavior determines dosing, steady state, and safety.

Pages 1–10

Lesson roadmap

Elimination is the irreversible shift of drug from one part of the body to another and consists mainly of excretion plus biotransformation.
Excretion removes intact drug or metabolites. The kidney is the major route through filtration, secretion, and reabsorption.
Metabolism alters drug molecules through Phase I and Phase II reactions, often increasing polarity and renal elimination while sometimes creating active or toxic metabolites.
Clinical pharmacokinetics uses kinetics and parameters—especially bioavailability, volume of distribution, and clearance—to guide dosage.
Therapeutic monitoring keeps concentrations within the therapeutic window, especially for drugs with a narrow margin of safety.

Three anchor parameters

Bioavailability
Fraction reaching circulation unchanged
Volume of distribution
Apparent space occupied by drug
Clearance
Body’s ability to eliminate drug
⭐ Must know
Clearance is the lecture’s most important parameter for obtaining the dosage rate.

Core relationships

Clearance = Rate of elimination / Plasma concentration
ROA = CL × C
Loading dose = Vd × Target concentration
Maintenance dose = Dosing rate × Dosing interval

Whole-lesson high-yield anchors

  • More polar / more ionized / more water-soluble → generally easier excretion.
  • More protein-bound → less glomerular filtration.
  • First order → constant fraction removed; half-life constant.
  • Zero order → constant amount removed; elimination pathway saturable.
  • 4–5 half-lives → near steady-state plasma concentration.
  • Peak is the highest level after a dose; trough is the lowest level between doses.
  • Larger therapeutic window → safer drug.

Big picture: what metabolism can do

Increase elimination

Increase polarity, water solubility, and ionization.

Decrease activity

Convert an active drug to an inactive metabolite.

Preserve activity

Convert an active drug to another active metabolite.

Activate a prodrug

Convert an inactive precursor to an active drug.

Create toxicity

Produce reactive or toxic intermediates such as NAPQI.

Overall exam frame: identify the drug property or process, predict whether elimination rises or falls, then connect the change to half-life, plasma concentration, therapeutic effect, and toxicity.

Drug Elimination & Excretion

Separate elimination into its two components, then trace the routes and factors that decide whether a drug stays in the body or leaves it.

Pages 1–2

Drug elimination

Drug elimination is the irreversible shift of drugs from one part of the body to another.

Excretion
Removal of the intact drug.
Biotransformation / Metabolism
Chemical breakdown or alteration of a drug, commonly toward metabolites that can be eliminated.
⚠️ Pitfall
Elimination of the parent molecule does not always end drug action because some drugs form active metabolites.

Factors affecting elimination

FactorLecture relationship
Organ perfusionLow flow in shock or hypotension → decreased elimination. Increased perfusion → increased elimination.
Protein bindingIncreased binding → decreased elimination.
MetabolismIncreased metabolism → decreased plasma half-life and increased elimination.
Polarity and sizeMore polar drugs are more easily eliminated.
Disease / pathologic stateGenerally decreases elimination.
Genetic variationsMay change metabolic or excretory handling.
ClearanceVolume of blood cleared of drug per unit time.

Forms and routes of excretion

Drugs may be excreted as the unchanged parent drug or as metabolites.

Polar metabolites

Water-soluble; high excretion and low absorption.

Non-polar metabolites

Lipid-soluble; high reabsorption back into plasma and low excretion.

Routes

  • Kidneys — major route.
  • GIT — biliary excretion with enterohepatic circulation and fecal excretion.
  • Lungs — exhalation / pulmonary route.
  • Skin, sweat, saliva, tears, and breast milk.

Manipulating urinary excretion

Ionized drugs do not diffuse passively and are less likely to be reabsorbed.

  • Urinary alkalinization → increases excretion of weak acids.
  • Urinary acidification → increases excretion of weak bases.
⭐ Must know
For aspirin poisoning, the lecture gives sodium bicarbonate to alkalinize urine.
✅ Key point
If a weak acid is in an acidic tubular environment, it tends to remain reabsorbed.
Kidney handling

Renal Drug Excretion

Follow the drug through the nephron: passive filtration at the glomerulus, active secretion in the proximal tubule, then passive reabsorption in the distal tubule.

Pages 1–2, 9

The three renal processes

Glomerular filtration — passive movement of sufficiently small, unbound molecules through the glomerular membrane.
Active tubular secretion — transporter-mediated movement from peritubular capillaries into the proximal tubule.
Passive reabsorption — uncharged drug diffuses from tubular fluid back into plasma, especially in the distal tubule.

Glomerulus

  • Acts as a dialyzing membrane.
  • Filtration is a passive process.
  • Removes molecules smaller than albumin; the lecture notes diffusion of molecular weight <20,000.
  • Albumin molecular weight is listed as 68,000, making it difficult to filter.
  • Highly protein-bound drugs are not filtered.
  • Small, unbound drugs are cleared rapidly.
  • Approximately 20% of unbound drug is filtered.
🔬 Lab cue
Inulin clearance estimates GFR because inulin is neither reabsorbed nor secreted.

Proximal tubule

  • Site of active tubular secretion.
  • Important for drugs not readily filtered at the glomerulus.
  • Primary kidney mechanism cited for eliminating antibiotics, antivirals, antidiabetic medications, and other chemotherapies.
  • Drugs pass through the peritubular capillaries.
  • The lecture states that it excretes 80% of ionized forms.
  • Uses a carrier system, so the process is saturable and allows competition.

Weak bases

Natural substrate: histamine.

Weak acids

Natural substrate: uric acid.

Distal tubule

  • Site of passive reabsorption.
  • Uncharged drugs may diffuse out of the kidney and escape elimination.
  • Reabsorption predominates; the lecture notes that 99% of water is reabsorbed into the plasma compartment.
  • More polar, water-soluble, ionized compounds are less reabsorbed and therefore more readily excreted.
✅ Key point
If a drug remains ionized in tubular fluid, think less passive reabsorption and more excretion.

Renal sequence recap: glomerulus = passive filtration; proximal tubule = active secretion; distal tubule = passive reabsorption.
Biotransformation framework

Drug Metabolism Map

Metabolism can proceed from Phase I to Phase II, directly through Phase II, or in some drugs from Phase II to Phase I. Its overall purpose is chemical alteration—not automatically inactivation.

Pages 2–4

Biotransformation and metabolites

Biotransformation is chemical alteration of a drug in the body; metabolites are its by-products.

Active metabolite
  • Retains pharmacologic activity.
  • May still be reabsorbed.
  • Less polar, less ionized, and lipid-soluble in the lecture’s description.
Inactive metabolite
  • No pharmacologic activity.
  • Prepared for excretion.
  • More polar, more water-soluble, and more ionized.

Sites of drug-metabolizing enzymes

Liver is the main site. Other sites listed are the lungs, intestinal mucosa, skin, placenta, and kidney.

Subcellular locations include the endoplasmic reticulum, mitochondria, cytosol, lysosomes, nuclear envelope, and plasma membrane.

Metabolic sequence

✅ Key point
A drug may undergo Phase I then Phase II, proceed directly to Phase II, or undergo Phase II before Phase I, as cited for isoniazid.

Phase I: non-synthetic

Parent drugs undergo direct modification through oxidation, reduction, hydrolysis, or deamination. A polar group is inserted or unmasked, usually making the compound more polar and water-soluble.

Microsomal

Centered on CYP450 enzymes in the smooth ER.

Non-microsomal

Includes hydrolysis, MAO metabolism, and alcohol metabolism.

Phase II: synthetic

Drugs or Phase I metabolites are conjugated with endogenous substances such as acetate, glucuronate, sulfate, or glycine.

Conjugation occurs at hydroxyl, amino, or sulfhydryl functions and generally produces compounds that are more polar, less lipid-soluble, and inactive.

⚠️ Do not confuse
Phase II does not always require Phase I first.

What metabolism changes

Renal elimination

Often increased by greater polarity, water solubility, and ionization.

Pharmacologic activity

May be reduced, preserved, created from a prodrug, or converted into toxicity.

Half-life

Higher lipid solubility and lower polarity are associated with longer persistence.

Membrane transport

Highly polar drugs are poorly absorbed and poorly transported across membranes.

Non-synthetic reactions

Phase I Reactions

Phase I exposes or inserts polar groups. Oxidation is predominant, but reduction, hydrolysis, and deamination also contribute.

Page 3

Oxidation

  • Predominant Phase I reaction.
  • May be CYP450-dependent or independent.
  • Cytochrome P450 / mixed function oxidase is concentrated in the smooth ER of liver cells.
  • Isoenzymes are part of microsomal metabolism.
  • They are not highly selective for substrates.
  • The lecture states that CYP3A4 handles about 50% of drug metabolism and that CYP3A4 plus CYP2D6 account for 75%.

Sites and enzymes

Microsomal CYP450 monooxygenase and cytoplasmic enzymes; sites include the ER and microsomes.

Examples

  • Paracetamol — N-oxidation.
  • Ibuprofen — hydroxylation.
✅ Key point
Microsomal oxidation includes hydroxylation and dealkylation.

Reduction

  • Substrate gains electrons.
  • More likely when oxygen content is low.
  • May activate xenobiotics rather than detoxify them.
  • Enzyme: hepatic nitroreductase.
  • Site: cell cytoplasm.
  • Examples include nitroreduction, dehalogenation, and oxide reduction.
  • Dantrolene is given as an example of nitro reduction in malignant hyperthermia.

Hydrolysis

A non-microsomal process involving addition of water followed by bond breakage.

ComponentLecture detail
EnzymesEsterases and amidases; cholinesterases hydrolyze ester and amide drugs.
Intracellular sitesER and cytoplasm.
Extracellular siteCirculating plasma.
ExampleProcaine (ester) — pseudocholinesterase.
Contrast exampleLidocaine (amide) — liver microsomal CYP450.

Other non-microsomal pathways

Monoamine oxidase (MAO)

Metabolizes endogenous amine neurotransmitters such as 5-HT and dopamine, and exogenous compounds such as tyramine.

Alcohol metabolism

Alcohol is metabolized to an aldehyde and then an acid by dehydrogenases. The lecture emphasizes genetic polymorphism in this pathway.

Deamination

Listed as a Phase I reaction in the lecture.

Phase I recap: oxidation is dominant and often microsomal; hydrolysis, MAO activity, and alcohol metabolism are non-microsomal.
Synthetic reactions and outcomes

Phase II & Effects of Metabolism

Conjugation usually makes drugs more polar and excretable, but metabolism can also preserve activity, activate prodrugs, or produce toxicity.

Pages 3–4, 8

Glucuronidation

  • Most common conjugation reaction.
  • Inducible.
  • May undergo enterohepatic cycling.
  • Examples: morphine and chloramphenicol.
  • Activity is low in neonates.
🧠 Clinical cue
Low neonatal glucuronyl transferase activity is linked in the lecture to chloramphenicol toxicity and gray baby syndrome.

Acetylation

  • Shows genotypic variation: fast versus slow metabolizers.
  • The lecture associates drug-induced SLE with acetylator status and gives hydralazine, procainamide / clonazepam as transcribed, and isoniazid among examples across sections.
  • Isoniazid may proceed through Phase II before Phase I.
⚠️ Exam trap
Acetylation is a Phase II synthetic reaction, not a Phase I reaction.

Glutathione conjugation

  • Protects hepatocytes and other cells.
  • Glutathione depletion is associated with acetaminophen hepatotoxicity.
  • Excess unconjugated acetaminophen undergoes oxidation to NAPQI, a toxic metabolite.
⭐ Must know
N-acetylcysteine is the antidote given in the lecture and is ideally administered within 8–16 hours.

Reactive products described

  • Epoxides — highly reactive intermediates that form covalent bonds and may cause carcinogenesis, teratogenicity, and tissue damage.
  • Hydroxylamines — used as reducing agents and may act as antioxidants for fatty acids.
  • Activated oxygen / superoxides — compounds containing superoxide ion.
  • Free radicals — highly reactive, short-lived, and neutralized by antioxidants / free-radical scavengers.
Patient and environment

Factors Affecting Biotransformation

Metabolism varies with age, nutrition, disease, temperature, genetics, environment, diet, and co-administered drugs.

Pages 4–5, 8–9

Age

Very young
  • Metabolic sites are not fully developed.
  • Less enzyme synthesis → decreased metabolism.
  • Chloramphenicol toxicity may produce gray baby syndrome: cyanosis, diarrhea, circulatory collapse, and death.
  • The summary notes enzyme development over the first 1–2 months.
Very old
  • Wear and tear, organ shrinkage, reduced liver function, and reduced hepatic blood flow.
  • Decreased tissue and enzymatic activity.
  • The lecture summary notes that elderly patients are often given ½ the adult dose.

Sex / gender-related factors

  • The lecture states there is generally not much difference, but hormones and body composition can affect selected drugs.
  • Females: estradiol generally inhibits degradation of certain drugs; estrogen may induce enzymatic activity; more fat tissue is associated with decreased metabolism.
  • Males: testosterone promotes enzymatic degradation; more lean tissue is associated with faster oxidation.
  • CYP3A4 activity is cited as higher in women, while other systems may be higher in men.
  • Menopause, oral contraceptive use, menstruation, and pregnancy may affect metabolism.
  • The summary states males have higher BMR and pregnancy may increase female metabolism.

Nutrition

Malnutrition and protein deficiency reduce enzyme synthesis and lower biotransformation, which prolongs half-life, raises drug levels, and may cause toxicity.

Disease

  • Liver disease may increase drug toxicity.
  • Cardiac disease may slow distribution through reduced blood flow.
  • Hypothyroidism → slow metabolism.
  • Hyperthyroidism → fast metabolism.
  • Pathologic conditions involving liver, lungs, or CVS generally decrease metabolism in the summary.

Temperature and altitude

  • Fever / increased temperature may enhance metabolism.
  • Hypothermia may decrease metabolism.
  • High altitude decreases metabolism through lower oxygen and reduced drug oxidation.

Genetic variation

  • Drug-metabolizing systems differ among populations; polymorphisms are studied in pharmacogenomics.
  • Rapid acetylators are cited among Asians: more metabolites formed and drug-induced hepatitis may occur.
  • Slow acetylators are cited among Caucasians: more free drug and peripheral neuropathy.
  • Isoniazid is combined with pyridoxine / vitamin B6 to address neuropathy risk.
  • Inherited aldehyde dehydrogenase deficiency may cause alcohol-related flushing.
  • Pseudocholinesterase deficiency may cause prolonged apnea after succinylcholine.
  • G6PD deficiency predisposes RBCs to oxidative hemolysis from some drugs.

CYP450 and drug–drug interaction

Enzyme Inducers & Inhibitors

Predict the direction: induction speeds metabolism and lowers the affected drug level; inhibition slows metabolism and raises the affected drug level.

Page 5, 8

Enzyme induction

  • Increases metabolism.
  • Increases synthesis of CYP450 enzymes.
  • Usually requires several days to reach maximal induction.
  • Leads to lower levels and reduced effect of the non-inducer drug.
⭐ Mnemonic
John Bar Puts GRease on Ethan’s Car: St. John’s wort, Barbiturates, Phenytoin, Griseofulvin, Rifampicin, Ethanol, Carbamazepine.

Enzyme inhibition

  • Decreases metabolism.
  • Leads to higher levels and increased effect / toxicity of the non-inhibiting drug.
  • The lecture also links inhibition to reduced blood flow to the metabolizing organ.
⭐ Mnemonic
Queenie is SICK of GRAV-E: Quinidine, Sulfonamides, Isoniazid, Cimetidine, Ketoconazole, Grapefruit juice, Ritonavir, Amiodarone, Valproic acid, Erythromycin.

Elimination behavior

First-Order vs Zero-Order Kinetics

The key distinction is whether the body removes a constant fraction or a constant amount per unit time.

Pages 5–6, 10

First-order kinetics

  • Also called first-order elimination.
  • Most common type of elimination.
  • Linear and non-saturable within the lecture’s framing.
  • A constant fraction or percentage is removed per unit time.
  • Rate is directly proportional to the remaining concentration.
  • Drug concentration does not saturate the enzyme or carrier system.
  • Double the dose → double the concentration.
  • Concentration falls by 50% for every half-life.
  • Elimination rate varies; half-life stays constant.

Zero-order kinetics

  • Also called Michaelis–Menten kinetics / zero-order elimination.
  • Nonlinear and saturable.
  • A constant amount is removed per unit time.
  • Elimination rate is constant regardless of concentration because the elimination mechanism is saturated.
  • A small dose increase may push the drug to toxic levels.
  • Metabolic or transport systems may be saturated.
  • Elimination rate stays constant; half-life varies.

Lecture mnemonic for zero-order drugs

⭐ Zero WHATT PowEr
Warfarin, Heparin, Aspirin, Theophylline, Tolbutamide, Phenytoin, Ethanol. The lecture notes that monitoring should be done when these drugs are given.

Quantitative dosing tools

Pharmacokinetic Parameters & Dosing

Connect volume of distribution and clearance to half-life, then use clearance and target concentration to calculate dosage rate.

Pages 6–7, 9–10

Bioavailability

The fraction of drug reaching the circulation unchanged.

Volume of distribution

The apparent space in the body to which drug is uniformly distributed at a concentration equal to plasma concentration.

✅ Key point
At constant clearance, a drug with a high Vd has a longer elimination half-life than one with a lower Vd.

Clearance

A measure of the body’s ability to eliminate drug: the volume of blood cleared of drug per unit time.

Half-life

Time required for plasma drug concentration to decrease by 50%.

t½ = 0.693 × (Vd / Cl)
  • A function of volume of distribution and clearance.
  • Applied in the lecture to first-order kinetics.
  • Useful for estimating time to steady state.
  • Only drug in the central compartment can be directly eliminated by liver and kidneys.

After 1 half-life

50% remains.

After 2

25% remains.

After 3

12.5% remains.

After 4

6.3% remains.

After 5

3.2% remains.

Clearance equations

Cl = Rate of elimination / Plasma concentration
Total systemic Cl = Clrenal + Clhepatic + Clothers
ROA = CL × C
  • At steady state, drug input = drug output.
  • Dosage rate / rate of administration equals the rate of elimination.
  • Clearance depends on the drug and the condition of elimination organs.
⭐ Must know
Clearance is the most important parameter for getting the dosage rate and can lead to toxicity if altered.

Peak level

  • Highest plasma concentration after each dose.
  • IV: within a few minutes.
  • Oral: about 1–2 hours.

Trough / valley level

  • Lowest plasma concentration between dosing intervals.
  • Best time to obtain blood samples.

Steady state

  • Equilibrium between dose input and elimination.
  • Achieved after about 4 half-lives; the lecture also states 4–5 half-lives for near steady state.

Loading dose

A single dose or series of doses used to reach the target concentration rapidly without waiting for 4 half-lives.

LD = Vd × Target concentration
  • Useful when the drug has a long half-life.
  • May be given as a single dose through the selected route.
  • Depends on elimination half-life, dosing interval, and target concentration.
  • Followed by a maintenance dose.

Maintenance dose

Maintains a steady-state concentration within the optimal therapeutic range.

Maintenance dose = Dosing rate × Dosing interval
Dosing rate = Clearance × Target concentration
  • Computed from clearance.
  • Given as repetitive doses or continuous infusion.
  • Usually follows loading dose or one half-life.
  • The lecture notes it is approximately half the loading dose.
Safety and concentration targets

Therapeutic Window & Drug Monitoring

Keep plasma concentration above the minimum effective level but below the minimum toxic level, especially when the therapeutic index is narrow.

Pages 7–8

Therapeutic range / window

The dosage range between the minimum effective therapeutic concentration or dose and the minimum toxic concentration or dose.

Below threshold

No clinical effect.

Within window

Desired therapeutic effect.

Above threshold

Toxicity.

⭐ Must know
The larger the therapeutic range, the safer the drug. Ibuprofen and paracetamol are given as examples of larger windows.

Therapeutic index

A measure of the margin of safety.

Therapeutic index = TD50 (or LD50) / ED50

Determined from a quantal dose-response curve.

Repeated doses and plateau

  • For drugs following first-order kinetics, concentration rises exponentially with repeated doses until a plateau is reached.
  • The plateau corresponds to the steady-state level.
  • At steady state, peaks and troughs stop progressively rising or falling when doses are given regularly and none are missed.

Why therapeutic drug monitoring is done

  • Determine drug toxicity.
  • Guide acute therapy.
  • Guide maintenance therapy.

Indications

  • Narrow therapeutic index.
  • Dose-related toxicity, overdose, surreptitious use, or misdispensing.
  • Therapeutic inefficacy from non-compliance, poor absorption, or unexpectedly rapid elimination.
  • Prophylactic dose selection.
  • Dose adjustment using pharmacokinetic principles.
  • Potential drug–drug interactions.
  • Use of a loading-dose regimen.

  • GSH depletion + acetaminophen excess → NAPQI toxicity.

Induction / inhibition

✅ Induction
↑ metabolism → ↓ affected drug level → ↓ effect.
John Bar Puts GRease on Ethan’s Car.
⚠️ Inhibition
↓ metabolism → ↑ affected drug level → ↑ effect or toxicity.
Queenie is SICK of GRAV-E.

Acute alcohol inhibits; chronic alcohol induces.

First vs zero order

First order

Constant fraction; half-life constant; rate variable.

Zero order

Constant amount; rate constant; half-life variable; saturable.

⭐ Zero-order mnemonic
Zero WHATT PowEr: Warfarin, Heparin, Aspirin, Theophylline, Tolbutamide, Phenytoin, Ethanol.

Peak, trough, loading, maintenance

  • Peak: highest level after a dose.
  • Trough: lowest level between doses; best sampling time.
  • Loading dose: reach target quickly; depends primarily on Vd and target concentration.
  • Maintenance dose: maintain target; depends on clearance.
  • Steady state: drug input equals drug output.

“If you see X → think Y”

Highly protein-bound drug

Think less glomerular filtration.

Ionized, water-soluble drug

Think less reabsorption and more excretion.

Renal impairment

Think lower clearance and longer half-life.

Inducer added

Think lower affected drug level.

Inhibitor added

Think higher affected drug level / toxicity.

Same percentage removed

Think first order.

Same amount removed

Think zero order.

Need immediate target level

Think loading dose.

Narrow therapeutic index

Think therapeutic drug monitoring.

Common traps and warnings

  • Do not assume metabolism always means inactivation.
  • Do not assume Phase II always follows Phase I.
  • Do not confuse active secretion in the proximal tubule with passive reabsorption in the distal tubule.
  • Do not confuse constant fraction with constant amount.
  • Do not use half-life as the main dosage-rate parameter; the lecture identifies clearance.
  • Do not draw a routine trough sample at the peak; the trough is the lowest level between doses.
  • A small dose increase in zero-order kinetics may cause a disproportionate concentration rise and toxicity.
  • For acetaminophen excess, remember NAPQI and N-acetylcysteine.
Final sweep: elimination pathway → drug polarity / binding → kinetics → half-life and clearance → target concentration → therapeutic window and monitoring.
